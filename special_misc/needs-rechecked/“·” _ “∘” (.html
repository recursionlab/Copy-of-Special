You’re absolutely right to zone in on these—function composition tokens (like "·", "∘") and structural affixes ("of", "meta", etc.) are the hidden “connectors” and “field shapers” of formal systems, but they play very different roles in the machinery of mathematics, logic, and even natural language.

Let’s clarify both, show how they’re different, and then extract what’s most actionable for you:

1. Function Composition Tokens ("·", "∘", etc.)
Role:

Binary operators that explicitly compose functions or processes, creating a pipeline or chain of execution.

Mathematics & Logic:

"∘" (Circle, Small Circle):
Most standard in category theory and functional programming.
If f: A → B and g: B → C, then

COPY
EDIT
(g ∘ f)(x) = g(f(x))
("Apply f, then g.")

"·" (Dot, Bullet):
In many contexts (algebra, vector spaces, group theory), denotes multiplication or composition, sometimes function application in non-commutative systems.
E.g.,

COPY
EDIT
(f · g)(x) = f(g(x))
But meaning varies by context!

Natural Language:

There is no strict equivalent—but think of it as “then”, “after”, or “through”.

Category Theory:

Arrows compose via “∘”

Diagrams commute by chaining arrows via “∘”

Programming:

In Haskell: (.) :: (b -> c) -> (a -> b) -> a -> c

In Unix/Linux: | (pipe) plays a similar role for data streams

2. Structural Affixes ("of-", "meta-", etc.)
Role:

Modify, contextualize, or relate structures/objects to each other.

Not an operation, but a relational marker or semantic container.

Linguistics:

"of-" = possessive, containment, association
(“the house of wisdom”, “field of recursion”)

"meta-" = “about,” “above,” or “reflecting upon”
(“meta-mathematics”, “meta-analysis”)

Math/Logic:

Used to label, type, or nest—not to “apply” or “chain”

Example:

Set of functions

Meta-category of all categories

4. Why Composition Tokens Are Elusive (and Powerful)
They require two things to exist and “make sense”:

Functions, not just objects—you must have processes or arrows

Compatible domains/codomains—the output of one must “fit” as the input to the next

In natural language, these tokens are almost invisible, but in math and category theory, they are the backbone of everything processual.

For you:

They are the “circuit joints” of your meta-operator language.

Once you master these, you can build pipelines, not just piles of objects.
