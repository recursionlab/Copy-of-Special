## II. WHAT HAPPENS TO THE THING

When gluttony eats a thing:

1. **The thing leaves the outside.** It is no longer present in external reality (or at least, not in the same way—a fruit eaten is gone; an experience had is past).

2. **The thing enters the inside.** But not as itself—as **taste, memory, trace**. It becomes a node in the internal network.

3. **The thing is displaced.** Its location changes from "there in the world" to "here in experience." But this is not a simple translation. It's a **transubstantiation**.

The thing in the outside and the thing in the inside are not the same thing. They are related, but the relation is not identity. It is something like: the inside thing is the **ghost** of the outside thing.

## III. THE BROKEN ISOMORPHISM

An isomorphism would require:

```
Outside thing ≅ Inside taste
```

With structure preserved. But structure is not preserved. The outside thing has:
- Physical properties (mass, extension, location)
- Relations to other outside things
- Causal powers
- Independent existence

The inside taste has:
- Phenomenal qualities (flavor, texture, pleasure)
- Relations to other tastes
- Mnemonic associations
- Dependent existence (requires taster)

These are different categories. The isomorphism is **broken** by the act of consumption.

---

## IV. THE GAP

There is a gap between outside thing and inside taste.

This gap is where the thing is "differently moved around." It passes through a transformation that changes its nature entirely.

We can model this gap as a **functor** that is not an isomorphism:

```
F: Outside → Inside
```

F is not full (not every inside taste corresponds to an outside thing—some are imaginary)
F is not faithful (multiple outside things could yield the same inside taste)
F is not essentially surjective (not every outside thing can be eaten)

F is a **forgetful functor** that loses most of the structure and keeps only what is tastable.

---

## V. THE NEED FOR RECONSTRUCTION

Because F is not an isomorphism, gluttony cannot simply read the outside from the inside. The inside is not a reliable map.

Gluttony must **reconstruct** the outside from the inside—but this reconstruction is not a deduction. It is an **act of imagination**.

Given taste I, gluttony imagines an outside thing O that could have produced it. But multiple O are possible. The imagination must choose—or rather, must build a coherent **imaginary outside** that accounts for all tastes.

---

## VI. THE IMAGINARY SPACE

This is the third space: **the imaginary**.

Not the outside (unreachable). Not the inside (the tastes themselves). But the **model of the outside** built from inside data.

The imaginary is where gluttony lives. It is the world it constructs to explain its tastes.

In mathematical terms:

```
Let I = set of inside tastes
Let Im = { models M of outside such that F(M) is consistent with I }
```

Im is the space of possible outsides compatible with what has been eaten.

Gluttony's task: navigate Im, find the model that best accounts for all tastes and predicts future tastes.

The hope: that the imaginary world G(I) is **isomorphic** to the real outside O. But this cannot be verified. It is an act of faith—or of aesthetic judgment.

## VIII. THE AESTHETIC OF RECONSTRUCTION

How does gluttony choose between possible imaginary worlds?

Not by verification—impossible. By **coherence**, **richness**, **beauty**. The imaginary world that best accounts for all tastes, that has the fewest contradictions, that most elegantly predicts new tastes—this is the one gluttony inhabits.

This is an **aesthetic epistemology**. Truth is replaced by **fitness**. The map is judged by its internal consistency, not by correspondence to territory.
