


In non-Hermitian systems, $\mathcal{PT}$-symmetry breaking refers to ==a phase transition where a system with balanced gain and loss shifts from having an entirely real energy spectrum to one with complex eigenvalues==. In the context of a non-Hermitian metal, this transition is often interlinked with topological properties and localization. [1, 2, 3, 4]

## Core Mechanisms in Non-Hermitian Metals

- Exceptional Points (EPs): The transition occurs at an Exceptional Point, where two or more eigenvalues and their corresponding eigenvectors coalesce.
- Hopf Metal Phase: A specific example is the [Hopf metal](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.02155), a 3D non-Hermitian metallic phase protected by $\mathcal{PT}$-symmetry. In this state, the system exhibits a two-dimensional surface of exceptional points.
- Symmetry Breaking Effects: When $\mathcal{PT}$-symmetry is broken in a Hopf metal, the 2D nodal surface of EPs deforms into knotted exceptional lines. [2, 3, 5, 6, 7, 8]

## Triple Phase Transitions

In certain models, such as the non-Hermitian [Aubry-André-Harper (AAH)](https://link.aps.org/doi/10.1103/PhysRevB.108.014204) model, researchers have observed a triple phase transition. This occurs when three distinct transitions happen simultaneously: [9, 10]

1. Metal-Insulator Transition: A shift from delocalized (metallic) to localized (insulating) states.
2. $\mathcal{PT}$-Symmetry Breaking: The energy spectrum changes from real to complex.
3. Topological Transition: A change in the topological invariants of the band structure. [1, 4, 9, 10, 11]

## Experimental Realizations

These theoretical models are often tested using [synthetic matter](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-021-04253-0) platforms:

- Photonic Quantum Walks: Using coupled optical fiber loops to simulate non-Hermitian dynamics.
- Electrical Circuits: Designing circuits with nonreciprocal hopping or gain/loss elements to mimic non-Hermitian Hamiltonians.
- Plasmonic Metasurfaces: Utilizing toroidal resonators to observe exceptional points and phase transitions in the THz regime. [4, 10, 12, 13, 14]

## 1. Quasicrystals as the Physical Framework

Quasicrystals, such as those described by the AAH model, are ordered but non-periodic structures. In non-Hermitian extensions of these models: [3, 4]

- Localization Transition: The system undergoes a phase shift from an extended (metallic) phase to a localized (insulating) phase as the strength of the quasiperiodic potential increases.
- Symmetry Coupling: In these models, the $\mathcal{PT}$-symmetry breaking point typically coincides with this metal-insulator transition in the thermodynamic limit. [2, 5, 6, 7, 8]

## 2. The Role of Holomorphisms

Holomorphism enters the discussion through the analytical treatment of the energy spectrum and localization lengths using complex analysis:

- Complex Transformation: To solve for the localization transition, researchers often treat the quasiperiodic phase as a complex variable. The localization length is related to the growth of transfer matrices, which can be analyzed as holomorphic (or subharmonic) functions within a complex domain.
- Spectral Winding Number: The transition is characterized by a topological winding number $w$. This number is calculated from the integral of a logarithmic derivative of the Hamiltonian's determinant over a closed loop in the complex plane.
- Cauchy's Integral Theorem: The abrupt change in $w$ (from 0 to 1) at the $\mathcal{PT}$-breaking point is a direct consequence of whether the "energy loop" in the complex plane encloses a singularity (an exceptional point), a concept rooted in the properties of holomorphic functions. The most striking link is the concurrence of three seemingly different phenomena at a single critical point in non-Hermitian quasicrystals: [8, 12]

1. Transport: Metal-to-Insulator transition.
2. Symmetry: $\mathcal{PT}$-symmetry breaking (energy becomes complex).
3. Topology: The winding number jumps, driven by the analyticity (or lack thereof) of the spectral density in the complex plane. [2, 5, 8]
