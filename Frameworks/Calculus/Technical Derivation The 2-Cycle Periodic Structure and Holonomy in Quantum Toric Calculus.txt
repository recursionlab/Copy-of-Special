Technical Derivation: The 2-Cycle Periodic Structure and Holonomy in Quantum Toric Calculus

1. Introduction: The Strategic Shift to Non-Commutative Toric Foundations

Quantum Toric Geometry represents a profound deformation of classical theory, wherein the fundamental tori are replaced by non-commutative counterparts to better model quantum integrable systems and non-commutative T-duality. This strategic shift is necessitated by the breakdown of classical manifold theory when confronted with irrational quotients. In the classical regime, a toric manifold is an equivariant compactification of a torus; however, preserving geometric integrity in the quantum regime requires treating these objects as non-commutative (NC) spaces or stacks.

A central dichotomy exists within the deformation parameter \sim. When \sim is rational, the resulting algebra of functions is Morita equivalent to a commutative algebra, effectively remaining "classically flavored." Conversely, when \sim is irrational, the space is truly non-commutative, and traditional topological quotients yield non-Hausdorff spaces where leaves wind densely. To maintain a rigorous category of morphisms, we move from point-set topology to stacks and NC-algebras, which effectively resolve these quotients. This transition suggests a "Chimeric Algebraic Geometry" based on non-standard extensions of the complex numbers, providing a necessary bridge for the B-side of the mirror symmetry puzzle.

2. The Algebraic Foundation: Born-Heisenberg-Jordan Commutation Relations

The definition of the quantum 2-torus (T^2_{\mathbb{R},\sim}) arises from the algebraic necessity of non-commuting periodic generators. These relations transform the manifold into a quantum object where \sim acts as the "Planck length" of the system.

The Fundamental Relation The algebraic structure is governed by the relation: XY = e^{2\pi i\sim} YX This expression is the Weyl exponential form of the Born-Heisenberg-Jordan commutation relation. Here, X and Y are periodic generators of the algebra A_\sim. When \sim = 0, we recover the commutative Gelfand-Naimark limit. As \sim deviates from zero, the generators cease to commute, manifesting the non-commutative nature of the underlying space.

Model of the Morphism Category The categorical relationship between classical and non-commutative frameworks is bridged by Morita equivalence (\sim_M), as detailed in the following synthesis:

Category	Classical (Commutative)	Non-Commutative (NC)
Primary Object	Spaces / Manifolds	NC-Spaces (Morita classes [A]_{\sim_M})
Algebraic Equivalent	Commutative Algebras	NC-Algebras / \sim_M
Morphism Tool	Smooth Mappings	Hilsum-Skandalis bi-bundles
Equivalence	Gelfand-Naimark Theorem	Stackification / Morita Equivalence

This commutation relation is the analytical manifestation of the geometric holonomy found within the Kronecker foliation.

3. Geometric Derivation: The Kronecker Foliation and Holonomy Mapping

Following A. Connes, the quantum torus is interpreted geometrically as the leaf space of a foliation in the second dimension. This interpretation utilizes the projection of the Euclidean plane \mathbb{R}^2 onto the torus T^2 via the exponential map: E: (x, y) \mapsto (\exp(2\pi ix), \exp(2\pi iy))

The Holonomy Map The Kronecker foliation of slope \sim is the image of the linear foliation of \mathbb{R}^2 under E. For irrational \sim, each leaf is a line winding densely. The holonomy—the shift encountered by a leaf as it traverses a transversal vertical circle S^1—is captured by the rotation map \rho_\sim: S^1 \to S^1, generating the discrete group of rotations \langle \rho_\sim \rangle. The resulting leaf space is the quotient S^1 / \langle \rho_\sim \rangle.

Naive vs. Homotopic Dimension A critical distinction arises in the dimension count of this structure:

* Naive Dimension: \dim(T^2) - \dim(\text{Leaf}) = 2 - 1 = 1. This justifies the notation T_{1, \mathbb{R}, \sim} often used in the literature for the 2nd-dimension quantum torus.
* Homotopic Dimension: The homotopy quotient T^2 \times_{E(\sim)} E\mathbb{R} remains homotopy equivalent to T^2, preserving a dimension of 2.

This "2nd cycle" ensures that the space retains the topological information of a 2-manifold despite its apparent one-dimensional quotient. This geometric holonomy is formalized via stackification and the logarithmic representation of quantum lattices.

4. The C_A Calculus: Stackification and Periodic Homology

To resolve non-Hausdorff quotients, we employ the "Calculus of Stacks" and the Connes Convolution Algebra mapping (C). This mapping acts as a functor bridging the gap between the geometric category (Stacks/Groupoids) and the algebraic category (NC-Algebras).

The Stackification Process From the groupoid (T, \sim), we derive three distinct but related objects:

1. The Non-Hausdorff Space: The "naive" topological quotient.
2. The NC-Algebra (A_T): The algebra defined by the Born-Heisenberg-Jordan relations.
3. The Stack (\mathcal{T}): The richest object, preserving all groupoid information.

Periodic Cyclic Homology The "2-Cycle" oscillation is confirmed through periodic cyclic homology. Despite a naive dimension of 1, the homological behavior mirrors that of a two-dimensional space. This periodicity allows the quantum torus to function as a compactification of the complex plane, providing the homological depth necessary for the calibration of quantum fans.

5. Calibration Mechanics and the Gerbe Structure

Calibration involves adding gerbe degrees of freedom to a quantum fan, a process physically analogous to "turning on the B-field" in the Seiberg-Witten sense. This serves to desingularize moduli spaces and ensures that periodic cyclic homology forms a coherent bundle.

The Calibration Homomorphism A calibration is defined by an epimorphism h: \mathbb{Z}^n \to \Gamma, where \Gamma is a q-lattice (a finitely generated additive subgroup of \mathbb{R}^d spanning it over the real number field). This mapping identifies virtual generators J, which account for the extra degrees of freedom. In the polytopal case, the image of the quantum moment map—where the inverse image of a point is a real Lagrangian quantum torus—can be analyzed via a non-algebraic version of the Kempf-Ness Theorem.

The Gerbe/Band Relationship Per Proposition 6.20, a calibrated quantum toric stack \mathcal{X}^{cal}_{\Delta,h,J} functions as a gerbe over the uncalibrated version X_{\Delta,\Gamma,v} with band \mathbb{Z}^a (where a = n - \text{rank}(\Gamma)). This calibrated stack is completely determined by a classifying map: X_{\Delta,\Gamma,v} \to B\mathbb{Z}^a This "lifts" the stack, adding a layer of symmetry that resolves singularities in the rational locus of the moduli space.

By converging algebraic commutation, geometric foliation holonomy, and homological stackification, Quantum Toric Geometry establishes a robust, periodic framework. These cycles ensure that the non-commutative torus preserves the requisite geometric information to serve as the building block for quantum integrable systems and mirror symmetry.
