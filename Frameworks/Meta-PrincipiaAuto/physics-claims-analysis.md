## Chapter 1: Geometric Foundations: Torsion as the Necessary Extension of Spacetime
To evaluate the novelty of the proposed theory, it is essential to first establish the established limits of classical gravity models, General Relativity and its immediate extension, Einstein-Cartan theory.
### 1.1 General Relativity (GR) and the Curvature-Only Constraint
General Relativity, formalized by the Einstein Field Equations, is intrinsically built upon Riemannian geometry. This framework assumes gravity is solely defined by the curvature of spacetime, which is sourced by the energy-momentum tensor T_{\mu\nu} (mass and energy). The geometry employs the metric g_{\mu\nu} and the symmetric Levi-Civita connection.
A critical limitation arises when attempting to integrate fermionic matter, such as electrons and quarks, into this framework. Fermions are inherently described by spinors, which require the local Lorentz covariance necessary for describing rotational and boost symmetries. Riemannian geometry fundamentally lacks the requisite structure to naturally embody this local gauge symmetry , resulting in an incomplete description of physical reality at the quantum level. The physics demands a geometric structure capable of accommodating spin.
### 1.2 The Einstein-Cartan-Sciama-Kibble (ECSK) Baseline
The Einstein-Cartan-Sciama-Kibble (ECSK) theory, proposed first by Cartan in 1922 and later rediscovered by Sciama and Kibble, resolves the spinor issue by transitioning to Riemann-Cartan geometry. This geometry allows the affine connection to possess an antisymmetric component, known as torsion (T_{\mu\nu}^{\lambda}), alongside the symmetric curvature component.
In standard ECSK theory, torsion is not an independent field but is sourced directly by the classical canonical spin tensor S_{\mu\nu}^{\lambda} via the Cartan equations. This inclusion ensures the continuity equations and conservation laws for rotational symmetries are properly expressed, thereby accommodating spinors. The inclusion of torsion is thus not an arbitrary modification but a necessary geometric extension required for the consistent description of quantum matter. If the torsion component vanishes, the theory seamlessly reverts to GR.
However, standard ECSK theory suffers from a major limitation: its torsion field is non-dynamical. Torsion does not propagate in free space and only exists within the confines of matter sources, effectively vanishing rapidly outside the source material. For the proposed Torsion-Consciousness Connection to be physically viable—that is, for information to modulate and propagate through spacetime—the theory must move beyond ECSK into a more general Metric-Affine Gravity (MAG) or Gauge Gravity framework that treats torsion as an independent, propagating field. This dynamical requirement suggests the existence of quantized torsional excitations, or "torsionons".
### 1.3 The Scale Conundrum: Constraints on Torsion Magnitude
Any successful theoretical extension must confront the severe empirical limits placed on torsion. Existing experimental bounds constrain the observable magnitude of torsion to extremely small values. Measurements have limited the axial torsion vector component K_z to \leq 1.5 \times 10^{-15} \text{ m}^{-1}.
Furthermore, astrophysical analyses demonstrate that torsion effects from known high-density sources, such as uniformly rotating neutron stars, are negligible when compared to the star's standard energy density effects. Significant torsion effects are only predicted to arise at densities far beyond nuclear saturation, such as those expected near black hole singularities or in the early universe. A caveat exists: if the source of torsion is ultralight fermionic particles (with masses around 10^{-9} to 10^{-12} eV), often considered dark matter candidates, their spin contribution relative to the matter density becomes significantly larger, making them detectable even at nuclear saturation densities.
Therefore, if the Torsion-Consciousness coupling is to manifest with a measurable impact on spacetime geometry, the coupling constant must either be extremely massive (which would violate existing weak-field tests) or, critically, the Informational Source (\mathcal{I}_{\mu\nu}) must involve exotic, ultralight quantum fields, suggesting an intrinsic link between consciousness and dark sector physics.
