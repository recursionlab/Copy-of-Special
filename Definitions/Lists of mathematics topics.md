**Lists of mathematics topics** cover a variety of topics related to [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"). Some of these lists link to hundreds of articles; some link to only a few. The template below includes links to alphabetical lists of all mathematical articles. This article brings together the same content organized in a manner better suited for browsing. Lists cover aspects of basic and advanced mathematics, methodology, mathematical statements, integrals, general concepts, mathematical objects, and reference tables. They also cover equations named after people, societies, mathematicians, journals, and meta-lists.

The purpose of this list is *not* similar to that of the [Mathematics Subject Classification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics_Subject_Classification "Mathematics Subject Classification") formulated by the [American Mathematical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Mathematical_Society "American Mathematical Society"). Many mathematics journals ask authors of research papers and expository articles to list subject codes from the Mathematics Subject Classification in their papers. The subject codes so listed are used by the two major reviewing databases, *[Mathematical Reviews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_Reviews "Mathematical Reviews")* and *[Zentralblatt MATH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zentralblatt_MATH "Zentralblatt MATH")*. This list has some items that would not fit in such a classification, such as [list of exponential topics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_exponential_topics "List of exponential topics") and [list of factorial and binomial topics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_factorial_and_binomial_topics "List of factorial and binomial topics"), which may surprise the reader with the diversity of their coverage.

#### Combinatorics

[Combinatorics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combinatorics "Combinatorics") concerns the study of [discrete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Countable_set "Countable set") (and usually [finite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_set "Finite set")) objects. Aspects include "counting" the objects satisfying certain criteria (*[enumerative combinatorics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enumerative_combinatorics "Enumerative combinatorics")*), deciding when the criteria can be met, and constructing and analyzing objects meeting the criteria (as in *[combinatorial designs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combinatorial_design "Combinatorial design") and [matroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matroid "Matroid") theory*), finding "largest", "smallest", or "optimal" objects (*[extremal combinatorics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extremal_combinatorics "Extremal combinatorics")* and *[combinatorial optimization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combinatorial_optimization "Combinatorial optimization")*), and finding [algebraic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra "Algebra") structures these objects may have (*[algebraic combinatorics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_combinatorics "Algebraic combinatorics")*).

#### Number theory

The branch of mathematics deals with the properties and relationships of numbers, especially positive integers. [Number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory") is a branch of pure mathematics devoted primarily to the study of the integers and integer-valued functions. German mathematician Carl Friedrich Gauss said, "Mathematics is the queen of the sciences—and number theory is the queen of mathematics." Number theory also studies the natural, or whole, numbers. One of the central concepts in number theory is that of the [prime number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_number "Prime number"), and there are many questions about primes that appear simple but whose resolution continues to elude mathematicians.

#### Dynamical systems and differential equations

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Limitcycle.svg/250px-Limitcycle.svg.png)

Phase portrait of a continuous-time dynamical system, the Van der Pol oscillator.

A [differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equation "Differential equation") is an equation involving an unknown function and its derivatives.

In a [dynamical system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamical_system "Dynamical system"), a fixed rule describes the time dependence of a point in a geometrical space. The mathematical models used to describe the swinging of a clock pendulum, the flow of water in a pipe, or the number of fish each spring in a lake are examples of dynamical systems.

#### Information theory and signal processing

[Information theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_theory "Information theory") is a branch of [applied mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Applied_mathematics "Applied mathematics") and [social science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_science "Social science") involving the quantification of [information](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information "Information"). Historically, information theory was developed to find fundamental limits on compressing and reliably [communicating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communication "Communication") data.

[Signal processing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signal_processing "Signal processing") is the analysis, interpretation, and manipulation of [signals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signal_\(electrical_engineering\) "Signal (electrical engineering)"). Signals of interest include [sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Audio_signal_processing "Audio signal processing"), [images](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_processing "Image processing"), biological signals such as [ECG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ECG "ECG"), [radar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radar "Radar") signals, and many others. Processing of such signals includes [filtering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filter_\(signal_processing\) "Filter (signal processing)"), storage and reconstruction, separation of information from [noise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noise "Noise"), [compression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_compression "Data compression"), and [feature extraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feature_extraction "Feature extraction").

#### Game theory

[Game theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Game_theory "Game theory") is a branch of [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") that uses [models](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Model_\(abstract\) "Model (abstract)") to study interactions with formalized incentive structures ("games"). It has applications in a variety of fields, including [economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economics "Economics"), [anthropology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthropology "Anthropology"), [political science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_science "Political science"), [social psychology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_psychology "Social psychology") and [military strategy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_strategy "Military strategy").

[Mathematicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician") study and research in all the different areas of mathematics. The publication of new discoveries in mathematics continues at an immense rate in hundreds of scientific journals, many of them devoted to mathematics and many devoted to subjects to which mathematics is applied (such as theoretical [computer science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science "Computer science") and [theoretical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theoretical_physics "Theoretical physics")).
