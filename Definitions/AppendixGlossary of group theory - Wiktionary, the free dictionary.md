## A

[abelian group](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/abelian_group "abelian group")

A group ${\displaystyle (G,*)}$ is *abelian* if ${\displaystyle *}$ is commutative, i.e. ${\displaystyle g*h=h*g}$ for all ${\displaystyle g,h\in G}$. Likewise, a group is *nonabelian* if this relation fails to hold for any pair ${\displaystyle g,h\in G}$.

## C

[coset](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/coset "coset")

If ${\displaystyle G}$ is a [group](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/group "group"), and ${\displaystyle H}$ is a [subgroup](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/subgroup "subgroup") of ${\displaystyle G}$, and ${\displaystyle g}$ is an element of ${\displaystyle G}$, then *gH* = {  *gh*: *h* ∈ *H*  } is the **left coset** of *H* in *G* with respect to *g*, and *Hg* = {  *hg*: *h* ∈ *H*  } is the **right coset** of *H* in *G* with respect to *g*.

## D

[direct product](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/direct_product "direct product")

**[direct sum](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/direct_sum "direct sum")**, and **[semidirect product](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/semidirect_product "semidirect product")** of groups. These are ways of combining groups to construct new groups; please refer to the corresponding links for explanation.

## F

[factor group](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/quotient_group "quotient group")

Or **quotient group**. Given a group *G* and a normal subgroup *N* of *G*, the *quotient group* is the set *G* / *N* of left cosets { *aN*: *a* ∈ *G* } together with the operation *aN* \* *bN* = *abN*. The relationship between normal subgroups, homomorphisms, and factor groups is summed up in the [fundamental theorem on homomorphisms](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=fundamental_theorem_on_homomorphisms&action=edit&redlink=1 "fundamental theorem on homomorphisms (page does not exist)").

[finitely generated group](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=finitely_generated_group&action=edit&redlink=1 "finitely generated group (page does not exist)")

If there exists a finite set *S* such that *<* S *\>* = *G*, then *G* is said to be *finitely generated*. If S can be taken to have just one element, G is a [cyclic group](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cyclic_group "cyclic group") of finite order, an [infinite cyclic group](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=infinite_cyclic_group&action=edit&redlink=1 "infinite cyclic group (page does not exist)"), or possibly a group {e} with just one element.

[free group](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/free_group "free group")

Given any set *A*, one can define a multiplication of words as follows: (abb)\*(bca)=abbbca. The free group generated by *A* is the smallest *group* containing this [semigroup](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/semigroup "semigroup").

## G

[general linear group](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/general_linear_group "general linear group")

Denoted by GL(*n*, *F*), is the group of *n* -by- *n* [invertible matrices](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/invertible_matrix "invertible matrix"), where the elements of the matrices are taken from a [field](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/field "field") *F* such as the real numbers or the complex numbers.

[group](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/group "group")

A set together with an [associative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/associative "associative") operation which admits an [identity element](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/identity_element "identity element") and such that every element has an [inverse](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=inverse_element&action=edit&redlink=1 "inverse element (page does not exist)").

[group isomorphism](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=group_isomorphism&action=edit&redlink=1 "group isomorphism (page does not exist)")

Group homomorphisms that have [inverse functions](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/inverse_function "inverse function"). The inverse of an isomorphism, it turns out, must also be a homomorphism.

[group homomorphism](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=group_homomorphism&action=edit&redlink=1 "group homomorphism (page does not exist)")

These are functions *f*: (*G*,\*) → (*H*,×) that have the special property that  
*f* (*a* \* *b*) = *f* (*a*) × *f* (*b*)  
for any elements *a* and *b* of *G*.

[group representation](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=group_representation&action=edit&redlink=1 "group representation (page does not exist)")

(not to be confused with the *presentation* of a group). A homomorphism from a group to a general linear group. One basically tries to "represent" a given abstract group as a concrete group of invertible [matrices](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/matrix "matrix") which is much easier to study.

## I

isomorphic groups

Two groups are *isomorphic* if there exists a group isomorphism mapping from one to the other. Isomorphic groups can be thought of as essentially the same, only with different labels on the individual elements.

## K

[kernel](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/kernel "kernel")

Given a group homomorphism, the [preimage](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/preimage "preimage") of the identity in the [codomain](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/codomain "codomain") of the group homomorphism. Every normal subgroup is the kernel of a group homomorphism and vice versa.

## N

[normal subgroup](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/normal_subgroup "normal subgroup")

*H* is a normal subgroup of *G* if for all *g* in *G* and *h* in *H*, *g* \* *h* \* *g* <sup>−1</sup> also belongs to *H*.

## O

[order](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/order "order")

Of a group ${\displaystyle (G,*)}$, the [cardinality](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cardinal_number "cardinal number") (i.e. number of elements) of ${\displaystyle G}$. (A group with finite order is called a *finite* group.)

[order](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/order "order")

Of an element of a group. Suppose ${\displaystyle x\in G}$ and there exists a [positive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/positive "positive") [integer](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/integer "integer") ${\displaystyle m}$ such that ${\displaystyle x^{m}=e}$, then the smallest possible ${\displaystyle m}$ is called the *order* of ${\displaystyle x}$. The order of a finite group is [divisible](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/divisible "divisible") by the order of every element.

## P

[p-group](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=p-group&action=edit&redlink=1 "p-group (page does not exist)")

If *p* is [prime](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/prime_number "prime number"), then a *p* -group is a group *G* where for each element *g* ∈ *G*, there exists a positive integer *m* such that the order of *g* is *p <sup>m</sup>*. A finite group is a p-group if and only if its group order (i.e. the number of its elements) is itself a power of p.

p-subgroup

A subgroup which is also *p* -group. (The study of *p* -subgroups is the central object of the [Sylow theorems](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Sylow_theorems "Sylow theorems").)

## S

[simple group](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/simple_group "simple group")

Simple groups are those groups with { *e* } and itself as the only normal subgroups. The name is misleading as its structure could be extremely complex. An example is the [monster group](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/monster_group "monster group"), a group of order more than one million. Every finite group is built up from simple groups through the use of [group extensions](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=extension_problem&action=edit&redlink=1 "extension problem (page does not exist)"), so the study and classification of finite simple groups is central to the study of finite groups in general. As a result of extensive effort over the second half of the 20th century, the finite simple groups have all been [classified](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classification_of_finite_simple_groups "w:Classification of finite simple groups").

[subgroup](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/subgroup "subgroup")

Given group (*G*,\*), a [subset](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/subset "subset") *H* which remains a group when the operation \* is restricted to *H*. Given a set *S* of *G*. We denote by *<* S *\>* the smallest subgroup of *G* containing *S*.
