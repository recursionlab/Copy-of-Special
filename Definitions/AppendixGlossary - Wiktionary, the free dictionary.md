## Symbols

\*

An [asterisk](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/asterisk#Noun "asterisk") appearing before a term (an affix, a root, a word, etc.), indicates the term is not attested but reconstructed; for example, *[\*werdʰh₁om](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Indo-European/werd%CA%B0h%E2%82%81om "Reconstruction:Proto-Indo-European/werdʰh₁om")* is the reconstructed Proto-Indo-European word for *word*.

\[ \]

[Brackets](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bracket#Noun "bracket") surrounding a quotation indicate that the quotation either contains a mere mention of a term (for example, "some people find the word ***manoeuvre*** hard to spell") rather than an actual use of it (for example, "we need to **manoeuvre** carefully to avoid causing upset"), or does not provide an actual instance of a term but provides information about a related term.

## A

a.

" [Ante](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ante "ante") " (Latin for "before"). Hence, a quotation from " *a.* 1924" is a quotation from no later than the year 1923.

[abbreviation](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/abbreviation "abbreviation")

A shortened form of a word or phrase, such as an [initialism](#initialism), [acronym](#acronym), or [clipping](#clipping).

[ablative case](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ablative_case "ablative case")

A [case](#case) that indicates separation, or moving away from something. It is used alone or with certain [prepositions](#preposition). For example, if English had a fully [productive](#productive) case system that included the ablative case, then in the phrase *came from the city*, either "the city" or "from the city" would likely be in the ablative. In some languages, such as Latin, this case has acquired many other uses and does not strictly indicate separation anymore.

[ablaut](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ablaut "ablaut")

In [Proto-Indo-European](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Proto-Indo-European "Proto-Indo-European"), or any of its descendants (the [Indo-European](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Indo-European "Indo-European") languages), a system of [vowel](#vowel) alternation in which the vowels that are used in various parts of the word can change depending on meaning. The system is used for purposes of [inflection](#inflection) and word derivation. In the Germanic languages, it forms the basis of the [strong verbs](#strong_verb). A specific form of ablaut is referred to as a **grade**; see for instance [zero-grade](#zero-grade). Compare also [umlaut](#umlaut).

[absolutive case](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/absolutive_case "absolutive case")

A [case](#case) used to indicate the [patient](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/patient#Noun "patient") or [experiencer](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/experiencer "experiencer") of a [verb](#verb) 's action.

[abstract noun](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/abstract_noun "abstract noun")

A [noun](#noun) that denotes an idea, emotion, feeling, quality or other abstract or intangible concept, as opposed to a concrete item, or a physical object. [Antonym](#antonym) of [concrete noun](#concrete_noun).

[abstract verb](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/abstract_verb "abstract verb")

*See **[multidirectional verb](#multidirectional_verb)**.*

[accusative case](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/accusative_case "accusative case"), acc.

A [case](#case) that is usually used as the [direct object](#direct_object) of a [verb](#verb). For example, if English had a fully [productive](#productive) case system, then *ball* in "The man threw the ball" would most likely be in the accusative.

[acronym](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/acronym "acronym")

An [abbreviation](#abbreviation), usually formed from the initial letters of a phrase, that is pronounced as the “word” it would spell, such as *[NATO](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/NATO#English "NATO")*.

Contrast an [initialism](#initialism), where the letters are pronounced individually, and a [syllabic abbreviation](#syllabic_abbreviation), which is formed from syllables (rather than letters) of the underlying phrase.

[active voice](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/active_voice "active voice")

The [voice](#voice) verb form in which the grammatical [subject](#subject) is the person or thing doing the action (compare [passive voice](#passive_voice)), e.g. *The boy kicked the ball.* (See also *[voice](#voice)*)

[acute accent](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/acute_accent "acute accent")

A [diacritic](#diacritic) mark ( ´ ) used that can be placed above a number of letters in many languages of the Latin, Greek and Cyrillic writing systems.

[AD](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/AD "AD")

[Anno Domini](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/anno_Domini "anno Domini") (Latin for "in the year of the Lord"). Year-numbering system equivalent to [CE](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/CE "CE").

[adapted](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/adapt "adapt") [borrowing](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/borrowing "borrowing")

A [loanword](#loanword) formed with the addition of an [affix](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/affix "affix") to conform the term to the normal [morphology](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/morphology "morphology") of the [language](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/language "language"), e.g. Polish *[normatywny](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/normatywny#Polish "normatywny")*, borrowed from French *[normatif](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/normatif#French "normatif")* and adapted with Polish *[\-ny](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-ny#Polish "-ny")*.

Contrast [unadapted borrowings](#unadapted_borrowing).

*adj*

*See **[adjective](#adjective)**.*

[adjective](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/adjective "adjective")

A word like *[big](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/big#English "big")* or *[childish](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/childish#English "childish")* that usually serves to modify a [noun](#noun).

[admirative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/admirative "admirative")

A verb form similar to [mirative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mirative "mirative"), found primarily in some languages of the Balkan sprachbund (i.e. namely Albanian, Bulgarian and Macedonian), which expresses surprise, irony, doubt, or reportedness on the part of the speaker.

[adverb](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/adverb "adverb")

A word like *[very](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/very#English "very")*, *[wickedly](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/wickedly#English "wickedly")* or *[often](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/often#English "often")* that usually serves to modify an [adjective](#adjective), [verb](#verb), or other adverb.

[adverbial](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/adverbial "adverbial")

Relating to an [adverb](#adverb). For example, an *adverbial participle* is a [participle](#participle) that functions like an adverb in a sentence.

[adverbial clause](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/adverbial_clause "adverbial clause")

A type of [dependent clause](#dependent_clause) that modifies a verb in an [adverbial](#adverbial) fashion. Examples are ***When my friend arrives**, I will take him out to dinner* and ***If it rains**, I will go home* (the latter example being specifically a *conditional clause*).

[affix](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/affix "affix")

A [bound morpheme](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bound_morpheme "bound morpheme") added to a word’s stem; a [prefix](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/prefix "prefix"), [suffix](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/suffix "suffix"), [interfix](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/interfix "interfix"), etc. In the narrow sense, a synonym of **[suffix](#suffix)**.

[affricate](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/affricate "affricate")

A [consonant](#consonant) sound made up of a [plosive](#plosive) and a [fricative](#fricative) pronounced simultaneously, together behaving within a language's [phonology](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/phonology "phonology") as if one single consonant. English has the affricates /t͡ʃ/, as in ***ch** ur **ch***, and /d͡ʒ/, as in ***j** u **dg** e*. Some languages are known to distinguish affricates from an otherwise-identical sequence of a plosive followed by a fricative, such as Polish *[czy](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/czy#Polish "czy")* /t͡ʂɘ/ vs. *[trzy](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/trzy#Polish "trzy")* /tʂɘ/.

[agent noun](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/agent_noun "agent noun")

A noun that denotes an [agent](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/agent#English:_grammar "agent") that does the action denoted by the verb from which the noun is derived, such as "cutter" derived from "to cut". Such an agent can be either a person or a thing, and either [sentient](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sentient#English "sentient") or [nonsentient](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nonsentient#English "nonsentient"): thus definitions often begin, " *One who, or that which,* \[does X\]."

AHD

The [American Heritage Dictionary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Heritage_Dictionary "w:American Heritage Dictionary").

For historical reasons, this abbreviation is sometimes used here to identify a respelled pronunciation that is given in [enPR](#enPR) form.

[alliteration](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/alliteration "alliteration")

The repetition of sounds or letters at the beginning of two or more words immediately succeeding each other.

alternative form

In definitions: An "alternative form" of a given word is another spelling of that word which is pronounced differently: for example, *killikinick* is an alternative form of *kinnikinnick*. (A spelling which is pronounced the same is an "alternative spelling": for example, *judgement* is an alternative spelling of *judgment*.)

As a header: The header "Alternative forms" [encompasses](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Wiktionary:Entry_layout#Alternative_forms "Wiktionary:Entry layout") both alternative spellings (*judgement*, *judgment*; *sulphur*, *sulfur*) and alternative forms (*killikinick*, *kinnikinnick*), as well as differences in hyphenation (*tea-cup*, *teacup*), capitalization (*LASER*, *laser*), and other similar differences (*naïveté*, *naiveté*).

[ambitransitive verb](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ambitransitive_verb "ambitransitive verb")

Capable of being either [transitive](#transitive) or [intransitive](#intransitive) depending on usage. For instance, *[eat](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/eat#English "eat")* and *[read](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/read#English "read")* optionally take a [direct object](#direct_object): "I eat daily", "She likes to read" (both intransitive), "Read this book", "I do not eat meat" (both transitive). [Ergative verbs](#ergative_verb) (q.v.) are a kind of ambitransitive verb. Compare [ditransitive verb](#ditransitive_verb).

[analogy](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/analogy "analogy")

An etymological process in which a word or form is created after a certain pattern in an attempt to right a perceived irregularity. For example, in English, *[dove](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dove#English "dove")* as the past tense of *[dive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dive#English "dive")* (instead of *[dived](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dived#English "dived")*) is by analogy with strong verbs like *[drive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/drive#English "drive")* → *[drove](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/drove#English "drove")* and *[weave](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/weave#English "weave")* → *[wove](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/wove#English "wove")*.

[anaphoricity](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/anaphoricity "anaphoricity")

Anaphorical pronouns are personal or demonstrative pronouns that refer to an earlier-mentioned person or object. See also: [Wikipedia's article on *anaphoricity*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaphora_\(linguistics\) "w:Anaphora (linguistics)")

[anglicisation](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/anglicisation "anglicisation"), [anglicization](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/anglicization "anglicization")

The modification of a foreign ([borrowed](#loanword)) word to make it more English in form.

[annexed state](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/annexed_state "annexed state")

In [Berber languages](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Category:Berber_languages "Category:Berber languages"), a grammatical [case](#case) or [state](#state) of the [noun](#noun), part of a binary opposition with the [free state](#free_state). The annexed state is characterized by modification of the noun's initial syllable: in Northern Berber, [masculine](#masculine) nouns typically take a *w-* or *y-* prefix (e.g. *[argaz](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/argaz#Kabyle "argaz")* (“man”) → *[wergaz](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=wergaz&action=edit&redlink=1 "wergaz (page does not exist)")*), while [feminine](#feminine) nouns may lose their initial vowel. The annexed state marks a noun in a [dependency](#dependency) relationship: after most [prepositions](#preposition), as the [subject](#subject) of a [verb](#verb), after numerals, or as a [determiner](#determiner) of another noun. Also called **[construct state](#construct_state)**.

Contrast *[free state](#free_state)*.

[angry](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/angry "angry") [register](#register)

Belonging to the angry linguistic [register](#register), used only when the speaker is angry. This register is quite rare, but is found in the [Bikol languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bikol_languages "w:Bikol languages") of the central Philippines.

[animate](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/animate "animate")

Having a [referent](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/referent "referent") that includes a human or animal. Many languages (such as the [Slavic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavic_languages "w:Slavic languages")) classify nouns based on animacy, using different [inflections](#inflection) or words with animate and inanimate nouns.

[antepenultima](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/antepenultima "antepenultima")

The third-to-last [syllable](#syllable) of a word, before the [penultima](#penultima).

[antonym](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/antonym "antonym")

A word with a meaning that is the opposite of a meaning of another word. For example, *[good](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/good#English "good")* is an antonym of *[bad](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bad#English "bad")*.

Contrast [synonym](#synonym).

[aorist](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/aorist "aorist")

A grammatical category of [verbs](#verb) that is often a [perfective](#perfective) past.

[apheresis](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/apheresis "apheresis")

The removal or omission of the initial sound or syllable from a word. Examples in English are *[scarp](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/scarp#English "scarp")* from earlier *[escarp](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/escarp#English "escarp")*, and colloquial *[splain](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/splain#English "splain")* from *[explain](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/explain#English "explain")*.

[aphesis](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/aphesis "aphesis")

The removal or omission of an initial unstressed vowel from a word, a type of [apheresis](#apheresis).

[apocope](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/apocope "apocope")

The removal or omission of the final sound or syllable of a word. Frequently occurs in Italian, Spanish, and other Romance languages.

[applicative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/applicative "applicative")

A grammatical voice or verb form where an oblique argument of the verb is promoted to a core argument.

[approximant](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/approximant "approximant")

A [consonant](#consonant) sound produced by restricting the air flow through the mouth only slightly, resulting in a smooth sound. In English, the approximants are /l/, /ɹ/, /w/, /j/ (as in the initial sounds of *loo*, *rue*, *woo* and *you*). Approximants are distinguished from [fricatives](#fricative), in which the air is constricted enough to cause a rough, hissing or buzzing sound, and [plosives](#plosive), in which the air is blocked completely for a short period of time.

[archaic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/archaic "archaic")

No longer in general use, but still found in some contemporary texts that aim for an antique style, like historical novels. For example, *[thee](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/thee#English "thee")* and *[thou](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/thou#English "thou")* are archaic pronouns, having been almost completely superseded by *[you](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/you#English "you")*. *Archaic* is a stronger term than *[dated](#dated)*, but not as strong as *[obsolete](#obsolete)*. (See [Wiktionary:Obsolete and archaic terms](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Wiktionary:Obsolete_and_archaic_terms "Wiktionary:Obsolete and archaic terms").) Whereas an archaic term names a still-extant thing or non-outdated concept, a [historical](#historical) term names a former thing or outdated concept.

[areal](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/areal "areal")

Distributed across multiple languages inhabiting a particular area, due to [language contact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/language_contact "w:language contact") among them rather than due to [inheritance](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/inheritance "inheritance") from a common ancestor. The term can be applied either to words (see *[loanword](#loanword)*) or features of languages such as in [grammar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/grammar "w:grammar"), [morphology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/morphology "w:morphology"), and [phonology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/phonology "w:phonology"). See also *[Wanderwort](#wanderwort)* and *[stratum](#stratum)*.

[article](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/article "article")

A type of [determiner](#determiner) that is used as a grammatical indicator in some languages, and is usually central to the grammar and syntax of that language. In English, the articles are the [definite](#definite) article *[the](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/the#English "the")*, and the [indefinite](#indefinite) articles *[a](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/a#English "a")* and *[an](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/an#English "an")*. Some languages may have more articles, such as the French [partitive](#partitive) articles *[du](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/du#French "du")*, *[de la](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/de_la#French "de la")* and *[des](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/des#French "des")*, while many languages lack articles altogether.

[aspect](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/aspect "aspect")

A property of a verb form indicating the nature of an action as [perfective](#perfective) (complete) or [imperfective](#imperfective) (incomplete or continuing).

[aspirated *h*](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/aspirated_h "aspirated h")

In French, an initial ⟨h⟩ that is treated as a [consonant](#consonant); that is to say, [liaison](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/liaison "liaison") and [elision](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/elision "elision") are not permitted at the beginning of a word with an aspirated ⟨h⟩.

[assimilation](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/assimilation "assimilation")

Assimilation is a common phonological process by which one sound becomes more like a nearby sound. This can occur either within a word or between words. See also *[dissimilation](#dissimilation)*.

[atelic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/atelic "atelic")

The [aspect](#aspect) of a verb that denotes an action without a definite endpoint or a goal that is tended towards, or rather an action with [cumulative reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cumulativity_\(linguistics\) "w:Cumulativity (linguistics)") (such that the expression for that action may describe two or more actions of that kind that, when combined, still constitute a form of that action); contrast **[telic](#telic)**. A kind of telicity distinction can be seen in English when specifying a duration in a (simple past) verb phrase: atelic verb phrases take *[for](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/for#English "for")* (*I built a house for an hour.* *She loved me for years.*) (See also **[Telicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telicity "w:Telicity")** on Wikipedia.[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telicity "w:Telicity") )

[attributive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/attributive "attributive")

*See also: [Appendix:English nouns § Attributive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:English_nouns#Attributive "Appendix:English nouns")*
1. An adjective that stands in a syntactic position where it directly modifies a noun, as opposed to a [predicative](#predicative) adjective, which stands in a [predicate](#predicate) position but which modifies the [subject](#subject) of the [clause](#clause). For example, in *the big green house*, *big* and *green* are attributive adjectives, whereas in *The house is big and green*, *big* and *green* are predicative adjectives. Some adjectives are only-attributive like *[close](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/close#Adjective "close")* (in *a close friend*) or *[plain](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/plain#English "plain")* (in *plain nonsense*).
2. A noun or adjective (or phrase) that names a real object with the *attributes* of another real object. For example, a [noun adjunct](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/noun_adjunct "noun adjunct"). This is in contrast to a [substantive](#substantive) noun or adjective, which names a real object that is the actual *substance* named by the noun or adjective.

[augment](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/augment "augment")

1. In some [Indo-European](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Indo-European "Indo-European") languages, a prefixed [vowel](#vowel) (e.g., Sanskrit *[अ-](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E0%A4%85-#Sanskrit:_augment "अ-")* (a-) and Ancient Greek *[ἐ-](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%90-#Ancient_Greek "ἐ-")* (e-)) indicating a past tense in a verb.
2. In [Celtic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Celtic "Celtic") languages, especially Old Irish, a preverb, usually *[ro-](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ro-#Old_Irish "ro-")*, used to give a verb a resultative or potential meaning.
3. In certain Bantu languages, a [morpheme](#morpheme) prefixed to a [noun class](#noun_class) prefix.

[augmentative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/augmentative "augmentative")

A word form expressing large size, importance, intensity, or seniority.

[augmentless](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/augmentless "augmentless")

Lacking an [augment](#augment).

[auxiliary verb](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/auxiliary_verb "auxiliary verb") or [auxiliary](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/auxiliary "auxiliary")

A verb that accompanies another verb in a clause. It is used to indicate distinctions in [tense](#tense), [mood](#mood), [voice](#voice), [aspect](#aspect) or other grammatical nuances. English examples are *[can](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/can#English "can")*, *[will](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/will#English "will")*, *[have](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/have#English "have")*, *[be](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/be#English "be")*.

[avoidance](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/avoidance "avoidance") term

A word standardly used to replace a [taboo](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/taboo "taboo") word.

## B

[back-formation](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/back-formation "back-formation")

A term formed by reinterpretation, or [reanalysis](#reanalysis) of an older term, often by removing an apparent or real prefix or suffix from it; for example, the noun *[pea](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pea#English "pea")* arose because the final /z/ sound in *[pease](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pease#English "pease")* sounded like a plural suffix. Similarly, the verb *[edit](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/edit#English "edit")* is a back-formation from the earlier noun *[editor](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/editor#English "editor")*. Not to be confused with [clipping](#clipping), which just shortens a word without changing meaning or part of speech.

[back vowel](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/back_vowel "back vowel")

Vowel sound produced in the back of the mouth. Examples in English include /uː/ in [rule](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rule#English "rule") or /əʊ/ in [pole](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pole#English "pole").

Contrast [front vowel](#front_vowel).

[backronym](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/backronym "backronym")

A word that is either not an [acronym](#acronym) but is taken to be one and for which a full form is invented by [back-formation](#back-formation), or is an acronym and for which an alternative full form is invented by back-formation.

[backslang](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/backslang "backslang")

A form of [slang](#slang) in which the spelling of words is reversed.

[bahuvrihi](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bahuvrihi "bahuvrihi")

Another term for an *[exocentric](#exocentric) compound*.

Contrast *[tatpurusa](#tatpurusa)*, *[karmadharaya](#karmadharaya)* and *[dvandva](#dvandva)*.

[barytone](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/barytone "barytone")

With the stress elsewhere than upon the final syllable. Compare with [oxytone](#oxytone), [paroxytone](#paroxytone), and [proparoxytone](#proparoxytone).

base state

In Luganda, the form of a [noun](#noun) in which there is not an [initial vowel](#initial_vowel).

Contrast *[topic state](#topic_state)*.

[BC](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/BC "BC")

Before Christ. Year-numbering system equivalent to [BCE](#BCE).

[BCE](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/BCE "BCE")

Before the Common, Current or Christian Era. Year-numbering system equivalent to [BC](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/BC "BC"). [AD](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/AD "AD") is expressed as [CE](#CE). To automatically switch most dates to use the "BC"/"AD" style, check the Gadgets section in [Special:Preferences](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Special:Preferences "Special:Preferences") (or [WT:PREFS](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Wiktionary:PREFS "Wiktionary:PREFS") if you do not have an account).

[blend](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/blend "blend")

A word or name that combines two words, typically starting with the start of one word and ending with the end of another, such as *[smog](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/smog#English "smog")* (from *[smoke](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/smoke#English "smoke")* and *[fog](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fog#English "fog")*). Many blends are [portmanteaus](#portmanteau).

See also *[merger](#merger)*.

[borrowing](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/borrowing "borrowing")

See *[loanword](#loanword)*.

[bound form](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bound_form "bound form")

A unit of language that can only be used as part of a word, not as a word on its own (such as the English suffix -ly). These types of [morphemes](#morpheme) are ‘bound’ in that they are restricted on where and how they are allowed to occur, needing to be attached to a freestanding morpheme in order to exist. Many suffixes are often bound.

[bowdlerization](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bowdlerize "bowdlerize")

The removal, from a text, of words or phrases that are considered offensive or vulgar.

[broadly](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/broadly "broadly")

Indicates a less precise (wider) use of words; short for *broadly speaking*. For most practical purposes, synonymous with *[loosely](#loosely)*.

Contrast *[strict](#strict)* (*narrow*) senses.

[broken plural](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/broken_plural "broken plural")

An [irregular](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/irregular "irregular") [plural](#plural) formed by changing the pattern of [vowels](#vowel) inside the [singular](#singular) form, found in the [Semitic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semitic_languages "w:Semitic languages") and other [Afroasiatic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afroasiatic_languages "w:Afroasiatic languages") such as the [Berber languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berber_languages "w:Berber languages"). Examples in Arabic are *[كُتُب](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D9%83%D8%AA%D8%A8#Arabic "كتب")* (kutub, “books”) and *[مَكَاتِب](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D9%85%D9%83%D8%A7%D8%AA%D8%A8#Arabic "مكاتب")* (makātib, “desks”), respectively the plural forms of *[كِتَاب](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D9%83%D8%AA%D8%A7%D8%A8#Arabic "كتاب")* (kitāb, “book”) and *[مَكْتَب](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D9%85%D9%83%D8%AA%D8%A8#Arabic "مكتب")* (maktab, “desk”). Compare plurals in English formed by internal processes such as [umlaut](#umlaut), as with *[teeth](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/teeth#English "teeth")*, plural of *[tooth](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tooth#English "tooth")*. Broken plurals contrast with *[sound plurals](#sound_plural)*, which are formed by adding an ending to the singular (similarly to how plurals are typically formed in English and most other languages).

c., ca.

" [Circa](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/circa "circa") " (Latin for "about"). Hence, a quotation from "c. 1924" or "ca. 1924" is a quotation from approximately 1924.

[calque](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/calque "calque")

A borrowing by word-for-word or [morpheme](#morpheme) -for-morpheme translation: a [loan translation](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/loan_translation "loan translation"). For example, the English expression *[it goes without saying](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/it_goes_without_saying#English "it goes without saying")* is a calque (a literal, word-for-word translation) of French *[ça va sans dire](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%C3%A7a_va_sans_dire#French "ça va sans dire")*, and *[flea market](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/flea_market#English "flea market")* is a calque of French *[marché aux puces](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/march%C3%A9_aux_puces#French "marché aux puces")* (literally “ [market](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/market "market") with [fleas](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/flea "flea") ”). Contrariwise, the term *[skyscraper](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/skyscraper#English "skyscraper")* was calqued into French as *[gratte-ciel](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gratte-ciel#French "gratte-ciel")* (literally “scrapes-sky”).

Calques are distinguished from [semantic loans](#semantic_loan) in that calques are not considered idiomatic expressions at the time that they are coined, whereas semantic loans would have had a pre-existing idiomatic meaning.

[case](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/case "case")

One of the forms of a noun, used to indicate its function in the phrase or sentence. Examples include: [nominative](#nominative_case), [accusative](#accusative_case), [genitive](#genitive_case), [dative](#dative_case).

cat.

[category](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/category "category")

A collection of entries, used to categorize or group entries of words that are similar in syntax (for example, English plural nouns) or in sense (for example, English words pertaining to sports); *see* [Wiktionary:Categorization](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Wiktionary:Categorization "Wiktionary:Categorization").

[catenative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/catenative "catenative") verb

A [verb](#verb) able to be immediately followed by the full or bare [infinitive](#infinitive), or [gerund](#gerund) (i.e. non-finite verbs).

[causative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/causative "causative") verb

A transitive verb that is derived from another verb, and expresses the act of making someone/something do the action of the base verb. Examples in English are: *[lay](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lay#English "lay")* (cause to *[lie](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lie#English "lie")*), *[set](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/set#English "set")* (cause to *[sit](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sit#English "sit")*) and *[raise](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/raise#English "raise")* (cause to *[rise](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rise#English "rise")*). Compare **[factitive verbs](#factitive)** (e.g. *[shorten](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/shorten#English "shorten")*, *[enslave](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/enslave#English "enslave")*), which are similar but are derived from adjectives or nouns.

[CE](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/CE "CE")

[censored](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/censored "censored")

Of a spelling, a word that is censored possibly as a euphemism, or sometimes to bypass the chat filter, as in Spanish *[mrd](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mrd#Spanish "mrd")* from *[mierda](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mierda#Spanish "mierda")* (“shit”).

[cf.](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cf. "cf.")

"Confer"; "see"; "compare" – often used to indicate a word with similar, or opposite meaning.

[childish](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/childish "childish")

Language used colloquially by babies or children. These terms should be avoided in formal language.

[circumfix](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/circumfix "circumfix")

An [affix](#affix) containing both a [prefixing](#prefix) and a [suffixing](#suffix) element existing as a single [morphological](#morphological) unit. For example, the word *[embolden](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/embolden#English "embolden")* consists of *[bold](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bold#English "bold")* (adjective) circumfixed with *[em- -en](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/em-_-en#English "em- -en")*, a variant of *[en- -en](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/en-_-en#English "en- -en")*.

[circumlocutory](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/circumlocutory "circumlocutory")

Refers to a roundabout or indirect way of speaking; the use of more words than necessary to express an idea.

[CJK](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/CJK "CJK"), [CJKV](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/CJKV "CJKV")

Chinese, Japanese, Korean, (and Vietnamese); [CJK characters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CJK_characters "w:CJK characters").

[classifier](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/classifier "classifier")

*See **[counter](#counter)**.*

[clause](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/clause "clause")

A word or group of words that functions as a single unit in the [syntax](#syntax) of a [sentence](#sentence), where the [head](#head) (or central) word is a [verb](#verb); normally distinguished from a [phrase](#phrase), which is a similar unit where the head word is some other [part of speech](#part_of_speech), such as a [noun](#noun), [adjective](#adjective) or [preposition](#preposition). For example, the [sentence](#sentence) *The man entered the house, which was large and blue* contains two clauses, the [independent clause](#independent_clause) *The man entered the house* and the [dependent clause](#dependent_clause) *which was large and blue* (here the dependent clause is specifically a [relative clause](#relative_clause), although not all dependent clauses are relative clauses).

[clipping](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/clipping "clipping")

A shortening of a word, without changing meaning or part of speech. Not to be confused with [back-formation](#back-formation), which changes meaning, or [ellipsis](#ellipsis), which shortens by omission of whole words. Also differs from [abbreviation](#abbreviation), which shortens the written—rather than spoken—form of a word or phrase.

See also *[apheresis](#apheresis)*, *[apocope](#apocope)*.

[clitic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/clitic "clitic")

A word that attaches to a phrase and cannot be used on its own, such as English *['s](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%27s#English "'s")*. Many languages have clitic [pronouns](#pronouns) ([weak pronouns](#weak_pronoun)), which may be contrasted with [emphatic](#emphatic) or [strong](#strong) pronouns; for example, English *['ll](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%27ll#English "'ll")* is a clitic version of *[will](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/will#English "will")* and generally attaches to the preceding word (*he'll*, *a little dab'll do ya*).

[cognate](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cognate "cognate")

[Descended](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/descend "descend") from the same source [lexeme](#lexeme) of an [ancestor](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ancestor "ancestor") language.

[coinage](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/coinage "coinage")

A new term intentionally created with a definite meaning.

[collective](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/collective "collective")

Expressing a collection or aggregate of individuals by a [singular](#singular) form.

[collocation](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/collocation "collocation")

A sequence of words or terms that are used together more often than by chance, i.e. that appears together relatively more often. A collocation under a particular definition of a polysemic term should reflect a sequence that distinguishes usage with the particular definition from most other definitions of the term.

[colloquial](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/colloquial "colloquial")

Denotes words belonging to a spoken or vernacular variety of a language. These words are only properly used in a casual or familiar context.

This label is only used with some languages; with others it is treated as a synonym of *[informal](#informal)*. If used, *colloquial* is stronger than *informal* (a general rule of thumb is that informal words may be used in the running text of a newspaper article, while a colloquial term would likely not appear without quotation marks).

**Note:** It is a common misconception that *colloquial* somehow denotes "local" or a word being " [regional](#regional) ". This is incorrect; the word root for *colloquial* is related to *[locution](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/locution#English "locution")*, not *[location](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/location#English "location")*.

[combining form](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/combining_form "combining form")

A particular form of a term which is used as an [affix](#affix). For example, *[Afro-](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Afro-#English "Afro-")* (as in words like *[Afrocentric](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Afrocentric#English "Afrocentric")*, *[Afro-American](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Afro-American#English "Afro-American")*) is the *combining form* of *[African](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/African#English "African")*.

[comitative case](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/comitative_case "comitative case"), com.

A [case](#case) that denotes accompaniment.

[common gender](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/common_gender "common gender"), c

"Of common [gender](#gender) ". Some languages have a distinct common gender that combines [masculine](#masculine) and [feminine](#feminine) but is distinguished from [neuter](#neuter). In other languages, a "noun of common gender" is a pair of nouns, one masculine and one feminine, that are identical in form, and that have the same sense except that one refers to men and the other to women. Distinguish [epicene](#epicene).

[comparable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/comparable "comparable")

Of an adjective or adverb: able to be compared, having [comparative](#comparative) and [superlative](#superlative) forms that end in *\-er* and *\-est* (adjectives only), or in conjunction with the words *more* or *most*, or sometimes *further* or *furthest*. Examples: *big*, *bigger*, and *biggest*; *talented*, *more talented*, and *most talented*; *upstairs*, *further upstairs*, and *furthest upstairs*. Some adjectives are truly **[incomparable](#incomparable)**, such as *daily*, *additional*, and *else*. Many other adjectives, such as *unique*, *existential*, and *bearable* are generally considered incomparable, but controversially so, where examples can be readily cited of something being " *more bearable* " or " *most perfect* ".

[comparative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/comparative "comparative")

An [inflection](#inflection), or different form, of a **[comparable](#comparable)** adjective showing a relative quality, usually denoting "to a greater extent" but not "to the ultimate extent". (See also *[superlative](#superlative)* and *[degrees of comparison](#degrees_of_comparison)*.) In English, the *comparative* form is usually formed by appending *\-er*, or using the word *more*. For example, the comparative of *hard* is " *harder* "; of *difficult*, " *more difficult* ".

[compound](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/compound "compound")

A word or name that combines two or more words without altering them, such as *[dishcloth](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dishcloth#English "dishcloth")* (from *[dish](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dish#English "dish")* and *[cloth](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cloth#English "cloth")*) or *[high school](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/high_school#English "high school")* (from *[high](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/high#English "high")* and *[school](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/school#English "school")*); the latter type is called an [open compound](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/open_compound "open compound"). Compound terms are indicated in etymologies using
```
{{compound}}
```
. (See also *[Wiktionary:Etymology § Compound](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Wiktionary:Etymology#Compound "Wiktionary:Etymology")*.)

[concrete noun](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/concrete_noun "concrete noun")

A [noun](#noun) that denotes something tangible or material, such as a person or place. [Antonym](#antonym) of [abstract noun](#abstract_noun).

[concrete verb](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/concrete_verb "concrete verb")

*See **[unidirectional verb](#unidirectional_verb)**.*

[conditional mood](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/conditional_mood "conditional mood")

The [mood](#mood) of a [verb](#verb) used to signify that something is [contingent](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/contingent "contingent") upon the outcome of something else. The conditional mood in English is normally introduced by the word *would*, as in *If I were rich, I would be happy*.

[conflation](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/conflation "conflation")

*See **[contamination](#contamination)**.*

[conjugation](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/conjugation "conjugation")

The [inflection](#inflection) of [verbs](#verb). See also *[declension](#declension)*.

[conjunction](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/conjunction "conjunction")

A word used to join other words or phrases together into sentences.

[connegative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/connegative "connegative")

A [verb](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verb "verb") form used with the [negative verb](#negative_verb).

[connotation](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/connotation "connotation")

The [subjective](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/subjective "subjective") and non [analytical](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/analytical "analytical") meaning (definition) of a word, such as its emotional overtones, as contrasted with its [objective](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/objective "objective") and [analytical](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/analytical "analytical") meaning (definition), which constitutes its [denotation](#denotation) (an example is given there).

[consonant](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/consonant "consonant")

Any sound that is not the dominant sound of a [syllable](#syllable), the dominant sound generally being a [vowel](#vowel).

[consonantism](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/consonantism "consonantism")

The sequence of [consonants](#consonant), or the quality peculiar to the consonants of a given word or group of words.

[consonant gradation](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/consonant_gradation "consonant gradation")

A form of consonant [mutation](#mutation) found in certain, particularly Uralic, languages, which is historically conditioned by the openness of the following syllable.

[construct state](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/construct_state "construct state")

In some languages, a grammatical form that is used in construing a noun or adjective with another noun or adjective. In the [Semitic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Semitic "Semitic") languages, the construct form is usually a noun modified by a following noun in a genitive construction. The construct state of such a noun *X* can usually be translated to English as *X of*. In [Persian](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Persian "Persian"), the construct state is typically used for all nouns and adjectives in a [noun phrase](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/noun_phrase "noun phrase") except the very last.

[contamination](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/contamination "contamination")

The influence of one term on the development of another term whereby they come to have similar meanings or a similar sound; [conflation](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/conflation "conflation"). Extreme cases of conflation can result in a [merger](#merger) or [blend](#blend) of two words.

[contraction](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/contraction "contraction")

A word or phrase shortened in speech, sometimes represented in writing with a [punctuation mark](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/punctuation_mark "punctuation mark") in place of missing letters (e.g. English *[do](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/do#English "do") [not](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/not#English "not")* → *[don't](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/don%27t#English "don't")* or Romanian *[nu](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nu#Romanian "nu") [e](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/e#Romanian "e")* → *[nu-i](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nu-i#Romanian "nu-i")*).

[contranym](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/contranym "contranym")

A term with two opposite meanings.

[conversion](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/conversion "conversion")

The process whereby a new word is created without changing the form, often by allowing the word to function as a new part of speech.

[coordinate term](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/coordinate_term "coordinate term")

A term that is a different type of the same [hypernym](#hypernym) (loosely "category"). *Car* and *bicycle* are coordinate terms to each other, both being [hyponyms](#hyponym) of a shared [hypernym](#hypernym) *vehicle*. Although the term can be applied broadly – e.g. *car* and *asteroid* are both *things* –, such usage is not useful in Wiktionary.

[coordinative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/coordinative "coordinative") [compound](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/compound "compound")

A [nominal](#nominal) [compound](#compound) in which the two parts are coordinated and the intended meaning of the compound as a whole is a combination of the two parts. Also known as a *[dvandva](#dvandva) compound*. Examples in English are not common but are found in many other languages, e.g. Greek *[μαχαιροπίρουνο](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BC%CE%B1%CF%87%CE%B1%CE%B9%CF%81%CE%BF%CF%80%CE%AF%CF%81%CE%BF%CF%85%CE%BD%CE%BF#Greek "μαχαιροπίρουνο")* (machairopírouno, “cutlery”, literally “knife-fork”) and Yiddish *[טאַטע־מאַמע](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D7%98%D7%90%D6%B7%D7%98%D7%A2%D6%BE%D7%9E%D7%90%D6%B7%D7%9E%D7%A2#Yiddish "טאַטע־מאַמע")* (tate-mame, “parents”, literally “papa-mama”).

Contrast [exocentric](#exocentric) and [endocentric](#endocentric) compounds.

[copula](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/copula "copula")

A verb that links and equates its [subject](#subject) with its object; also called a [linking verb](#linking_verb). The most common copula is the verb *to be*, but others exist, such as *to seem*, *to appear* and *to sound*. The object of a copula often has special properties. For example, it can be an [adjective](#adjective) (*John is **very tall**, the water runs **cold***) when most verbs require their objects to be [nouns](#noun) or [pronouns](#pronoun). In addition, in languages with [case](#case) distinctions, the object of a copula is most commonly in the [nominative](#nominative) case, while the object of other verbs is usually in a different case, such as the [accusative](#accusative) case. Many languages (e.g. Arabic, Hebrew, Russian) use a *null copula* (i.e. no word at all) in the present tense when English would use the words *am*, *are* or *is*.

[corpus](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/corpus "corpus")

A [collection](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/collection#Noun "collection") of [writings](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/writing#Noun "writing"), often a large one such as a [libraryful](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/libraryful#Noun "libraryful") of [documents](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/document#Noun "document") in a particular [language](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/language#Noun "language"), and sometimes a [subset](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/subset#Noun "subset"), such as those from a specific [era](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/era#Noun "era"), a specific [demographic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/demographic#Noun "demographic"), a specific [genre](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/genre#Noun "genre") or [register](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/register#English:_language_register "register"), or a particular [author](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/author#Noun "author"); also, such a collection in the form of an electronic [database](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/database "database") used for [linguistic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/linguistic "linguistic") analyses. [Dictionaries](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dictionary#Noun "dictionary") are built using the builders' own command of a language plus reliance on many [quotations](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/quotation "quotation") ([quotes](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/quote#Noun "quote")) from [corpora](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/corpus#Noun "corpus"), which in [lexicography](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lexicography "lexicography") are often called [citations](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/citation#Noun "citation"). Wiktionary offers help with finding and citing citations at [Wiktionary:Quotations/Resources](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Wiktionary:Quotations/Resources "Wiktionary:Quotations/Resources") and a deeper list at [Wiktionary:Corpora](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Wiktionary:Corpora "Wiktionary:Corpora").

[corruption](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/corruption#English:_usage_prescription "corruption")

A change in the form of a word, expression or text in a non-specific, sporadic or nonstandard way, often as a result of [misunderstanding](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/misunderstanding "misunderstanding"), [mispronunciation](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mispronunciation "mispronunciation") or [mistranscription](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mistranscription "mistranscription").

Some linguistic scientists avoid using this term for [language change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/language_change "w:language change") because it connotes [prescriptive](#prescriptive) value judgments about language changes that "should have been prevented" or that "have [debased](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/debase "debase") the language." In contrast, linguistic science's [epistemology](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/epistemology "epistemology") of language does not hold that a [pidgin](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pidgin#Noun "pidgin"), for example, is "inferior" to the languages from which it was formed. The term *corruption* as applied to language change was long widely used, though, even in dictionaries, and is still widely encountered. In many cases, where an older dictionary might say that word X is a corruption of word Y, some newer dictionaries might say that word X is by alteration from word Y.

[count form](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/count_form "count form"), [count plural](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/count_plural "count plural")

In some [Slavic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Slavic "Slavic") [languages](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/language "language"), such as [Bulgarian](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Bulgarian "Bulgarian") and [Russian](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Russian "Russian"), a [distinct](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/distinct "distinct") [plural](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/plural "plural") form taken by certain [nouns](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/noun "noun") when explicitly modified by a [quantity](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/quantity "quantity").

[countable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/countable "countable"), countable noun, [count noun](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/count_noun "count noun")

Describes a noun which can be freely used with the [indefinite article](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/indefinite_article "indefinite article") (*a* or *an* in English) and with numbers, and which therefore has a [plural](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/plural "plural") form. [Antonym](#antonym) of *[uncountable](#uncountable)* or *mass noun*.

[counter](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/counter "counter")

In linguistics, counters, [measure words](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/measure_word "measure word") or [classifiers](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/classifier "classifier") are words that are used in combination with a numeral to indicate an amount of something represented by some noun. They denote a unit or measurement and are used with [mass nouns](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mass_noun "mass noun"), and sometimes also with [count nouns](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/count_noun "count noun"). (Compare *[singulative](#singulative).*)

## D

[dated](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dated "dated")

Formerly in common use, and still in occasional use, but now unfashionable; for example, *[wireless](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/wireless#English "wireless")* in the sense of "broadcast radio tuner", *[groovy](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/groovy#English "groovy")*, and *[gay](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gay#English "gay")* in the sense of "bright" or "happy" are all dated. *Dated* is not as strong as *[archaic](#archaic)* or *[obsolete](#obsolete)*. See [Wiktionary:Obsolete and archaic terms](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Wiktionary:Obsolete_and_archaic_terms "Wiktionary:Obsolete and archaic terms").

[dative case](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dative_case "dative case"), dat.

A [case](#case) that is usually used as the [indirect object](#indirect_object) of a verb. For example, if English had a fully [productive](#productive) case system, then *him* in "She gave him the ball" would most likely be in the dative.

[declension](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/declension "declension")

The [inflection](#inflection) of [nouns](#noun) and words like them, or used together with them (i.e. [nominals](#nominal)). See also *[conjugation](#conjugation)*.

[defective](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/defective "defective")

Normally would be expected to have a full set of [inflected](#inflection) forms, but some of the inflections do not exist or are never used. Examples include the English defective verbs *[can](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/can#English "can")* and *[shall](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/shall#English "shall")*, which do not have [infinitive](#infinitive) forms (there is no \* *to can* or \* *to shall*), or the French verb *[falloir](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/falloir#French "falloir")*, whose finite forms occur only in the third person singular (*[il faut](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=il_faut&action=edit&redlink=1 "il faut (page does not exist)")*; there is no \* *je faus* or \* *ils fallent*).

defective spelling

In languages with [matres lectionis](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mater_lectionis "mater lectionis") ([consonant](#consonant) letters representing [vowels](#vowel)), the form including no additional ones. The opposite is called [plene spelling](#plene_spelling) when it is the standardized spelling and [excessive spelling](#excessive_spelling) when it has even more of those letters than the standard spelling requires.

[definite](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/definite "definite")

Refers to forms of words that present something as known, identified, or immediately identifiable; in English, this is the basic meaning of the [article](#article) *[the](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/the#English "the")*; in some languages, this is a nominal or adjectival [inflection](#inflection). In the Germanic languages, adjectives inflected as definite are referred to as "weak". In Hungarian, the definite conjugation is used to indicate [definite objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungarian_verbs#Definite_and_indefinite_conjugations "w:Hungarian verbs"), including *him*, *her*, *it*, *them*, and the formal *you*.

[degrees of comparison](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/degrees_of_comparison "degrees of comparison")

[Inflections](#inflection) of adjectives and adverbs which allow comparisons. English has three *degrees of comparison*: [positive](#positive), [comparative](#comparative) and [superlative](#superlative). Some other languages have other degrees, e.g.: [comparative superlative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/comparative_superlative "comparative superlative"), [relative superlative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/relative_superlative "relative superlative"), [elative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/elative "elative").

[demonym](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/demonym "demonym")

A [noun](#noun) or [adjective](#adjective) referring to an inhabitant or native of a specific place. Sometimes the demonym is clearly related to the place name, as in English *[Mexico](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Mexico#English "Mexico")* and corresponding demonym *[Mexican](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Mexican#English "Mexican")*, but sometimes it is not, as in the *[Netherlands](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Netherlands#English "Netherlands")* vs. *[Dutch](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Dutch#English "Dutch")*. Some languages (e.g. most Slavic languages) have different (but typically related) adjective and noun forms of a given demonym, while in other languages (e.g. most Romance languages), they are the same. English is inconsistent in this regard; contrast *[Mexican](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Mexican#English "Mexican")* (either an adjective or noun) with *[Swedish](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Swedish#English "Swedish")* (adjective) vs. *[Swede](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Swede#English "Swede")* (noun). In some cases in English, such as *[Chinese](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Chinese#English "Chinese")*, there is no single-word singular noun form of a demonym that is fully accepted as [standard](#standard) in modern usage, leading to locutions such as *Chinese person* or *person from China*.

[denominal](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/denominal "denominal")

Derived from a [noun](#noun).

[denotation](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/denotation "denotation")

The [objective](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/objective "objective") and [analytical](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/analytical "analytical") meaning (definition) of a word, as contrasted with its emotional overtones, which constitute its [connotation](#connotation). For example, the words *start* and *commence* usually have the same denotation (i.e., "begin"), but they often have ([subjective](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/subjective "subjective")) connotational difference (such that a parent might say, "start doing your homework as soon as you finish your snack and bathroom break" but would usually not say "commence doing your homework \[…\]" because it sounds odd in a certain way (out of place in the context, unless emphasizing the word for humor, vocabulary building, or some other special purpose).

[dependent](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dependent "dependent")

(In Greek and in the Gaelic languages) A verb form which is not used independently but preceded by a particle to form the negative or a tense form.

[dependent clause](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dependent_clause "dependent clause")

A [clause](#clause) that cannot stand on its own as a complete [sentence](#sentence), as opposed to an [independent clause](#independent_clause). Also known as a [subordinate clause](#subordinate_clause). Logically, a dependent clause modifies a word in another clause in the sentence. Common examples are (1) [relative clauses](#relative_clause) (also known as "adjective clauses" or "adjectival clauses"), which modify nouns (e.g. *The man **whom I saw yesterday** is leaving today*); (2) [adverbial clauses](#adverbial_clause), which modify verbs in an adverbial fashion (e.g. ***When it is time to leave**, I will go home*), and [noun clauses](#noun_clause), which take the place of nouns (e.g. *I said **that my name is John*** or *I suggested **that he leave***).

[deponent](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/deponent "deponent")

(In Greek, Latin, and some Gaelic and Nordic languages) A verb with an active meaning which conjugates in a passive manner.

[derived terms](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Wiktionary:Entry_layout_explained#Derived_terms "Wiktionary:Entry layout explained")

A post- [POS](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/POS "POS") heading listing terms in the same language that are morphological derivatives. Confusingly, "derived" is also used in etymology sections to indicate that the term comes from a term in *another* language, but is not directly [borrowed](#loanword) or [inherited](#inherited).

[derogatory](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/derogatory "derogatory")

*See **[pejorative](#pejorative)**.*

[descendants](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Wiktionary:Entry_layout_explained#Descendants "Wiktionary:Entry layout explained")

A post- [POS](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/POS "POS") heading listing terms in other languages that have borrowed or inherited the word.

[descriptive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/descriptive "descriptive")

Concerning [linguistic description](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/linguistic_description "w:linguistic description"): how a language is [in fact](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/in_fact "in fact") used (as evidenced by [corpora](#corpus)), as contrasted with how some people think that it [ought to](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ought_to "ought to") be used, which is sometimes different; compare *[prescriptive](#prescriptive)*. (Also: [Wiktionary's policy upholding description](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Wiktionary:Neutral_point_of_view#In_the_treatment_of_words "Wiktionary:Neutral point of view").)

[descriptive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/descriptive "descriptive") [compound](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/compound "compound")

A [nominal](#nominal) [compound](#compound) in which one part modifies the other, where both parts refer to the intended meaning of the compound as a whole. Also known as a *[karmadharaya](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/karmadharaya "karmadharaya") compound*. The intended meaning of the compound as a whole is an extension of the sum-of-parts meaning of the compound. Examples in English are " [blackboard](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/blackboard "blackboard") " (a type of board which is \[often\] black) and " [houseboat](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/houseboat "houseboat") " (a boat which is also a house).

Contrast [determinative](#determinative) compounds, where the modified component but not the modifier refer to the intended meaning as a whole, as in " [rainbow](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rainbow "rainbow") " and " [footstool](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/footstool "footstool") ". Descriptive compounds are a subtype of [endocentric](#endocentric) compounds, which are in turn contrasted with [exocentric](#exocentric) and [coordinative](#coordinative) compounds.

[desiderative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/desiderative "desiderative")

A verb form indicating a wish.

[determinate verb](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/determinate_verb "determinate verb")

In the [Slavic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavic_languages "w:Slavic languages"), another term for *[unidirectional verb](#unidirectional_verb)*.

[determinative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/determinative "determinative") [compound](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/compound "compound")

A [nominal](#nominal) [compound](#compound) in which one part modifies the other, where the modified component (but not the modifier) refers to the intended meaning of the compound as a whole. Also known as a *[tatpurusa](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tatpurusa "tatpurusa") compound*. Examples in English are " [rainbow](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rainbow "rainbow") " (a type of [bow](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bow "bow"), caused by the rain) and " [footstool](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/footstool "footstool") " (a type of [stool](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/stool "stool"), intended for one's feet).

Contrast [descriptive](#descriptive) compounds, where both the modifier and modified component refer to the intended meaning as a whole, as in " [blackboard](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/blackboard "blackboard") " or " [houseboat](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/houseboat "houseboat") ". Determinative compounds are a subtype of [endocentric](#endocentric) compounds, which in turn are contrasted with [exocentric](#exocentric) and [coordinative](#coordinative) compounds.

[determiner](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/determiner "determiner")

A noun modifier that expresses the in-context reference or quantity of a noun or noun phrase. Determiners are often considered adjectives, but in fact are not quite the same; for example, in English, *[big](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/big#English "big")* is an adjective, so “the big car” is grammatical while \*“He saw big car” is not, but *[some](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/some#English "some")* is a determiner, so \*“the some car” is not grammatical while “He saw some car” is. In English, adjectives can *sometimes* stand alone without a noun, while determiners nearly *always* can (contrast \*“He saw big” with “He saw some”), such that they are sometimes considered pronouns as well as adjectives.

[deverbal](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/deverbal "deverbal")

A word, often a [substantive](#substantive), derived from a [verb](#verb).

Contrast *[denominal](#denominal)*.

[diachrony](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/diachrony "diachrony")

A viewpoint of analysis of a language or phrase within a language which considers the [historical changes](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/historical_linguistics "historical linguistics") over time which have shaped its state at a given later time. The term is typically used to contrast with *[synchrony](#synchrony)*.

[diacritic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/diacritic "diacritic")

A diacritic – also diacritical mark, diacritical point, diacritical sign, or an accent – is a [glyph](#glyph) added to a letter, or basic glyph. Examples: [acute](#acute) ( ´ ), double acute ( ˝ ), [breve](#breve) ( ˘ ), inverted breve ( ̑ ), [cedilla](#cedilla) ( ¸ ), [diaeresis](#diaeresis) / [umlaut](#umlaut) ( ¨ ), [macron](#macron) ( ¯ ), [overring](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/overring "overring") ( ˚ ), [perispomene](#perispomene) ( ͂ ), etc. The main use of a diacritical mark is to change the sound-values of the letters or cadence of a word to which they are added. Examples are the diaereses in the borrowed French words *[naïve](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/na%C3%AFve#English "naïve")* and *[Noël](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/No%C3%ABl#English "Noël")*, which show that the vowel with the diaeresis mark is pronounced separately from the preceding vowel; the acute and [grave](#grave) accents, which can indicate that a final vowel is to be pronounced, as in *[saké](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sak%C3%A9#English "saké")* and poetic *breathèd*; and the cedilla under the ⟨c⟩ in the borrowed French word *[façade](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fa%C3%A7ade#English "façade")*, which shows it is pronounced /s/ rather than /k/.

[diaeresis](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/diaeresis "diaeresis")

A [diacritic](#diacritic) ( ¨ ), typically placed over a [vowel](#vowel) letter. Depending on the language, it usually either indicates that the vowel is sounded separately from the preceding vowel (as in French *[naïf](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/na%C3%AFf#French "naïf")* (“naive”) and the dated English spelling *[coöperate](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/co%C3%B6perate#English "coöperate")*), or that the vowel has a different (often specifically [fronted](#front_vowel)) pronunciation compared with the base vowel without the diaeresis. The latter usage is typical of [German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_language "w:German language") and certain other [Germanic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germanic_languages "w:Germanic languages"), as in *[über](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%C3%BCber#German "über")* (“over”) and *[König](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/K%C3%B6nig#German "König")* (“king”), and in this case the diacritic often goes by the alternative name of *[umlaut](#umlaut)*.

[dialectal](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dialectal "dialectal")

Of or relating to a [dialect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/dialect "w:dialect") (which is, for the purposes of [Wiktionary's labels](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Wiktionary:Context_labels "Wiktionary:Context labels"), synonymous with [variety](#variety)).

[diminutive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/diminutive "diminutive")

A word form expressing smallness, youth, endearment, unimportance, or contempt. Opposite of [intensive](#intensive).

[direct object](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/direct_object "direct object")

The [noun](#noun) or noun [phrase](#phrase) that a [verb](#verb) is directly acting upon. In some languages direct objects are marked with the [accusative case](#accusative_case).

→ [transitive verb](#transitive_verb), [indirect object](#indirect_object)

[disjunctive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/disjunctive "disjunctive") [pronoun](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pronoun "pronoun")

A [pronoun](#pronoun) not used in immediate conjunction with the [verb](#verb) of which it is the [subject](#subject) (e.g. Spanish *[mí](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/m%C3%AD#Spanish "mí")*).

[displaced](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/displace "displace")

Of an older word: having been replaced by a newer word.

[dissimilation](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dissimilation "dissimilation")

A phonological process whereby one of a pair of similar sounds within a word or phrase becomes dissimilar: for example, the shift l…l > r…l in the derivation of Middle French *[coronnel](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/coronnel#Middle_French "coronnel")* from Old Italian *[colonnello](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/colonnello#Italian "colonnello")*. It has an opposite effect to [assimilation](#assimilation).

[distal](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/distal "distal")

Indicates distance from the speaker.

Contrast *[proximal](#proximal)*.

[ditransitive verb](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ditransitive_verb "ditransitive verb")

(of a verb) taking two objects, such as *[give](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/give#English "give")* in “Give me the ball” (where *[me](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/me#English "me")* is an [indirect object](#indirect_object) and *[the](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/the#English "the") [ball](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ball#English "ball")* is a [direct object](#direct_object)). Compare [intransitive](#intransitive_verb), [transitive](#transitive_verb), and [ambitransitive](#ambitransitive_verb) verbs.

[doublet](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/doublet "doublet")

One of two (or more) words in a language that have the same [etymon](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/etymon "etymon") but in the modern language have different [phonological](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/phonological "phonological") forms. Doublets can come about e.g., as [loanwords](#loanword) from two different but related languages, as loanwords acquired from the same language at two different stages, as one loanword from a related language plus its native [cognate](#cognate), or as derivatives formed at two different stages in the history of a language. For example *[lever](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lever#Noun "lever")* and *[levator](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/levator#Noun "levator")* are doublets (more at [Category:English doublets](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Category:English_doublets "Category:English doublets")).

Compare [piecewise doublet](#piecewise_doublet).

[dual](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dual "dual"), [dual number](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dual_number "dual number")

A [grammatical number](#number) that indicates exactly two items or individuals. Usually contrasts with [singular](#singular_number) and [plural](#plural_number).

[dummy pronoun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/dummy_pronoun "w:dummy pronoun")

A [pronoun](#pronoun) that has no referent. For instance, *[it](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/it#English "it")* in *it is good to know that you are okay* is a dummy [subject](#subject). It is used in order to provide the verb *[is](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/is#English "is")* with a syntactic subject, because English does not allow a [null subject](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/null-subject_language "w:null-subject language").

[dvandva](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dvandva "dvandva")

Another term for a *[coordinative](#coordinative) compound*.

Contrast [bahuvrihi](#bahuvrihi), [tatpurusa](#tatpurusa) and [karmadharaya](#karmadharaya).

[dysphemism](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dysphemism "dysphemism")

A term carrying negative connotations or imagery to replace a (more) neutral original.

Contrast *[euphemism](#euphemism)*.

(See also *[degrees of comparison](#degrees_of_comparison)*.)

[endonym](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/endonym "endonym")

A name used by a group or category of people to refer to themselves or their language (contrast [exonym](#exonym)). Also called [autonym](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/autonym "autonym") or [selfname](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/selfname "selfname").

[endocentric](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/endocentric "endocentric") [compound](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/compound "compound")

A [nominal](#nominal) [compound](#compound) in which one part modifies the other, where the modified compound refers to the intended meaning of the compound as a whole. Examples in English are " [blackboard](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/blackboard "blackboard") " (a type of [board](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/board "board")), " [houseboat](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/houseboat "houseboat") " (a type of [boat](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/boat "boat")), " [rainbow](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rainbow "rainbow") " (a type of [bow](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bow "bow")) and " [footstool](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/footstool "footstool") " (a type of stool). Endocentric compounds are categorized into two subtypes, [descriptive](#descriptive) compounds (where the modifier also refers to the intended meaning of the compound as a whole, as in [blackboard](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/blackboard "blackboard") and [houseboat](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/houseboat "houseboat")) and [determinative](#determinative) compounds (where the modifier does not refer to the intended meaning of the compound as a whole, as in [rainbow](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rainbow "rainbow") and [footstool](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/footstool "footstool")). For example, a [houseboat](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/houseboat "houseboat") is a type of [house](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/house "house") as well as a type of [boat](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/boat "boat"), and a [blackboard](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/blackboard "blackboard") is (usually) [black](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/black "black"). However, a [rainbow](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rainbow "rainbow") is not a type of [rain](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rain "rain") (rather it is a [bow](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bow "bow") that is caused *by* the rain), and similarly a [footstool](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/footstool "footstool") is not a type of [foot](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/foot "foot") (it is a stool intended *for* the feet). Endocentric compounds are contrasted with [exocentric](#exocentric) and [coordinative](#coordinative) compounds.

[factitive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/factitive "factitive") verb

A transitive verb that is derived from an adjective or noun, and expresses the act of making someone/something have the properties (or have more of the properties) of the base adjective or noun. Examples in English include: *[shorten](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/shorten#English "shorten")* (make *[shorter](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/shorter#English "shorter")*), *[strengthen](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/strengthen#English "strengthen")* (make *[stronger](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/stronger#English "stronger")*) and *[enslave](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/enslave#English "enslave")* (make a *[slave](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/slave#English "slave")* of).

[false cognate](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/false_cognate "false cognate")

A word in a language that bears a [phonetic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/phonetic "phonetic") and [semantic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/semantic "semantic") resemblance to a word in another or the same language but is not [etymologically](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/etymology "etymology") related to it and thus not a true [cognate](#cognate). Examples include English *[day](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/day#English "day")* and Portuguese *[dia](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dia#Portuguese "dia")*; English *[dog](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dog#English "dog")* and Mbabaram *[dog](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dog#Mbabaram "dog")*; German *[Feuer](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Feuer#German "Feuer")* and French *[feu](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/feu#French "feu")* (both meaning “fire”); Malay *[dua](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dua#Malay "dua")* and Sanskrit *[द्व](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E0%A4%A6%E0%A5%8D%E0%A4%B5#Sanskrit "द्व")* (dva) (both meaning “two”).

Compare **[false friend](#false_friend)**.

[false friend](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/false_friend "false friend")

A word in a language that bears a phonetic resemblance to a word in another language, often because of a common etymology, but has a different meaning. [fossilisation](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fossilisation "fossilisation"), [fossilization](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fossilization "fossilization")

The process by which a word, [morpheme](#morpheme) or [feature](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/feature "feature") of a language ceases to have independent function but is retained as a fixed component of something else within the language, becoming a " [fossil](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fossil "fossil") ". An example of a fossil element in English is the plural *[\-en](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-en#English:_plural_noun "-en")* in some words such as *[children](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/children#English "children")*, *[brethren](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/brethren#English "brethren")* and *[oxen](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/oxen#English "oxen")*; this suffix formerly applied to a much broader class of nouns. For instances of fossilization that appear as standalone [words](#words), see ***[fossil word](#fossil_word)*** above. Compare *un- [productive](#productive)*. [fricative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fricative "fricative")

A [consonant](#consonant) sound produced by air flowing through a constriction in the mouth, and typically producing a sibilant, hissing, buzzing or otherwise "rough" quality. In English, there are fricatives that are [voiceless](#voiceless) /f/, /s/, /ʃ/, /θ/ (as in the final sounds of *buff*, *bus*, *bash* and *bath*), and there are corresponding [voiced](#voiced) sounds /v/, /z/, /ʒ/, /ð/ (as in the final sounds of *above*, *buzz*, *beige* and *bathe*). Fricatives are distinguished from [plosives](#plosive), in which the air is blocked completely for a short period of time, and [approximants](#approximant), in which the air is not constricted enough to cause the characteristic rough sound of a fricative.

[front vowel](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/front_vowel "front vowel")

A [vowel](#vowel) produced in the front of the mouth. In English, the front vowels are /æ/, /ɛ/, /eɪ/, /ɪ/, /i/ (as in the vowels of *bat*, *bet*, *bait*, *bit*, and *beat* respectively).

[future tense](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/future_tense "future tense")

[genericized trademark](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/genericized_trademark "genericized trademark")

A successful brand name or trademark that has come to refer to the generic class of objects rather than the specific brand type. The inclusion of such a term in a dictionary reflects a [descriptive](#description) fact about how speakers use the language, but it does not control or inhibit a trademark owner's legal rights to defend the trademark's protected status, which is determined only through legal and judicial means. ([See usage notes](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/genericized_trademark#Usage_notes "genericized trademark").)

[genitive case](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/genitive_case "genitive case")

A [case](#case) that expresses possession or relation, equivalent to the English *[of](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/of#English "of")* or *[\-'s](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-%27s#English "-'s")*.

[gerund](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gerund "gerund")

Any of various non-finite [verb](#verb) forms in various languages. In English, a " [gerund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gerund#Gerunds_in_English "w:Gerund") " refers to a verb in its *[\-ing](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-ing#English "-ing")* form when used in a way that resembles the use of a [noun](#noun). Despite showing noun-like behavior in the context of the surrounding sentence, gerunds show verbal behavior in the context of their own internal clause: they can take direct objects or be modified by adverbs. In this way, gerunds are distinguished from deverbal nouns ending in *\-ing,* which occur in noun phrases that can take determiners or be modified by adjectives. For example, "manufacturing" is a gerund in the following sentence: "Efficiently manufacturing this device is difficult." It is a [verbal noun](#verbal_noun) (not a gerund) in this sentence: "The efficient manufacturing of this device is difficult." In other languages, gerund can refer to a form that often functions as an [adverb](#adverb) to form [adverbial](#adverbial) phrases or the continuous tense.

[ghost word](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ghost_word "ghost word")

A [fictitious](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fictitious "fictitious") or [erroneous](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/erroneous "erroneous") word, originally meaningless (not used in practice), that has been published in a dictionary or similarly authoritative reference work or otherwise listed as genuine, generally as the result of misinterpretation, misreading, or typographical error, but occasionally intentionally as a [fictitious entry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/fictitious_entry "w:fictitious entry").

[glide](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/glide "glide")

An [approximant](#approximant) [consonant](#consonant), such as /j/ or /w/, that is the consonantal equivalent of a vowel (respectively, /i/ and /u/ in the cases just given) and often alternates with that vowel in different phonological contexts. Also termed a *semivowel*.

[glottal stop](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/glottal_stop "glottal stop")

A [stop](#stop) [consonant](#consonant) (i.e. [plosive](#plosive)), denoted /ʔ/, produced by blocking the flow of air in the throat without producing any [articulation](#articulation) further forward in the mouth. Glottal stops are not [phonemes](#phoneme) in English but occur frequently in interjections such as [uh-oh](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/uh-oh "uh-oh") (pronounced \[ˈʔʌˈʔoʊ\] or similar) and at the beginning words starting with a vowel, particularly either after a pause or (in [rhotic accents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/rhotic_accent "w:rhotic accent") such as [American English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_English "w:American English")) after a word ending in a vowel. Glottal stops occur as phonemes in many languages, such as [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic_language "w:Arabic language") and [Hawaiian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawaiian_language "w:Hawaiian language").

[government](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/government "government")

A grammatical requirement that a word or sense imposes on its dependent words. With *case government*, the dependent word must be inflected in a particular [grammatical case](#case). For example, the Latin preposition *[ex](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ex#Latin "ex")* (“out of”) governs the ablative (requires that the complement is inflected in the ablative case). Also called *rection*.

[haplology](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/haplology "haplology")

The [elision](#elision) or [deletion](#deletion) of a part of a word (a sequence of [phonemes](#phoneme), or a series of letters) that is repeated (either exactly or with slight change). An example of haplological pronunciation is the UK English pronunciation of *[library](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/library#English "library")* as if spelled *libry*, where the sequence of [phonemes](#phoneme) /ɹəɹ/ is shortened to /ɹ/. An example of haplology relating to spelling is *[symbology](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/symbology#English "symbology")*, from *[symbol](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/symbol#English "symbol")* + *[\-ology](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-ology#English "-ology")*, where the sequence *olol* is shortened to *ol*.

Contrast with [reduplication](#reduplication), the repetition of a part of a word.

[hedge](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hedge "hedge")

In [pragmatics](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pragmatics "pragmatics"), a term (word, phrase, or clause) used to lessen the force of an utterance: for instance, to avoid giving insult or bragging about one's knowledge.

[hiatus](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hiatus "hiatus")

A syllable break between two vowels, without an intervening consonant. In some languages (e.g. many [Polynesian languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynesian_languages "w:Polynesian languages")), hiatuses within a word are very common, but many languages (e.g. many [Slavic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavic_languages "w:Slavic languages")) discourage or outright prohibit them. Hiatuses across a word boundary are often avoided either by insertion of a consonant, such as a [glottal stop](#glottal_stop) (/ʔ/), a [glide](#glide) (e.g. /j/ or /w/), or less commonly some other consonant (e.g. in [non-rhotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/non-rhotic "w:non-rhotic") varieties of English the so-called [intrusive R](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/intrusive_R "w:intrusive R"), as in *[law-r-and order](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/law_and_order#English "law and order")* or *[Maya-r-Angelou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_Angelou "w:Maya Angelou")*); or by [elision](#elision) of one of the two vowels, conversion of a vowel into a [glide](#glide), or [assimilation](#assimilation) of the two vowels into a single [long vowel](#long_vowel), as in the well-known [sandhi](#sandhi) phenomena of [Sanskit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanskit "w:Sanskit").

[higher](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/higher "higher") [register](#register)

Belonging to the higher linguistic [register](#register), meaning that it might be used when the speaker wishes to sound refined or educated, in [formal](#formal) situations, or when writing. Such terms are usually less common or known and are not used in everyday speech. See also *[literary](#literary)* which is a sub-category.

[historical](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/historical "historical")

Describing an *object* or *concept* which is no longer extant or current; for example, *[Czechoslovakia](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Czechoslovakia#English "Czechoslovakia")*, *[stomacher](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/stomacher#English "stomacher")*, or *[phlogiston](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/phlogiston#English "phlogiston")*. Distinguish: a *historical* term is still in use but refers to a thing no longer in current use; an *[obsolete](#obsolete)* term is no longer in use, while the thing it once referred to may or may not exist. Whereas an [archaic](#archaic) term names a still-extant thing or non-outdated concept, a historical term names a former thing or outdated concept.

[holonym](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/holonym "holonym")

A term describing something that is formed by other smaller, somehow combined or related things. For example, *[tree](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tree#English "tree")* is a holonym of *[leaf](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/leaf#English "leaf")*; *[body](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/body#English "body")* is a holonym of *[arm](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/arm#English "arm")*; *[Canada](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Canada#English "Canada")* is a holonym of *[British Columbia](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/British_Columbia#English "British Columbia")*, etc. The opposite of holonym, which describes things that are *part* of a whole, is called *[meronym](#meronym)*.

[homograph](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/homograph "homograph")

A word that is spelled the same as another word, usually having a different [etymology](#etymology).

[homonym](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/homonym "homonym")

A word which is spelled and pronounced the same as another word, but differs in meaning.

[homophone](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/homophone "homophone")

A word which is pronounced the same as another word but differs in spelling, meaning and/or origin.

[hortative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hortative "hortative")

A verbal construction (in some languages expressed with a dedicated grammatical mood) whereby a speaker encourages an action on the part of his own group; the hortative is for the first person plural what the [imperative](#imperative) is to the second person.

[hot](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hot "hot") word

A newly coined term, or newly adopted sense of an existing term, that has become very popular in a short time. It is kept provisionally as it is likely to remain in usage, even though it would otherwise fail the "spanning at least one year" requirement of Wiktionary's [criteria for inclusion](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Wiktionary:Criteria_for_inclusion#Spanning_at_least_a_year "Wiktionary:Criteria for inclusion").

[humorous](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/humorous "humorous")

A word, term, or phrase that is uttered [in jest](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/in_jest "in jest") ([jocularly](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/jocular "jocular")) or that originated in that way.

[hyperbole](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hyperbole "hyperbole")

Deliberate or unintentional overstatement, particularly if extreme.

[hypercorrect](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hypercorrect "hypercorrect")

Incorrect because of the misapplication of a standard rule; for example, *[octopi](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/octopi#English "octopi")* used as the plural form of *[octopus](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/octopus#English "octopus")* is hypercorrect because *[\-us](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-us#Latin "-us")* → *[\-i](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-i#Latin "-i")* is the rule for forming plurals of [originally-masculine nouns of the Latin second declension](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Latin_second_declension#Examples "Appendix:Latin second declension"), whereas *octopus* actually derives from Ancient Greek and has the plural form *[octopodes](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/octopodes#English "octopodes")* consistent with its etymology.

[hyperforeign](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hyperforeign "hyperforeign")

Incorrectly applying foreign reading rules, such as in pronouncing the "j" in [Taj Mahal](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Taj_Mahal "Taj Mahal") with \[ʒ\] rather than \[dʒ\], pronouncing [Beijing](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Beijing "Beijing") with \[ʒ\] rather than \[dʒ\], or dropping the \[t\] in [claret](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/claret "claret").

[hypernym](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hypernym "hypernym") or [hyperonym](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hyperonym "hyperonym")

A term indicating a [category](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/category "category") another term is part of, informally called “blanket” or “umbrella” term. For example, *[animal](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/animal#English "animal")* is a hypernym of *[bird](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bird#English "bird")*, which is in turn a hypernym of *[eagle](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/eagle#English "eagle")*. The opposite of hypernym, which indicates terms pertaining to a category, is *[hyponym](#hyponym)*.

[hyperthesis](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hyperthesis "hyperthesis")

A form of [metathesis](#metathesis) in which non-contiguous sounds are switched.

[hyphenation](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hyphenation "hyphenation")

The splitting of a word across a line boundary, with a hyphen at the end of the first part. For example, the hyphenation of *[hyphenation](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hyphenation#English "hyphenation")* is given as "hy‧phen‧ation" meaning that it is split across a line break as *hy-phenation* or as *hyphen-ation*.

[hypocorism](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hypocorism "hypocorism")

A term of endearment, often a [diminutive](#diminutive); a pet name; something that is smaller in a tender or affectionate sense.

[hyponym](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hyponym "hyponym")

A more specific term within a category described by another term, indicating a “type-of” relationship. For instance, *[alternative rock](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/alternative_rock#English "alternative rock")* is a hyponym of *[rock](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rock#English "rock")*, which in turn is a hyponym of *[music](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/music#English "music")*. The opposite of hyponym, which describes larger categories, is [hypernym](#hypernym).

## I

[ʾiḍāfa](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CA%BEi%E1%B8%8D%C4%81fa "ʾiḍāfa") (*[إِضَافَة](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D8%A5%D8%B6%D8%A7%D9%81%D8%A9#Arabic "إضافة")* (ʔiḍāfa))

The [possessive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/possessive "possessive") or [genitive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/genitive "genitive") construction in Arabic. See also [ezafe](#ezafe) and [izafet](#izafet).

[ideophone](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ideophone "ideophone")

A word that evokes an idea in sound, often a vivid impression of certain sensations or sensory perceptions, e.g. sound, movement, color, shape, or action. They may be more common in East Asian languages. In Chinese lexicography, such sense is usually described as *…… [的](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%9A%84#Chinese "的") [樣子](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%A8%A3%E5%AD%90#Chinese "樣子")* / *…… [的](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%9A%84#Chinese "的") [样子](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%A0%B7%E5%AD%90#Chinese "样子")* or *…… [貌](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%B2%8C#Chinese "貌")*, i.e. “the appearance of...”. The attributive form is [ideophonic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ideophonic#English "ideophonic").

[idiom](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/idiom "idiom")

A phrase whose meaning is unapparent or unobvious from the individual words that make it up, such as *[beat around the bush](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/beat_around_the_bush#English "beat around the bush")* (“avoid an uncomfortable topic”), *[come a cropper](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/come_a_cropper#English "come a cropper")* (“suffer misfortune”), or *[pay through the nose](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pay_through_the_nose#English "pay through the nose")* (“pay an unusually large amount”). Idioms are often, but not always *[set phrases](#set_phrase)*, and are usually distinct from *[proverbs](#proverb)*.

[idiomatic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/idiomatic "idiomatic")

Pertaining or conforming to the mode of expression characteristic of a language. Idioms, [collocations](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/collocation "collocation"), and [modal verbs](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/modal_verb "modal verb") are examples of idiomatic language.

[imitative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/imitative "imitative")

[Imitating](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/imitate "imitate") or [representing](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/represent "represent") the [sound](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sound#Noun "sound") of something. (The main class of examples is the [onomatopoeic](#onomatopoeic) ones.)

[imparisyllabic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/imparisyllabic "imparisyllabic")

A word with one or more inflections with more syllables than the lemma form. For example: the Greek *[μπακάλης](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BC%CF%80%CE%B1%CE%BA%CE%AC%CE%BB%CE%B7%CF%82#Greek "μπακάλης")* (bakális, “grocer”) and *[μπακάληδες](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BC%CF%80%CE%B1%CE%BA%CE%AC%CE%BB%CE%B7%CE%B4%CE%B5%CF%82#Greek "μπακάληδες")* (bakálides, “grocers”) and the Latin *[mens](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mens#Latin "mens")* (“mind”) and *[mentis](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mentis#Latin "mentis")* (“minds”) (compare [parisyllabic](#parisyllabic)).

[imperative mood](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/imperative_mood "imperative mood")

The [mood](#mood) of a [verb](#verb) expressing an order or command. An English example is the command *go!* Commands can also be, and often are, [polite](#polite), such as *please go downstairs*. In many languages imperative [inflections](#inflection) are the same as or similar to [subjunctive](#subjunctive) ones, reflecting the shared trait of non- [indicative](#indicative) nature: yet- [unrealized](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/unrealized "unrealized") ideas.

[imperfect](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/imperfect "imperfect")

The [imperfective past](#imperfective_past) tense of a verb, indicating that the action described happened repeatedly, habitually or continuously.

[imperfective](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/imperfective "imperfective")

An [aspect](#aspect) of the [verb](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verb "verb") which denotes an action or condition that does not have a fixed temporal boundary, but is [habitual](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/habitual "habitual"), [unfinished](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/unfinished "unfinished"), [continuous](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/continuous "continuous"), [repetitive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/repetitive "repetitive") or [in progress](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/in_progress "in progress"). Common in [Slavic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavic_languages "w:Slavic languages") such as Russian.

Contrast *[perfective](#perfective).*

imperfective past

A verb form of [imperfective](#imperfective) [aspect](#aspect) and [past](#past) [tense](#tense), which is used to describe an action or event which was happening habitually, continuously or repeatedly in the past, as in “Tom was painting the fence” or “Tom used to paint the fence.”

[impersonal](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/impersonal "impersonal")

A lack of [grammatical person](#person) altogether, or an indefinite/undefined person. An example is the English pronoun *[one](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/one#English "one")*. See also [impersonal verbs](#impersonal_verb).

[impersonal](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/impersonal "impersonal") verb

A verb that cannot take a [subject](#subject), or takes a third-person neuter subject pronoun (e.g. *[it](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/it#English "it")*) without an [antecedent](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/antecedent "antecedent"). The term *[weather verb](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/weather_verb#English "weather verb")* is also sometimes used in some texts, since such verbs of weather (e.g. *[rain](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rain#English "rain")*) are impersonal in many languages. Many verbs that are personal and [active](#active) in English are expressed in other languages using impersonal constructions. An example is the English sentence "I must do it", expressed in French using the impersonal verb *[falloir](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/falloir#French "falloir")* (“to be [necessary](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/necessary "necessary") ”), as in *[il](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/il#French "il") [faut](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/falloir#French "falloir") [que](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/que#French "que") [je](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/je#French "je") [le](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/il#French "il") [fasse](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/faire#French "faire")* (literally “ **It is necessary** that I do it”).

[inanimate](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/inanimate "inanimate")

Having a [referent](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/referent "referent") that does not include a human or animal. Many languages (such as the [Slavic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavic_languages "w:Slavic languages")) classify nouns based on animacy, using different [inflections](#inflection) or words with animate and inanimate nouns. For verbs, this indicates that they are usually applied only to inanimate objects or concepts, and rarely used in the first or second persons.

[inchoative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/inchoative "inchoative")

An [aspect](#aspect) of the [verb](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verb "verb") which denotes that a state is about to be entered or is in the process of being entered. Such verbs are often translated in English as *become* or *turn* followed by an adjective. Some languages have a specific suffix for forming inchoative verbs, such as *[\-scō](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-sco#Latin "-sco")* in Latin; compare *[rubeō](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rubeo#Latin "rubeo")* (“to be red”) vs. *[rubēscō](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rubesco#Latin "rubesco")* (“to turn red, to redden, to blush”).

[incomparable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/incomparable "incomparable")

(of adjectives and adverbs) unable to be compared, or lacking a [comparative](#comparative) and [superlative](#superlative) function. *See* **[comparable](#comparable)**. Examples of adjectives that are not comparable: *annual*, *first*, *extra*, *satin*, *six-figure*.

[indeclinable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/indeclinable "indeclinable"), [undeclinable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/undeclinable "undeclinable"), [invariable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/invariable "invariable") or [invariant](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/invariant "invariant")

In languages with [inflection](#inflection), lacking distinct inflected forms when they would be expected to exist. Indeclinable words have the same form in all cases. For example, the English noun *[sheep](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sheep#English "sheep")* is invariable because its plural is also *[sheep](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sheep#English "sheep")*. [Acronyms](#acronym) and [loanwords](#loanword) are often indeclinable in many languages. Poorly-attested words from ancient languages are sometimes denoted "indeclinable" when they can't be assigned to a declension class with certainty.

[indefinite](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/indefinite "indefinite")

Refers to forms of words that present something as not yet identified or not immediately identifiable; in English, this is the basic meaning of the [article](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/article "article") *[a](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/a#English "a")*; in some languages, this is a nominal or adjectival [inflection](#inflection). In the Germanic languages, adjectives inflected as indefinite are referred to as "strong". In Hungarian and also in Mansi, the indefinite conjugation indicates [no object](#intransitive_verb) or [indefinite objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungarian_verbs#Definite_and_indefinite_conjugations "w:Hungarian verbs"), including *me, us,* and the informal *you.*

[independent clause](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/independent_clause "independent clause")

A [clause](#clause) that can stand on its own as a complete [sentence](#sentence), as opposed to a [dependent clause](#dependent_clause).

[indeterminate verb](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/indeterminate_verb "indeterminate verb")

In the [Slavic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavic_languages "w:Slavic languages"), another term for *[multidirectional verb](#multidirectional_verb)*.

[indicative mood](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/indicative_mood "indicative mood")

The [mood](#mood) of a [verb](#verb) used in ordinary factual or objective statements.

[indirect object](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/indirect_object "indirect object")

A grammatical role of a [ditransitive verb](#ditransitive_verb) that usually manifests as a recipient or goal. In some languages indirect objects are marked with the [dative case](#dative_case).

→ [direct object](#direct_object)

[infix](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/infix "infix")

A [morpheme](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/morpheme "morpheme") or [affix](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/affix "affix") inserted inside a word.

[infinitive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/infinitive "infinitive")

A non- [finite](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/finite "finite") verb form considered [neutral](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/neutral "neutral") with respect to [inflection](#inflection); depending on language variously found used with [auxiliary verbs](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/auxiliary_verb "auxiliary verb"), in [subordinate clauses](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/subordinate_clause "subordinate clause"), or acting as a [gerund](#gerund), and often as the [dictionary form](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dictionary_form "dictionary form"). In English, the infinitive is formed with the word *[to](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/to#English "to")*, e.g. *to [read](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/read#English "read")*.

[inflection](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/inflection "inflection")

The change in form of a word to represent various grammatical categories, such as [tense](#tense) (e.g. past tense, present tense, future tense) or [number](#number) (e.g. singular, plural). For example, the verb *[run](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/run#English "run")* may be inflected to produce *[runs](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/runs#English "runs")*, *[ran](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ran#English "ran")*, and *[running](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/running#English "running")*. In highly inflected languages, such as Latin, there will be many more forms. Two major types of inflection are [conjugation](#conjugation) (inflection of [verbs](#verb)) and [declension](#declension) (inflection of [nouns](#noun), [adjectives](#adjective), and [pronouns](#pronoun)).

[informal](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/informal "informal")

Denotes spoken or written words that are used primarily in a [familiar](#familiar) or casual context. Do not confuse with *[slang](#slang)* or *[nonstandard](#nonstandard)*. See also *[colloquial](#colloquial)*.

[inherited](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/inherit "inherit")

Denotes words which etymologically are not [borrowings](#loanword) but derive through regular or sporadic [sound change](#sound_change), without additional affixation, from a corresponding term in the language that is its direct historical ancestor. For example, English *[one](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/one#English "one")* is *inherited* from Proto-Germanic *[\*ainaz](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Germanic/ainaz "Reconstruction:Proto-Germanic/ainaz")*.

[initialism](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/initialism "initialism")

An [abbreviation](#abbreviation) that is formed from the initial letters of a sequence of words. Initialisms that are pronounced as words, such as *[UNICEF](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/UNICEF#English "UNICEF")*, are usually called *[acronyms](#acronym)*, so the term *initialism* is generally only used for those that are pronounced letter by letter, such as *[U.S.](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/U.S.#English "U.S.")*

initial vowel

*See **[augment](#augment)**.*

inline reference

A reference whose point of application is indicated in the text.

[instrumental case](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/instrumental_case "instrumental case"), ins.

A [case](#case) used to express means or agency—and is generally indicated in English by "by" or "with" with the objective.

[intensifier](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/intensifier "intensifier")

A [word](#word) or [particle](#particle) that heightens the intensity of meaning of a term.

[intensive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/intensive "intensive")

A word form expressing great size, power or emotion. Opposite of [diminutive](#diminutive).

[interjection](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/interjection "interjection")

An expression of emotion ("ouch!", "wow!") or any of several kinds of expression that functions as a replacement of a sentence ([prosentence](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/prosentence "prosentence")) or that are not syntactically connected to a sentence, including curses ("damn!"), greetings ("hey", "bye"), response particles ("okay", "oh!", "m-hm", "huh?"), and hesitation markers ("uh", "er", "um"), and perhaps profanities, discourse markers and fillers.

[interfix](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/interfix "interfix")

An [empty morph](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/empty_morph "empty morph") (or meaningless morph) inserted between two [morphemes](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/morpheme "morpheme") in the process of word formation, such as *[\-i-](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-i-#English "-i-")* and *[\-o-](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-o-#English "-o-")* in English.

[internationalism](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/internationalism "internationalism")

A loanword that occurs in several languages with the same or similar meaning and etymology, often due to the occurrence of several simultaneous borrowings and/or a chain of successive borrowings happening in quick succession. This commonly results in the exact etymological lineage of a term being difficult or impossible to trace for a given language. For example *bus, doctor, hotel, internet, taxi,* or *television.* [International scientific vocabulary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_scientific_vocabulary "w:International scientific vocabulary") is a large subclass of internationalisms.

[interrogative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/interrogative "interrogative")

A word used to ask questions.

[intransitive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/intransitive "intransitive")

Of a [verb](#verb): not taking a [direct object](#direct_object); not [transitive](#transitive). For example, the verb *[listen](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/listen#English "listen")* does not usually take a direct object; it is grammatically incorrect to say "I listened the concert" (instead of the correct "I listened to the concert" with the [indirect object](#indirect_object) "to the concert").

Of an adposition (such as a [preposition](#preposition)), or of an [adverb](#adverb): not having a [nominal](#nominal) complement. [ʾiʿrāb](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CA%BEi%CA%BFr%C4%81b "ʾiʿrāb")

Also called [desinential inflection](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/desinential_inflection "desinential inflection"); in Arabic: *[إِعْرَاب](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D8%A5%D8%B9%D8%B1%D8%A7%D8%A8#Arabic "إعراب")* (ʔiʕrāb). A number of [inflectional](#inflection) endings, applied to Arabic nouns, adjectives, and verbs, which—with minor exceptions—do not appear in writing, and moreover are not pronounced in [pausa](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pausa "pausa"), i.e. at the end of a sentence or before a pause. Nevertheless, these endings are a regular and required element of Qur'ānic and Classical Arabic. In Modern Standard Arabic, however, they are rather often avoided due to dialectal influence. In nouns and adjectives, the ʾiʿrāb primarily has the function of marking the cases ([nominative](#nominative), [genitive](#genitive), [accusative](#accusative)), while in verbs it marks the moods ([indicative](#indicative), [subjunctive](#subjunctive), [jussive](#jussive)). All of these are only occasionally distinguishable without application of the ʾiʿrāb.

[irregular](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/irregular "irregular")

Not following the usual rules of [inflection](#inflection); for example, the plural of English *[man](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/man#English "man")* is *[men](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/men#English "men")*, which is irregular; the regularly formed plural would have been \* *mans*.

[izafet](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/izafet "izafet")

The [possessive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/possessive "possessive") or [genitive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/genitive "genitive") construction in [Turkic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkic_languages "w:Turkic languages") such as [Azerbaijani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azerbaijani "w:Azerbaijani"). The term, and sometimes the grammatical structure, is borrowed from Persian *[اِضافِه](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D8%A7%D8%B6%D8%A7%D9%81%D9%87#Persian "اضافه")* (ezâfe) (see [ezafe](#ezafe)), which comes in turn from Arabic *[إِضَافَة](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D8%A5%D8%B6%D8%A7%D9%81%D8%A9#Arabic "إضافة")* (ʔiḍāfa) (see [ʾiḍāfa](#ʾiḍāfa)). Native *izafet* constructions in Turkic languages are right-headed (the first term modifies the second), but some constructions (especially in elevated or older style, such as [Classical Azerbaijani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Azerbaijani "w:Classical Azerbaijani") and [Ottoman Turkish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Turkish "w:Ottoman Turkish")) are left-headed in imitation of Persian grammar. There are three native *izafet* constructions: (a) type I, where both nouns are zero-marked; (b) type II, where the first noun is zero-marked and the second receives a possessive suffix; and (c) type III, where the first noun is in the genitive case and the second receives a possessive suffix.

## L

[learned borrowing](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/learned_borrowing "learned borrowing")

A [loanword](#loanword) that was borrowed directly on purpose, instead of through normal [language contact](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/language_contact "language contact"), from another language, especially [classical languages](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/classical_language "classical language") such as Latin, Ancient Greek or Sanskrit, and which has not undergone significant reshaping due to [sound change](#sound_change) or analogy with inherited terms. Such borrowings are thus often [unadapted](#unadapted_borrowing). Compare with [semi-learned borrowings](#semi-learned_borrowing), which have been significantly reshaped ([adapted](#adapted_borrowing)), and [inherited](#inherited) terms, which have undergone all the normal sound changes of a language. A theme of Latin's influence on modern European languages is that in many cases the same Latin word has been borrowed into any given modern language multiple times in different eras, and the morphologic and semantic facts about each descendant word differ in predictable ways (by eras); a concise summary for the case of English (for example) is offered in some introductory textbooks, such as Burriss and Casson.[^1] A typical example of this process is that the Portuguese term *[artículo](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/art%C3%ADculo#Portuguese "artículo")* (“ [articulus](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/articulus "articulus") ”) is a learned borrowing from Latin *[articulus](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/articulus#Latin "articulus")* (“joint, limb, division”); the term *[artigo](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/artigo#Portuguese "artigo")* (“article”) is a semi-learned borrowing from the same term, which was borrowed early enough to undergo later sound changes that [lenited](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lenite "lenite") *c* into *g* and deleted *l* between vowels; and the term *[artelho](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/artelho#Portuguese "artelho")* (“toe”) is inherited from the same Latin term.

[lemma](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lemma "lemma")

The headword or citation form of an [inflected](#inflection) word, i.e. the form under which a word is found in a dictionary. For example, in English, nouns are usually listed under their singular form (*apple*, rather than *apples*), and verbs under their infinitive form (*open*, rather than *opens*, *opened* or *opening*). Which form is used as the lemma varies from language to language. For verbs, for example, French, German, Spanish and many other European languages use the [infinitive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/infinitive "infinitive"), but Latin, Greek and Bulgarian use the [first-person](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/first-person "first-person") [singular](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/singular "singular") [present](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/present "present") [indicative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/indicative "indicative"), while Macedonian uses the [third-person](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/third-person "third-person") [singular](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/singular "singular") [present](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/present "present") [indicative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/indicative "indicative"), and Arabic and Hebrew use the [masculine](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/masculine "masculine") [third-person](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/third-person "third-person") [singular](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/singular "singular") [past](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/past "past") [indicative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/indicative "indicative"). See also [Wiktionary:Lemmas](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Wiktionary:Lemmas "Wiktionary:Lemmas").

[letter case](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/letter_case "letter case")

[loanword](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/loanword "loanword") (also [loan](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/loan "loan") or [borrowing](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/borrowing "borrowing"))

A word that was [adopted](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/adopt#Verb "adopt") ([borrowed](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/borrow#Verb "borrow")) from another language, rather than formed within the language or inherited from a more ancient form of the same language. Such a word may be [adapted](#adapted_borrowing) or [unadapted](#unadapted_borrowing). Loanwords may still be recognisably foreign (having non-native spelling or unusual pronunciation) ([unadapted](#unadapted_borrowing)), or they may have become completely assimilated into the language (no longer perceived as foreign). For example, in English, *[schadenfreude](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/schadenfreude#English "schadenfreude")* is still recognisably German, whereas *[cellar](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cellar#English "cellar")* is fully assimilated and no longer recognisably Latin (from *[cellārium](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cellarium#Latin "cellarium")*). [merger](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/merger "merger")

1. In etymology, a [confluence](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/confluence "confluence") of two etymologically separate sources into eventually one and the same term, motivated by a coincidental [phono-semantic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/phono-semantic "phono-semantic") similarity between them. For example, Old English *[nǣmel](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/n%C3%A6mel#Old_English "næmel")* (“receptive, quick to grasp”) and *[numol](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/numol#Old_English "numol")* (“able to take, capable of holding”) apparently merged into English *[nimble](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nimble#English "nimble")*, which absorbed the [semantics](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/semantics "semantics") of both words. An extreme type of [conflation](#conflation). Contrast *[blend](#blend)*.
2. In phonology, a [sound change](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sound_change "sound change") that results in two [phonemes](#phoneme) (typically referring to [vowels](#vowel)) becoming one phoneme as a result of increasingly similar pronunciation over time. This need not affect an entire language, but will (at least initially) apply to a particular [variety](#variety) of the language, such as one regional “ [accent](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/accent "accent") ” or [dialect](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dialect "dialect"). Example: the recent [cot-caught merger](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cot-caught_merger "cot-caught merger") affecting some English speakers.

[meronym](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/meronym "meronym")

[metathesis](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/metathesis "metathesis")

A [sound change](#sound_change) in which two sounds or groups of sounds exchange position in a word. A form showing metathesis is described as *[metathetic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/metathetic#English "metathetic")*. A word that names an object from a single characteristic of it or of a closely related object, e.g. *[crown](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/crown#English "crown")* for the sovereign in a monarchy. Compare [synecdoche](#synecdoche), which overlaps substantially in [denotation](#denotation).

[middle voice](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/middle_voice "middle voice")

The [voice](#voice) verb form in which the [subject](#subject) of a verb performs some action upon itself, as in the English sentence *The plane landed* (contrast [active voice](#active_voice) *The pilot landed the plane* and [passive voice](#passive_voice) *The plane was landed (by the pilot)*). Often used interchangeably with the [mediopassive voice](#mediopassive_voice). In some languages, such as [Sanskrit](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Sanskrit "Sanskrit"), [Ancient Greek](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek"), [Icelandic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Icelandic "Icelandic") and [Albanian](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Albanian "Albanian"), the middle voice is distinguished [morphologically](#morphology) from the active voice. [Ancient Greek](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek"), in particular, has a three-way morphological distinction between active, middle and passive voices in the [aorist](#aorist) and [future](#future) tenses.

[minced oath](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/minced_oath "minced oath")

A euphemism based on a profanity that has been altered to reduce or remove the objectionable characteristics of the original expression. Examples in English include *[heck](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/heck#English "heck")* instead of *[hell](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hell#English "hell")* and *[dang](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dang#English "dang")* instead of *[damn](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/damn#English "damn")*.

[mixed](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mixed "mixed")

A type of declension in German that uses endings that are a combination of [strong](#strong_declension) and [weak](#weak_declension) endings. In adjectives, the mixed declension is used when accompanied by an indefinite article, or more generally by an *[ein](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ein#German "ein")* -word (a determiner that has a null ending in the nominative masculine and neuter singular, similar to the indefinite article *[ein](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ein#German "ein")*). The mixed declension uses strong endings precisely in those inflections where the indefinite article has a null ending, and weak endings otherwise. By analogy, nouns declined according to the mixed declension look like strong nouns in the singular (with genitive in *\-s* and/or *\-es*) but weak nouns in the plural (with plural in *\-n* and/or *\-en*). Most mixed nouns were once weak nouns that have transitioned to strong nouns in the singular but maintain the original weak plural ending. See [Category:German mixed nouns](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Category:German_mixed_nouns "Category:German mixed nouns") for a list of German mixed nouns.

[monolectic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/monolectic "monolectic")

Used of a grammatical form accomplished with one word (compare [polylectic](#polylectic) and [periphrastic](#periphrastic)).

[monosyllable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/monosyllable "monosyllable")

A word consisting of only one [syllable](#syllable).

[mood](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/grammatical_mood "grammatical mood")

One of the forms of a [verb](#verb), used to indicate the speaker's attitude toward what they are saying (e.g. a statement of fact, of desire, of command, etc.). Examples include [indicative](#indicative_mood), [subjunctive](#subjunctive_mood), [imperative](#imperative_mood), [conditional](#conditional_mood).

[morpheme](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/morpheme "morpheme")

The [smallest](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/small#Adjective "small") [linguistic unit](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/linguistic_unit "linguistic unit") within a [word](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/word#Noun "word") that can [carry](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/carry#Verb "carry") a [meaning](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/meaning#Noun "meaning").

[morphological](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/morphological "morphological")

In [linguistics](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/linguistics "linguistics"), the study of words and how they are formed from component parts ([morphemes](#morpheme)). It also refers to the structure of the words themselves. Some languages lean more heavily on morphology to carry grammatical distinctions, while others rely more on [syntax](#syntax). Compare English *I make*, *I will make*, *it is made* (using syntax to encode [person](#person), [tense](#tense) and [voice](#voice) distinctions) vs. Latin *[faciō](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/facio#Latin "facio")*, *[facem](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/facem#Latin "facem")*, *[facitur](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/facitur#Latin "facitur")* (using morphology).

[multidirectional](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/multidirectional "multidirectional") [verb](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verb "verb")

In the [Slavic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavic_languages "w:Slavic languages"), a verb of motion whose motion is multidirectional (as opposed to unidirectional) or indirect, or whose action is repeated or in a series ([iterative](#iterative)). Also called an [abstract verb](#abstract_verb) or [indeterminate verb](#indeterminate_verb). The opposite type of verb, which expresses a single, completed action, is termed a [unidirectional verb](#unidirectional_verb) (also [concrete verb](#concrete_verb) or [determinate verb](#determinate_verb)). Motion verbs in the [Slavic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavic_languages "w:Slavic languages") come in multidirectional/unidirectional lexical pairs, e.g. Russian *[ходи́ть](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D1%85%D0%BE%D0%B4%D0%B8%D1%82%D1%8C#Russian "ходить")* (xodítʹ, “to [go](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/go "go") ”, multidirectional) vs. *[идти́](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%B8%D0%B4%D1%82%D0%B8#Russian "идти")* (idtí, “to [go](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/go "go") ”, unidirectional), *[бе́гать](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%B1%D0%B5%D0%B3%D0%B0%D1%82%D1%8C#Russian "бегать")* (bégatʹ, “to run”, multidirectional) vs. *[бежа́ть](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%B1%D0%B5%D0%B6%D0%B0%D1%82%D1%8C#Russian "бежать")* (bežátʹ, “to run”, unidirectional), *[носи́ть](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%BD%D0%BE%D1%81%D0%B8%D1%82%D1%8C#Russian "носить")* (nosítʹ, “to carry”, multidirectional) vs. *[нести́](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%BD%D0%B5%D1%81%D1%82%D0%B8#Russian "нести")* (nestí, “to carry”, unidirectional). English does not make this distinction. For example, "I went to the post office" could be multidirectional (if I went there and came back) or unidirectional (if I am there now), and different Russian verbs would be used to translate "went" in these two circumstances. In Polish coming back does not cause multidirectional verbs to be used, only doing something many times (*Chodzę do biura.* 'I go to the office (every day).' vs. *Idę do biura* 'I am going to the office (now).') or moving without target (*Chodzę po pokoju* 'I am walking around the room.' vs. *Idę przez pokój.* 'I am walking across the room.') does. In many Slavic languages, multidirectional verbs are always [imperfective](#imperfective_aspect) in [aspect](#aspect), even with prefixes that are normally associated with the [perfective aspect](#perfective_aspect) (e.g. Polish *przybiegać*). Czech–Slovak languages work differently, as they differentiate between perfective pairs as well, e.g. Czech imperfective *nést* and *nosit*, perfective *nanést* and *nanosit* (the imperfective form is *nanášet*).

[mutation](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mutation "mutation")

The property of a sound in a word, often a [consonant](#consonant) but possibly a [vowel](#vowel) (as in [ablaut](#ablaut) and [umlaut](#umlaut)), altering due to its [morphological](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/morphology "morphology") or [syntactic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/syntax "syntax"), as opposed to [phonological](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/phonology "phonology"), context. Examples of consonant mutations include the word-initial consonant mutations found in many Celtic languages.

[mute *h*](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mute_h "mute h")

In French, an initial ⟨h⟩ that is treated like a [vowel](#vowel); that is to say, [liaison](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/liaison "liaison") and [elision](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/elision "elision") are permitted at the beginning of words that have a mute h.

Several expressions have similar properties, such as *[budge an inch](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/budge_an_inch#English "budge an inch")*: "I won't budge an inch" and "Will he even budge an inch?", but not normally \*"He budged an inch in the negotiations." [non-past tense](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/non-past "non-past")

The [tense](#tense) of a [verb](#verb) that does not pertain to the [past](#past); in particular, applicable to both the [present](#present) and the [future](#future). Common in some languages, such as Arabic. In English, the main verb in the sentences *I am running tomorrow* and *I am running now* can be said to be in the non-past tense, since the same verb can be used to express both the present and the future.

[nonstandard](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nonstandard "nonstandard")

Not conforming to the [standard variety](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/standard_variety "standard variety") or [standard language](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/standard_language "standard language") as accepted by the majority of its speakers; frequently considered incorrect.

[nonvirile](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nonvirile "nonvirile")

[object](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/object "object")

[orthographic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/orthographic "orthographic") [borrowing](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/borrowing "borrowing")

A [loanword](#loanword) that has been borrowed in its written form and re-pronounced according to the conventions of the target language, particularly in East Asian languages written with Chinese characters. For example, the Chinese name *[毛澤東](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/zh:%E6%AF%9B%E6%BE%A4%E6%9D%B1 "w:zh:毛澤東")* / *[毛泽东](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/zh:%E6%AF%9B%E6%B3%BD%E4%B8%9C "w:zh:毛泽东")* (Máo Zédōng) is rendered in Japanese as *[毛沢東](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ja:%E6%AF%9B%E6%B2%A2%E6%9D%B1 "w:ja:毛沢東")* (Mō Takutō): the spelling is the same, but the characters have been pronounced as if they were Japanese words, leading to a significantly different pronunciation. Sometimes, the pronunciations in the source and donor language have no etymological relationship. For example, the Japanese noun [葉 (は) 書 (がき)](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%91%89%E6%9B%B8#Japanese "葉書") (*hagaki*, “postcard”) was orthographically borrowed into Korean as [엽서(葉書)](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%EC%97%BD%EC%84%9C#Korean "엽서") (yeopseo, “postcard”); another example is that the Japanese verb [取 (と) り 消 (け) す](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%8F%96%E3%82%8A%E6%B6%88%E3%81%99#Japanese "取り消す") (*torikesu*, “cancel”) was orthographically borrowed into Chinese as [取消](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%8F%96%E6%B6%88#Chinese "取消") (qǔxiāo, “cancel”), and also into Korean as [취소(取消)](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%EC%B7%A8%EC%86%8C#Korean "취소") (chwiso, “cancel”).

## P

p.

*post* or after, often used in quotations. Hence, a quotation from "p. 1924" is a quotation from no earlier than 1924.

*See **[plural](#plural)**.*

[palatalization](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/palatalization "palatalization")

1. The state or quality of being [palatalized](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/palatalized "palatalized"), i.e. of pronouncing a sound with the tongue against the [palate](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/palate "palate") of the mouth that normally is not so pronounced. Some languages, such as [Russian](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Russian "Russian") and [Irish](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Irish "Irish"), have pairs of palatalized and unpalatalized [consonant](#consonant) [phonemes](#phoneme).
2. A [sound change](#sound_change) that involves a change of consonants to become palatalized or move in the direction of the palate; one of the most common of sound changes, and usually triggered by a following /e/, /i/ or /j/. In English, palatalization (known as [yod-coalescence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/yod-coalescence "w:yod-coalescence")) converted /t/, /d/, /s/, /z/ to /t͡ʃ/, /d͡ʒ/, /ʃ/, /ʒ/ before a /j/ (which was later lost), resulting in the sounds found in *na **t** ure*, *proce **d** ure*, *pre **ss** ure*, *mea **s** ure*, where the spelling still indicates the sound as it was prior to palatalization. Palatalization still operates [synchronically](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/synchronically "synchronically") before a /j/, producing, for instance, the pronunciations *[gotcha](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gotcha#English "gotcha")* and *[didja](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/didja#English "didja")* from *[got](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/got#English "got") [you](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/you#English "you")* and *[did](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/did#English "did") [you](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/you#English "you")*.

[palindrome](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/palindrome "palindrome")

A word, phrase, number or any other [sequence](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sequence "sequence") of units which has the property of reading the same [forwards](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/forwards "forwards") as it does [backwards](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/backwards "backwards"), character for character, sometimes disregarding punctuation, [capitalization](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/capitalization "capitalization") and [diacritics](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/diacritic "diacritic").

[paradigm](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/paradigm "paradigm")

A set of all forms which contain a common element, especially the set of all [inflectional](#inflection) forms of a word or a particular grammatical category.

[paragoge](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/paragoge "paragoge") (also [epithesis](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/epithesis "epithesis"))

The addition of sounds at the end of a word without changing its morphological structure or meaning. Cf. [prothesis](#prothesis), [epenthesis](#epenthesis).

[parisyllabic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/parisyllabic "parisyllabic")

A word whose inflections contain the same number of syllables as the lemma form (compare [imparisyllabic](#imparisyllabic)).

[paronym](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/paronym "paronym")

A word derived from the same [root](#root) or [stem](#stem) as another word; classes include [cognates](#cognate) and naturalizations of foreign words.

A near- [homophone](#homophone), a word that sounds like another word (some example pairs are cognate).

[paronymic attraction](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/paronymic_attraction "paronymic attraction")

The phenomenon of similar-sounding native words ([paronyms](#paronym)) affecting the development of loan words.

[paroxytone](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/paroxytone "paroxytone")

With the stress upon the [penultimate](#penultima) (second to last) syllable (e.g. *[εθνολόγος](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B5%CE%B8%CE%BD%CE%BF%CE%BB%CF%8C%CE%B3%CE%BF%CF%82#Greek "εθνολόγος")* (ethnológos)). (Compare with *[oxytone](#oxytone)*, *[proparoxytone](#proparoxytone)* and *[barytone](#barytone)*.)

[part of speech](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/part_of_speech "part of speech"), POS, PoS

The category that a word belongs to, with respect to how it's used as part of phrases and sentences. Examples are [nouns](#noun), [adjectives](#adjective) and [verbs](#verb). The part of speech is inherent in the word itself, and is independent of any specific role that the word may have within any given sentence (e.g. subject, [direct object](#direct_object)). Words may belong to more than one part of speech: English *[this](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/this#English "this")* is both a [determiner](#determiner) and a [pronoun](#pronoun), while *[coat](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/coat#English "coat")* is both a noun and a verb.

[partial calque](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/partial_calque "partial calque")

A term which is only in part a [calque](#calque), such that a part was translated and the other was [borrowed](#loanword) directly. For example, English *[liverwurst](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/liverwurst#English "liverwurst")* is a partial calque of German *[Leberwurst](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Leberwurst#German "Leberwurst")*: the first part *[Leber](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Leber#German "Leber")* (“liver”) was translated, but the second part *[Wurst](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Wurst#German "Wurst")* (“sausage”) was borrowed without translation. Another case is Portuguese *[verbo frasal](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verbo_frasal#Portuguese "verbo frasal")*, where *[verbo](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verbo#Portuguese "verbo")* (“verb”) is a translation, but *[frasal](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/frasal#Portuguese "frasal")* (“sentential”) is a [semantic loan](#semantic_loan) of English *[phrasal](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/phrasal#English "phrasal")* (which translates to Portuguese *[locucional](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/locucional#Portuguese "locucional")*). A partial calque is also known as a *[loanblend](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/loanblend "loanblend")*.

[participle](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/participle "participle")

A form of a [verb](#verb) that may function as an [adjective](#adjective) or [noun](#noun). English has two types of participles: the [present](#present) participle and the [past](#past) participle. In other languages, also [future](#future), [perfect](#perfect), [future perfect](#future_perfect) participles.

[particle](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/particle "particle")

A word that does not fall into the usual [part of speech](#part_of_speech) categories, but which modifies another word or the sentence as a whole. The English term *[like](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/like#Particle "like")* is used as a particle in many dialects. Particles are more common in other Indo-European languages (e.g. German *[doch](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/doch#German "doch")*, which marks a sentence as being surprising or rebutting a previous statement) and in East Asian languages (e.g. Japanese *[は](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E3%81%AF#Japanese "は")*, which marks the topic of a sentence). Many [clitics](#clitic) are particles.

[partitive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/partitive "partitive")

Indicating partialness or indeterminateness, such as "some water" or "something nice". In Dutch, it is a word form that is used when referring to undetermined things or amounts (example: *iets interessants* = "something interesting"). French has special partitive [articles](#article) which qualify indefinite [mass nouns](#mass_noun) (example: *J'ai **du** café* can be translated as either "I have **some** coffee" or simply "I have coffee").

[partitive case](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/partitive_case "partitive case")

A [case](#case) that expresses a partial object or an action that is not performed to completion.

[passive voice](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/passive_voice "passive voice")

the [voice](#voice) verb form in which the subject is not the person or thing doing the action, and is usually having the action done on them (compare [active voice](#active_voice)), e.g. *the ball was kicked (by the boy).* (See also *[voice](#voice)*)

[past tense](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/past_tense "past tense")

The [tense](#tense) of a [verb](#verb) used to refer to an event, transaction, or occurrence that did happen or has happened, or an object that existed, at a point in time before now. An English example is *[saw](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/saw#English "saw")* in *I saw my friend yesterday*.

[past historic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/past_historic "past historic")

A [tense](#tense) found primarily in writing in some languages, especially certain [Romance languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romance_languages "w:Romance languages"). It has the same meaning as the [preterite](#preterite) but is used particularly in [narrative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/narrative "narrative").

past perfect

Same as *[pluperfect](#pluperfect)*.

[paucal](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/paucal "paucal")

A [grammatical number](#number) found in some languages (e.g. [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic_language "w:Arabic language")) that indicates a small number of items or individuals, often around 3 to 10. Usually contrasts with [singular](#singular_number), [dual](#dual_number) and [plural](#plural_number).

[pejorative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pejorative "pejorative") (or [derogatory](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/derogatory "derogatory"))

A word form expressing a negative or belittling attitude towards the person or thing referred to. Compare English *[artsy-fartsy](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/artsy-fartsy#English "artsy-fartsy")* with the neutral equivalent *[artistic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/artistic#English "artistic")*. Some languages have specific prefixes or suffixes for expressing a pejorative attitude, e.g. Spanish *[\-ucho](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-ucho#Spanish "-ucho")* / *[\-acho](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-acho#Spanish "-acho")* or English *[schm-](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/schm-#English "schm-")*.

[pejorative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pejorative "pejorative") [collective](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/collective "collective")

A word form expressing both (pejorative and collective). Known for some collective words in Russian with *[\-ьё](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-%D1%8C%D1%91#Russian "-ьё")* (\-ʹjó) suffix, e.g. *[бабьё](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=%D0%B1%D0%B0%D0%B1%D1%8C%D1%91&action=edit&redlink=1 "бабьё (page does not exist)")* (babʹjó), *[мужичьё](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=%D0%BC%D1%83%D0%B6%D0%B8%D1%87%D1%8C%D1%91&action=edit&redlink=1 "мужичьё (page does not exist)")* (mužičʹjó), *[cтаричьё](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=c%D1%82%D0%B0%D1%80%D0%B8%D1%87%D1%8C%D1%91&action=edit&redlink=1 "cтаричьё (page does not exist)")* (ctaričʹjó), *[тряпьё](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D1%82%D1%80%D1%8F%D0%BF%D1%8C%D1%91#Russian "тряпьё")* (trjapʹjó), for some with *[\-ня́](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-%D0%BD%D1%8F#Russian "-ня")* (\-njá) suffix, e.g. *[алкашня́](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%B0%D0%BB%D0%BA%D0%B0%D1%88%D0%BD%D1%8F#Russian "алкашня")* (alkašnjá), *[волосня́](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%B2%D0%BE%D0%BB%D0%BE%D1%81%D0%BD%D1%8F#Russian "волосня")* (volosnjá), for some with *[\-та́](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-%D1%82%D0%B0#Russian "-та")* (\-tá) suffix, e.g. *[школота́](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D1%88%D0%BA%D0%BE%D0%BB%D0%BE%D1%82%D0%B0#Russian "школота")* (školotá).

[penultima](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/penultima "penultima")

The next-to-last [syllable](#syllable) of a word.

[perfect](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/perfect "perfect")

The [aspect](#aspect) of a verb indicating that the action described is completed. In English, it consists of the verb *have* + the past participle, e.g. *Tom has painted the fence* or *Tom has taken medicine*. Depending on the tense of *have* one can have present perfect, which are represented in the previous examples, or past perfect: *Tom had painted the fence*, *Tom had taken medicine*. *To have painted* is a perfect infinitive. See also *[Imperfect](#imperfect)*. Not to be confused with *[perfective](#perfective)*.

[perfective](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/perfective "perfective")

The [aspect](#aspect) of a verb that denotes viewing the event the verb describes as a simple whole rather than as having internal structure. For example, "she sat down" as opposed to "she was sitting down". As this may often lead to an interpretation of completeness of what is expressed by the verb, this aspect is generally associated with the past and future tenses. Common in [Slavic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavic_languages "w:Slavic languages") such as Russian. This term is often used interchangeably with *[aorist](#aorist) aspect*. Not to be confused with *[perfect](#perfect)*.

Contrast *[imperfective](#imperfective).*

[periphrastic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/periphrastic "periphrastic")

Using more words to produce a grammatical effect. For example, "more fair" is a *periphrastic* form of "fairer". The English future tense requires *periphrastic* usage: "I *will write* an essay." (Compare *[monolectic](#monolectic)* and *[polylectic](#polylectic)*.)

[person](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/person "person"), grammatical person

A grammatical category that indicates the relationship between the speaker and what is being spoken of. Examples are [first person](#first_person), [second person](#second_person) and [third person](#third_person).

[personal](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/personal "personal")

Having reference particular to humans and other entities having personality such as a named animal, a deity, a personification, etc. Compare *[animate](#animate).*

[phoneme](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/phoneme "phoneme")

An indivisible unit of sound in a given language, an abstraction of the physical speech sounds.

[phono-semantic compound](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/phono-semantic_compound "phono-semantic compound")

A Chinese character ([CJKV](#CJKV) character) composed of a component which is related to the meaning of the character and another component which is related to the sound of the character, example: the character *[媽](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%AA%BD#Chinese "媽")* / *[妈](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A6%88#Chinese "妈")* (mā, literally “mother”) ([OC](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Old_Chinese "Old Chinese") \*maːʔ) is a phono-semantic compound made up of the semantic *[女](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A5%B3#Chinese "女")* (literally “female”) (on the left-hand side of *媽* / *妈*) and the phonetic *[馬](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%A6%AC#Chinese "馬")* / *[马](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%A9%AC#Chinese "马")* (mǎ) (OC \*mraːʔ) (on the right-hand side of *媽* / *妈*).

[phono-semantic matching](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/phono-semantic_matching "phono-semantic matching")

A word [borrowed](#borrowing) into one language from another in a way that completely or partially preserves both the original sound and meaning. Phono-semantic matchings are especially common in Mandarin Chinese; examples are *[可口可樂](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%8F%AF%E5%8F%A3%E5%8F%AF%E6%A8%82#Chinese "可口可樂")* / *[可口可乐](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%8F%AF%E5%8F%A3%E5%8F%AF%E4%B9%90#Chinese "可口可乐")* (Kěkǒukělè, “Coca-Cola”, literally “tasty \[and\] entertaining”) and *[萬維網](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%90%AC%E7%B6%AD%E7%B6%B2#Chinese "萬維網")* / *[万维网](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E4%B8%87%E7%BB%B4%E7%BD%91#Chinese "万维网")* (Wànwéiwǎng, “world wide web”, literally “myriad dimensional net”).

[phrase](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/phrase "phrase")

1. A word or group of words that functions as a single unit in the [syntax](#syntax) of a [sentence](#sentence), usually consisting of a [head](#head), or central word, and elaborating words. Examples are *the good boy* (a [noun](#noun) phrase), *very strange* (an [adjective](#adjective) phrase), and *in the house* (a [prepositional](#preposition) phrase). Normally distinguished from a [clause](#clause), a similar group of words that contains a [verb](#verb).
2. Same as [set phrase](#set_phrase).

[piecewise](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/piecewise "piecewise") [doublet](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/doublet "doublet")

One of two (or more) derived words in a language that consist of components that all have the same [etymological](#etymology) roots, but which have entered the language at different points in time; in other words, one of two (or more) words that can be split into individual components and all of those components are [doublets](#doublet) of the equivalent components of the other words.

pl

*See **[plural](#plural)**.*

plene spelling

From [plene](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/plene "plene"), meaning [full](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/full "full"): In languages with [matres lectionis](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mater_lectionis "mater lectionis") ([consonant](#consonant) letters representing [vowels](#vowel)), the standardized form including those letters. The opposite of [defective spelling](#defective_spelling). A form with more letters representing vowels than the standard prescribes is called [excessive spelling](#excessive_spelling).

[plosive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/plosive "plosive")

A [consonant](#consonant) sound produced by completely blocking the airflow through the mouth for a short time. In English, the plosives are [voiceless](#voiceless) /p/, /t/, /k/ (as in the initial sounds of *pea*, *tea*, *key*) and the corresponding [voiced](#voiced) sounds /b/, /d/, /ɡ/ (as in the initial sounds of *buy*, *die*, *guy*). Plosives are distinguished from [fricatives](#fricative), in which the air is mostly but not completely blocked, enough to cause a rough, hissing or buzzing sound, and [approximants](#approximant), in which the airflow is only slightly constricted, resulting in a smooth sound.

[pluperfect](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pluperfect "pluperfect")

A verb form of [perfect](#perfect) [aspect](#aspect) and [past](#past) [tense](#tense), which is used to describe an action or event which is regarded as having been completed in the past, in relation to a time already in the past. E.g. *Tom had painted the fence before I got there*.

[plural](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/plural "plural")

A [grammatical number](#number) that indicates multiple items or individuals. Most languages contrast it with [singular](#singular_number), and plural indicates two or more. Some languages also possess the [dual](#dual_number) or even [trial](#trial_number) numbers; in these instances the plural indicates more than the highest specific number.

[plurale tantum](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/plurale_tantum "plurale tantum") (*pl.* pluralia tantum)

Same as plural only (see below).

[plural](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/plural "plural") [only](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/only "only")

A noun (or a sense of a noun) that is inherently plural and is not used (or is not used in the same sense) in the singular, such as *[pants](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pants#English "pants")* (in the senses of "legwear") or *[wheels](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/wheels#English "wheels")* (in the sense of "vehicle"), is *plural only* or *a [plurale tantum](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/plurale_tantum "plurale tantum")*. In practice, most pluralia tantum are found in the singular in rare cases. (*See* [Category:English pluralia tantum](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Category:English_pluralia_tantum "Category:English pluralia tantum").) Contrast words which are **[singular](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/singular "singular") [only](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/only "only") ([singularia tantum](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/singulare_tantum "singulare tantum"))**.

[poetic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/poetic "poetic")

Language, commonly associated with poetry, that is typically chosen to have an artistic or cultural impact on the listener. Poetic terms and forms may be considered [nonstandard](#nonstandard) or [archaic](#archaic) in non-poetic usage.

[polite](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/polite "polite")

Language that is [respectful](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/respectful "respectful"), [complaisant](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/complaisant "complaisant"), and (in some cases) [deferent](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/deferent "deferent"). Many languages have [inflections](#inflection) and [pronouns](#pronoun) dedicated specifically to forms that are politer than their [familiar](#familiar) counterparts.

[polylectic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/polylectic "polylectic")

Used of a grammatical form accomplished with more than one word (compare [monolectic](#monolectic) and [periphrastic](#periphrastic)).

[portmanteau](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/portmanteau "portmanteau")

A [blend](#blend) that combines meanings.

POS, PoS

*See **[part of speech](#part_of_speech)**.*

[positive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/positive "positive")

The 'normal' form of the [degrees of comparison](#degrees_of_comparison) of an adjective or adverb. Thus [big](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/big "big") is the *positive* form of the trio *big*, *bigger*, *biggest*.

positive polarity item

A term or construction that is generally found only in affirmative sentences, questions, conditionals, and certain other “positive polarity” contexts. See also *[negative polarity item](#negative_polarity_item)*.

[possessional adjective](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/possessional_adjective "possessional adjective")

An adjective indicating that a noun is in possession of some other noun (or a quality of that noun), commonly formed in English by adding the suffix *[\-ed](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-ed#English "-ed")*, *[\-ful](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-ful#English "-ful")*, *[\-y](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-y#English "-y")*, *[\-ous](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-ous#English "-ous")*, *[\-able](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-able#English "-able")*, *[\-ual](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-ual#English "-ual")*, *[\-ish](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-ish#English "-ish")*, *[\-ar](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-ar#English "-ar")*, *[\-esque](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-esque#English "-esque")* or *[\-like](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-like#English "-like")*. For example, an animal is [bushy-tailed](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bushy-tailed "bushy-tailed") if it is in possession of a bushy tail, a person filled with disdain is [disdainful](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/disdainful "disdainful"), and a garden is [leafy](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/leafy "leafy") if there are many leaves on the plants there. Commonly [idiomatic](#idiomatic), such as in [pinheaded](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pinheaded "pinheaded") or [red-faced](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/red-faced "red-faced"). Not to be confused with [possessive adjectives](#possessive_adjective), which indicate who possesses a noun, or [relational adjectives](#relational_adjective), which are often formed in the same way. See also [privative adjectives](#privative_adjective), which denote the opposite.

[possessive adjective](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/possessive_adjective "possessive adjective")

In English, the term *possessive adjective* is an older usage for what is now normally termed a [possessive determiner](#possessive_determiner), such as *my* / *mine*, *your* / *yours*, *his*, etc. In the [Slavic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavic_languages "w:Slavic languages"), however, a *possessive adjective* is an adjective derived from a proper name or from a common noun referring to a person, and indicates possession by that person. For example, in Russian, *[Петро́в](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%9F%D0%B5%D1%82%D1%80%D0%BE%D0%B2#Russian "Петров")* (Petróv) is an adjective meaning “Peter's”, derived from *[Пётр](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%9F%D1%91%D1%82%D1%80#Russian "Пётр")* (Pjotr, “Peter”); and in Czech *[mámin](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/m%C3%A1min#Czech "mámin")* is an adjective meaning “mom's/mum's”, derived from *[máma](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/m%C3%A1ma#Czech "máma")* (“mom, mum”). Such adjectives are used to varying degrees in place of the genitive case, and show up especially in fixed collocations such as the equivalents of English *[Adam's apple](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Adam%27s_apple#English "Adam's apple")* and *[Noah's ark](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Noah%27s_ark#English "Noah's ark")*.

[possessive determiner](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/possessive_determiner "possessive determiner") (*possessive adjective* in older usage)

A [determiner](#determiner) that indicates ownership. For example, in "her book", the word "her" indicates whose book it is. Not to be confused with [possessional adjectives](#possessional_adjective), which indicate what a noun possesses, and [possessive adjectives](#possessive_adjective) in [Slavic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavic_languages "w:Slavic languages"), which are adjectives that are semantically equivalent to the genitive case of proper names or personal nouns.

[postpositive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/postpositive "postpositive")

Placed after the word or phrase modified. Compare [prepositive](#prepositive).

[preconsonantal](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/preconsonantal "preconsonantal")

Immediately preceding a [consonant](#consonant) or consonant sound.

[predicate](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/predicate "predicate")

The part of a [sentence](#sentence) that follows the [subject](#subject). This generally consists of the [verb](#verb) and any [objects](#object). It can also consist of a [linking verb](#linking_verb) (e.g. "to be", "to seem", etc.) and a following [adjective](#adjective), which is termed a *predicate adjective* or *predicative adjective* (contrasted with an *[attributive](#attributive) adjective*, which directly modifies a [noun](#noun)). An example would be *[good](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/good#English "good")* in *The food is good*. Some languages, such as German and Russian, have a special declension for predicate adjectives.

[predicative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/predicative "predicative")

Describes a term that acts as the [predicate](#predicate) or part of the predicate of a [sentence](#sentence) (e.g. a *predicative [adjective](#adjective)*, such as *[good](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/good#English "good")* in *The food is good*). Some adjectives are only-predicative like *[afraid](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/afraid#English "afraid")* or *[glad](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/glad#English "glad")* in English. In Russian, it often specifically refers to an adjective-like [part of speech](#part_of_speech) that serves as the entire predicate of a sentence in an [impersonal](#impersonal) construction. Such constructions often cannot be translated word-for-word in English. An example is *[ску́чно](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D1%81%D0%BA%D1%83%D1%87%D0%BD%D0%BE#Russian "скучно")* (skúčno, “it is [boring](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/boring "boring") ”) in the sentence *[мне](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D1%8F#Russian "я") [ску́чно](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D1%81%D0%BA%D1%83%D1%87%D0%BD%D0%BE#Russian "скучно")* (mne skúčno, “I am [bored](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bored "bored") ”, literally “It is boring to [me](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/me "me") ”).

[prefix](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/prefix "prefix")

A [morpheme](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/morpheme "morpheme") added to the beginning of a word to modify its meaning.

[preposition](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/preposition "preposition")

A word, normally non- [inflecting](#inflection), that is typically employed to connect a following [noun](#noun) or [pronoun](#pronoun), in an [adjectival](#adjective) or [adverbial](#adverb) sense, with some other word. Examples of prepositions in English are *in*, *from* and *during*. Note that some languages have [postpositions](#postposition) instead of prepositions; they function like prepositions but come *after* the noun or pronoun being connected.

[prepositional case](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/prepositional_case "prepositional case")

A [case](#case) used in certain languages, especially Russian, after certain [prepositions](#preposition). In Russian, it corresponds to the [locative case](#locative_case) in other [Slavic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavic_language "w:Slavic language").

[prepositive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/prepositive "prepositive")

Placed before the word or phrase modified. Compare [postpositive](#postpositive).

pre-prefix

*See **[augment](#augment)**.*

[prescriptive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/prescriptive "prescriptive")

Concerning [linguistic prescription](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/linguistic_prescription "w:linguistic prescription"): how some users of a language think it [ought to](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ought_to "ought to") be used, as contrasted with how it is [in fact](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/in_fact "in fact") used, which is sometimes different; compare *[descriptive](#descriptive)*.

Prescription is not the same thing as [standardness](#standard). Example: Both of the following options are [standard](#standard), although one has a history of being [proscribed](#proscribed) (by some speakers, because of [contextual](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/contextual#Adjective "contextual") disapproval of degree of [formality](#formal)):

"Between you and I, we've got it all covered."

"Between you and me, we've got it all covered."

[present tense](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/present_tense "present tense")

The [tense](#tense) of a [verb](#verb) used primarily to refer to an event, transaction, or occurrence happening now or at the present time. The verb *[see](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/see#English "see")* in *I see my friend in the window* is in the present tense. In many languages, including English, the present tense may also be used to refer to past or future events in certain contexts: *My plane leaves tomorrow morning* (where *[leave](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/leave#English "leave")* is a present verb that refers to the future), or: *John Lennon dies of gunshot wounds* (as in a headline, where *[dies](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dies#English "dies")* is a present verb that refers to the past; see [historical present](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/historical_present "historical present")). While such uses are somewhat exceptional in English, the present tense is used much more widely in many languages. For example, the German present tense also covers some or most of the uses of the English future (*I will do*) and present perfect (*I have done*).

[preterite](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/preterite "preterite") (also spelled [preterit](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/preterit "preterit"))

A [tense](#tense) showing an action at a determined moment in the past. In general it is thus the union of the [past tense](#past_tense) with the [perfective](#perfective) aspect, although in some languages it is little more than a synonym for past tense.

[preterite-present verb](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/preterite-present_verb "preterite-present verb")

In [Germanic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Germanic "Germanic") languages, a verb that displays (or historically displayed) [ablaut](#ablaut) in the present tense, and thereby had *present* tense forms resembling the *past* (or *preterite*) tense of a [strong verb](#strong_verb). Most languages have no more than a handful of such verbs, and they are often used as [auxiliary verbs](#auxiliary_verb). English examples are *[shall](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/shall#English "shall")*, *[can](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/can#English "can")*, *[may](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/may#English "may")*.

Contrast [strong verb](#strong_verb), [weak verb](#weak_verb).

[prevocalic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/prevocalic "prevocalic")

Occurring before a following vowel.

[privative adjective](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/privative_adjective "privative adjective")

1. An adjective indicating that a noun refers to an object that is not of the class which that noun ordinarily refers to. For example, in the term "fake weapon", the word "fake" denotes that the object is not a weapon, just as the word "toy" in "toy car" denotes that it is not a car.
2. An adjective indicating that a noun lacks some other noun (or a quality of that noun), usually formed in English by adding the suffix *[\-less](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-less#English "-less")* or *[\-free](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-free#English "-free")* to the noun that is absent. For example, someone is [beardless](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/beardless "beardless") if they don't have a beard, while a coffee might be [sugar-free](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sugar-free "sugar-free") if it contains no sugar. Commonly [idiomatic](#idiomatic), such as in [brainless](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/brainless "brainless") or [heartless](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/heartless "heartless"). See also [possessional adjectives](#possessional_adjective), which denote the opposite.

[productive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/productive "productive")

Used to form new words and phrases. For example, when a new verb appears in Modern English, the productive suffix *[\-ed](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-ed#English "-ed")* is used to form its past participle; by contrast, the suffix *[\-en](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-en#English:_plural_noun "-en")* appears in many existing past participles, but is not productive, in that it is not (usually) used to form new ones.

[progressive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/progressive "progressive")

The [aspect](#aspect) of a verb, indicating that the action described is, was or will be continuing, uncompleted or repeated. A verb form indicating that an action is [in progress](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/in_progress "in progress"). In English, formed from a combination of *be* + the present participle (*\-ing* form) of the verb. So one can have present progressive (e.g. ***is** painting*), past progressive (e.g. ***was** painting*), future progressive (e.g. ***will be** painting*), etc. Similar to, but less general than, the [imperfective](#imperfective) [aspect](#aspect). (See also *[continuous](#continuous)*.)

[pronominal verb](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pronominal_verb "pronominal verb")

A form of [verb](#verb) that has an attached [pronoun](#pronoun); this is a prominent feature of [Romance](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Romance_language "Romance language") and Slavic languages. The verb–pronoun combination can have [reflexive](#reflexive), [reciprocal](#reciprocal), [passive](#passive), [subjective](#subjective) or [idiomatic](#idiomatic) semantics.

[pronoun](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pronoun "pronoun")

A [part of speech](#part_of_speech) that acts as a substitute for a [noun](#noun) or [noun phrase](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/noun_phrase "noun phrase") and refers to a topic of the discussion. Pronouns can refer to a participant in the discussion and can be used instead of a person's name, such as with the pronouns *[I](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/I#English "I")* and *[you](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/you#English "you")*. Other pronouns, such as *[he](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/he#English "he")*, *[she](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/she#English "she")*, and *[it](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/it#English "it")*, can be used to refer to other people or objects that have already been mentioned without repeating their names.

[pronunciation spelling](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pronunciation_spelling "pronunciation spelling")

A respelling that more closely reflects the pronunciation of a word.

[proparoxytone](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/proparoxytone "proparoxytone")

With the stress upon the [antepenultimate](#antepenultimate) (third to last) syllable (e.g. *[εθνικότητα](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B5%CE%B8%CE%BD%CE%B9%CE%BA%CF%8C%CF%84%CE%B7%CF%84%CE%B1#Greek "εθνικότητα")* (ethnikótita)). (Compare with *[oxytone](#oxytone)*, *[paroxytone](#paroxytone)*, and *[barytone](#barytone)*.)

[proper noun](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/proper_noun "proper noun")

A kind of [noun](#noun) that usually refers to a specific, unique thing, such as *[Earth](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Earth#English "Earth")* and *[the Alps](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Alps#English "Alps")*, though one language's proper noun may translate to another language using a *common* (not proper) noun. In English, proper nouns are usually capitalized, as are common nouns and adjectives derived from proper nouns. The same word may have both common-noun and proper-noun senses (such as *[German](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/German#English "German")*, which is both a proper noun denoting a certain language, and a common noun denoting a person from Germany), and most proper nouns can sometimes be used as common nouns – e.g. *[John](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/John#English "John")* is a proper noun that is a first name, but can be used a common noun with plural *[Johns](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Johns#English "Johns")* meaning "people named John".

[proscribed](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/proscribe "proscribe")

Some authorities or commentators recommend against or warn against the listed usage.

Wiktionary's use of this label does not mean that Wiktionary itself advocates the proscribing, nor that all speakers of the language agree with the proscribing. Wiktionary does not issue editorial opinions but rather simply reports facts about usage, including usage advice that exists and that some people follow because, for example, a citable [reference work](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/reference_work#English "reference work") is known (by many speakers) to recommend it.

Proscribing (forbidding) is understandable both (1) as the negative *type* of [prescribing](#prescriptive) when the latter means "dictating [dos and don'ts](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dos_and_don%27ts "dos and don'ts") " and (2) as the [opposite](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/opposite#Noun "opposite") of [prescribing](#prescriptive) when the latter means "preferring".

[prothesis](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/prothesis "prothesis")

The prepending of [phonemes](#phoneme) at the beginning of a word without changing its morphological structure, as in *[nother](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nother#English "nother")*, from *[other](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/other#English "other")* (“a whole nother thing”, via [tmesis](#tmesis)), or Spanish *[esfera](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/esfera#Spanish "esfera")* from Latin *[sphaera](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sphaera#Latin "sphaera")* (“sphere”).

[protologism](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/protologism#Noun "protologism")

A [neologism](#neologism) or any coined word with very limited use. By definition, protologisms do not meet Wiktionary's [criteria for inclusion](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Wiktionary:Criteria_for_inclusion "Wiktionary:Criteria for inclusion").

See also [Appendix:Protologisms](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Protologisms "Appendix:Protologisms").

[proverb](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/proverb "proverb")

A [phrase](#phrase) expressing a basic truth which may be applied to common situations.

[proximal](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/proximal "proximal")

Indicates nearness to the speaker.

Contrast *[distal](#distal)*.

[pseudo-acronym](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pseudo-acronym "pseudo-acronym")

A word that has the form of an [acronym](#acronym) or [initialism](#initialism) but no longer stands for anything.

[pseudo-anglicism](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pseudo-anglicism "pseudo-anglicism")

A word in a non-English language that is formed from English elements and may appear to be English, but that does not exist as an English word with the same meaning, such as *[Flipper](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Flipper#German "Flipper")* (“pinball machine”), *[サラリーマン](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E3%82%B5%E3%83%A9%E3%83%AA%E3%83%BC%E3%83%9E%E3%83%B3#Japanese "サラリーマン")* (sararīman, “white-collar worker”, literally “salary-man”) and *[afterwork](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/afterwork#Swedish "afterwork")* (“informal gathering after work”).

[pseudo-loan](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pseudo-loan "pseudo-loan")

A word in one language that appears to be a direct borrowing from another language, but does not exist in that language or has an unrelated meaning. Examples from English include French *[footing](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/footing#French "footing")* (“jogging”), German *[Bodybag](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Bodybag#German "Bodybag")* (“messenger bag, courier bag”), Japanese *[サラリーマン](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E3%82%B5%E3%83%A9%E3%83%AA%E3%83%BC%E3%83%9E%E3%83%B3#Japanese "サラリーマン")* (sararīman, “white-collar worker, ' [salaryman](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/salaryman "salaryman") '”) and Swedish *[afterwork](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/afterwork#Swedish "afterwork")* (“informal gathering after work”). Depending on the source language, more specific terms may be in use, e.g. *[pseudo-anglicism](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pseudo-anglicism "pseudo-anglicism")* for pseudo-loans from English; *[wasei-eigo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/wasei-eigo "w:wasei-eigo")* specifically for Japanese-language pseudo-loans from English; *[wasei-kango](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/wasei-kango "w:wasei-kango")* for Japanese-language pseudo-loans from Chinese; *[pseudo-Latinism](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Category:Pseudo-loans_from_Latin_by_language "Category:Pseudo-loans from Latin by language")* for pseudo-loans from Latin (cf. [hocus-pocus](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hocus-pocus "hocus-pocus"), [noli illegitimi carborundum](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/noli_illegitimi_carborundum "noli illegitimi carborundum")); *[pseudo-Hispanism](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Category:Pseudo-loans_from_Spanish_by_language "Category:Pseudo-loans from Spanish by language")* for pseudo-loans from Spanish (cf. [no problemo](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/no_problemo "no problemo"), [que sera sera](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/que_sera_sera "que sera sera")); *[pseudo-Gallicism](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Category:Pseudo-loans_from_French_by_language "Category:Pseudo-loans from French by language")* for pseudo-loans from French (cf. [vive la différence](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vive_la_diff%C3%A9rence "vive la différence"), [nom de plume](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nom_de_plume "nom de plume"), [triple entendre](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/triple_entendre "triple entendre")); *[pseudo-Germanism](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Category:Pseudo-loans_from_German_by_language "Category:Pseudo-loans from German by language")* (from German), *[pseudo-Japonism](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Category:Pseudo-loans_from_Japanese_by_language "Category:Pseudo-loans from Japanese by language")* (from Japanese), *[pseudo-Arabism](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Category:Pseudo-loans_from_Arabic_by_language "Category:Pseudo-loans from Arabic by language")* (from Arabic), *[pseudo-Italianism](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Category:Pseudo-loans_from_Italian_by_language "Category:Pseudo-loans from Italian by language")* (from Italian), etc.

## Q

[quantifier](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/quantifier "quantifier")

A word that specifies the amount of its related noun, such as [all](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/all "all"), [no](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/no "no"), [many](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/many "many"), [several](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/several "several"), [eight](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/eight "eight"), [half](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/half "half"), etc.

## R

[rare](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rare "rare")

A term or sense that is attested but not used commonly either in spoken or written language, even less so than [uncommon](#uncommon) terms. A rare term or sense is typically difficult to find even when deliberately searching for it, and may be attested only a handful of times in accessible [corpora](#corpus).

[reanalysis](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/reanalysis "reanalysis")

Analysis of a [lexeme](#lexeme) with a different structure from its original, often by misunderstanding. For example, *[hamburger](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hamburger#English "hamburger")*, which is originally *[Hamburg](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Hamburg#English "Hamburg")* + *[\-er](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-er#English "-er")*, was reanalyzed as *[ham](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ham#English "ham")* + *[burger](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/burger#English "burger")*, which produced words like *[cheeseburger](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cheeseburger#English "cheeseburger")*.

[rebracketing](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rebracketing "rebracketing")

The process by which a word originally derived from one source is broken down or bracketed into a different set of factors. The understanding of *[hamburger](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hamburger#English "hamburger")* as *[ham](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ham#English "ham")* + *[burger](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/burger#English "burger")* (rather than *[Hamburg](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Hamburg#English "Hamburg")* + *[\-er](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-er#English "-er")*) is an example of rebracketing. Also called **[metanalysis](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/metanalysis "metanalysis")**.

[reciprocal](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/reciprocal "reciprocal")

Used to indicate that subjects have other subjects as object. Pronouns can be reciprocal (in English *[each other](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/each_other#English "each other")*), as well as verbs [reciprocal](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/reciprocal "reciprocal") quality as [lexemes](#lexeme).

[reconstruction](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/reconstruction "reconstruction")

A word that is not recorded in actual texts or other media, but has been recreated from its descendant forms, using the [comparative method](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/comparative_method "comparative method") of linguistics.

[reducible](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/reducible "reducible")

In the [Slavic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavic_languages "w:Slavic languages"), a word (especially a noun or adjective) with an alternation between a vowel and no vowel in different forms of the word, pursuant to [Havlík’s law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Havl%C3%ADk%27s_law "w:Havlík's law"). In Russian, for example, the reducible masculine noun *[вене́ц](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%B2%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B5%D1%86#Russian "венец")* (venéc, “ [crown](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/crown "crown") ”) appears unreduced (with /e/ in the final syllable) in the masculine singular, and reduced (with no /e/) in other forms, e.g. genitive singular *[венца́](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%B2%D0%B5%D0%BD%D1%86%D0%B0#Russian "венца")* (vencá), nominative plural *[венцы́](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%B2%D0%B5%D0%BD%D1%86%D1%8B#Russian "венцы")* (vencý).

[reduplication](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/reduplication "reduplication")

The repetition of a word or a part of a word (as few as two [phonemes](#phoneme) in a word, or the whole sequence of phonemes in the word) with no or slight change. Compare [haplology](#haplology), the [elision](#elision) of a repeated part of a word.

[reflex](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/reflex "reflex")

The name given to a descendant word in a [daughter language](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/daughter_language "daughter language"), descended from an earlier language. For example, Modern English *[heat](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/heat#English "heat")* is the reflex of the Old English *[hǣtu](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/h%C3%A6tu#Old_English "hætu")*.

Rarely, this word is used in reverse, and the 'reflex' is actually the root word rather than the descendant word. However, this usage is usually filled by the term *[etymon](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/etymon#English "etymon")* instead.

[reflexive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/reflexive "reflexive")

Referring back to the subject, or having an object equal to the subject. Pronouns can be reflexive (e.g. *[myself](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/myself#English "myself")*, *[oneself](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/oneself#English "oneself")*). [Romance](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Romance_language "Romance language") and Slavic languages make extensive use of reflexive verb forms (e.g. Italian *[lavarsi](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lavarsi#Italian "lavarsi")*, Spanish *[lavarse](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lavarse#Spanish "lavarse")* (“to wash oneself”)). These are part of a larger group of [pronominal verbs](#pronominal_verb).

[register](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/register#English:_language_register "register")

A variety of a language used for a particular purpose or in a particular social setting. Encompasses variants such as [slang](#slang), [colloquial](#colloquial), [informal](#informal), [formal](#formal), etc.

[related terms](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Wiktionary:Entry_layout_explained#Related_terms "Wiktionary:Entry layout explained")

Words in the same language that have strong etymological connections but are **not** derived terms.

[relational](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/relational "relational")

Refers to a type of adjective in some languages, e.g. Latin, Ancient Greek, and the [Slavic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavic_languages "w:Slavic languages"), that takes the place of a noun when it modifies another noun. Consider an English phrase such as *chicken soup*. In English, this phrase can be constructed by simply placing a noun such as *chicken* in the position normally occupied by an adjective, i.e. directly before the noun. In languages such as Russian, however, this cannot be done, and instead the word *[ку́рица](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%BA%D1%83%D1%80%D0%B8%D1%86%D0%B0#Russian "курица")* (kúrica, “chicken”) must be replaced by the relational adjective *[кури́ный](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%BA%D1%83%D1%80%D0%B8%D0%BD%D1%8B%D0%B9#Russian "куриный")* (kurínyj, “related to chickens”) when forming the Russian equivalent *[кури́ный](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%BA%D1%83%D1%80%D0%B8%D0%BD%D1%8B%D0%B9#Russian "куриный") [суп](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D1%81%D1%83%D0%BF#Russian "суп")* (kurínyj sup, “chicken soup”). Generally, adjectives of this sort cannot be qualified by *more*, *less* or *very*. This concept relates to the concept of the [noun adjunct](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/noun_adjunct "noun adjunct") or, in English [attributive noun](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/attributive_noun "attributive noun").

[relative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/relative "relative")

1. Marking a [relative clause](#relative_clause). Often used of pronouns, such as *the tree **[which](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/which#English "which")**...*.
2. In the [Bantu languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bantu_languages "w:Bantu languages"), a [part of speech](#part_of_speech) that resembles an [adjective](#adjective) in function, but behaves morphologically and syntactically like a [relative clause](#relative_clause).

[relative clause](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/relative_clause "relative clause")

A [subordinate clause](#subordinate_clause) that modifies a [noun](#noun). In *The man who I saw yesterday is leaving today*, the clause *who I saw yesterday* is a relative clause. In English, relative clauses are often introduced by a [relative](#relative) [pronoun](#pronoun) such as *who*, *which* or *that*, but other languages often have different strategies for marking relative clauses.

[respelling](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/respelling "respelling")

A different spelling of a word, especially to show its pronunciation.

For example, the French word *[souris](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/souris#French "souris")* (literally “mouse”) was given the additional meaning *[computer](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/computer#English "computer") [mouse](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mouse#English "mouse")* in imitation of English *[mouse](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mouse#English "mouse")*, which already had both meanings. Semantic loans are distinguished from [calques](#calque) in that calques are not considered to have a pre-existing idiomatic meaning at the time that they are coined.

[semi-learned borrowing](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/semi-learned_borrowing "semi-learned borrowing")

A [loanword](#loanword) that was borrowed from a [classical language](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/classical_language "classical language") such as Latin or Sanskrit (especially when borrowed into a descendant of the same language), which has been partly reshaped by later [sound change](#sound_change) or analogy with inherited terms ([adapted](#adapted_borrowing)).

Contrast with [learned borrowings](#learned_borrowing), which have usually not been similarly reshaped ([unadapted](#unadapted_borrowing)), and [inherited](#inherited) terms, which have undergone all the normal sound changes of a language. For example, the Portuguese term *[artigo](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/artigo#Portuguese "artigo")* (“article”) is a semi-learned borrowing from Latin *[articulus](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/articulus#Latin "articulus")* (“joint, limb, division”), which was borrowed early enough to undergo later sound changes that [lenited](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lenite "lenite") *c* into *g* and deleted *l* between vowels, but did not undergo earlier sound changes, such as lowering short *i* to *e*. [standard](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/standard#English:_linguistics "standard")

Conforming to the [standard variety](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/standard_variety#Noun "standard variety") or [standard language](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/standard_language#Noun "standard language"). Contrast *[nonstandard](#nonstandard)*. Standardness in linguistics is [descriptive](#descriptive), but part of the phenomenon that description describes is the fact that some [prescriptive](#prescriptive) notions can influence standardness via a [feedback loop](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/feedback_loop "feedback loop").[^2] Usages can be [informal](#informal) but standard, even when they are not [formal](#formal) and even when they are [proscribed](#proscribed) by some speakers.

A [standard variety](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/standard_variety#Noun "standard variety") is often a [prestige variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/prestige_\(sociolinguistics\) "w:prestige (sociolinguistics)"). This can cause some confusion about [nonstandardness](#nonstandard) equalling *[inherent](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/inherent#Adjective "inherent")* "inferiority", "incorrectness", or "wrongness" when all it really equals is *[contextual](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/context#Noun "context")* inappropriateness. Within a single language, a [wordform](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/wordform#Noun "wordform") or [usage](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/usage#English:_language "usage") can be an [idiomatic](#idiomatic) norm within some [varieties](#variety) even when it is not the norm in the [standard variety](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/standard_variety#Noun "standard variety"). (For example, in English this is true of the [zero copula](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/zero_copula "zero copula") in a sentence such as *He gone already*.) It can also be the idiomatic norm in [informal](#informal) register within the standard variety even when it is often avoided [formally](#formal) therein. (In English this is true, for example, of most [contraction](#contraction) use.) These facts explain the important difference between [code-switching](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/code-switching#Noun "code-switching") and speaking "incorrectly" ([as if](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/as_if "as if") with *inherently* "wrong" forms or usage).

[strong declension](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/strong_declension#English "strong declension")

In German, a declension of adjectives, and of masculine and neuter nouns, that is defined in opposition to the [weak declension](#weak_declension) and [mixed declension](#mixed_declension). Strong nouns (typically) form their genitive in *\-s* and/or *\-es* and do not form their plural by adding *\-n* and/or *\-en*. In adjectives, the strong declension is a set of endings used when the adjective is not accompanied by a definite or indefinite article, and includes a fuller set of endings than that of the weak or mixed declensions (hence the name). Note that "strong", "weak" and "mixed" refer to inherent declensional properties of nouns, but all adjectives can be declined according to all three declensions, depending on whether an article precedes and what type of article it is. By analogy, the term "strong declension" is sometimes used in other inflected Germanic languages such as [Icelandic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icelandic "w:Icelandic") and [Old English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_English "w:Old English"). In these languages, adjectives are formed similarly to German but there is typically not a single strong noun declension. Rather, a strong noun is any noun that does not follow the weak declension.

strong pronoun

In Greek, an [emphatic pronoun](#emphatic).

[strong verb](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/strong_verb "strong verb") and strong conjugation

In [Germanic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Germanic "Germanic") languages, a verb that displays [ablaut](#ablaut). More specifically, a verb that has a change in [vowel](#vowel) between present and past. An English example is *[drink](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/drink#English "drink")*, *[drank](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/drank#English "drank")*, *[drunk](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/drunk#English "drunk")*. Note that some verbs show a vowel change, but not as a result of ablaut (e.g. *[think](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/think#English "think")*, *[thought](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/thought#English "thought")*); these are not considered strong verbs.

Contrast [weak verb](#weak_verb), [preterite-present verb](#preterite-present_verb).

[subject](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/subject "subject")

In a [clause](#clause): the word or word group (usually a noun phrase) that is dealt with. In [active clauses](#active_voice) with [verbs](#verb) denoting an action, the subject and the actor are usually the same.

Contrast [object](#object).

[subjunctive mood](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/subjunctive_mood "subjunctive mood")

The [mood](#mood) of a [verb](#verb) expressing an action or state which is [hypothetical](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hypothetical#Adjective "hypothetical") or [anticipated](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/anticipate#Verb "anticipate") rather than actual, including [wishes](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/wish "wish") and [counterfactuals](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/counterfactual#Noun "counterfactual"). Some English examples are *if I **were** rich* and *it’s important that he **be** here*. In many languages [imperative](#imperative) (command) [inflections](#inflection) are the same as or similar to subjunctive ones, reflecting the shared trait of non- [indicative](#indicative) nature: yet- [unrealized](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/unrealized "unrealized") ideas.

[subordinate clause](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/subordinate_clause "subordinate clause")

Same as [dependent clause](#dependent_clause).

[substantive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/substantive "substantive")

1. A noun or adjective (or phrase), that names a real object with *substance*. This is in contrast to an [attributive](#attributive) noun or adjective, which names a real object that carries the *attributes* of the named noun or adjective.
2. In [Mongolic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Mongolic "Mongolic") languages, it sometimes refers to a single [part of speech](#part_of_speech) that collectively encompasses nouns, adjectives and adverbs, due to their shared morphological behaviour.

substantivization

*See **[nominalization](#nominalization)**.*

[substratum](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/substratum "substratum")

A language [stratum](#stratum) which has lower cultural or political prestige than the one which it influences. Many historical **substrate** languages have gone extinct without ever having been attested, and so they must be inferred from their influences on surviving or attested languages. One such example is the [Pre-Greek](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Pre-Greek "Pre-Greek") substratum.

[suffix](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/suffix "suffix")

A [morpheme](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/morpheme "morpheme") added to the end of a word to modify its meaning.

[superlative](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/superlative "superlative")

An [inflection](#inflection), or different form, of a **[comparable](#comparable)** adjective showing a relative quality, denoting "to the ultimate extent". (See also *[comparative](#comparative)* and *[degrees of comparison](#degrees_of_comparison)*.) In English, the superlative form is often formed by appending *[est](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/est#English "est")*, or using the word *[most](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/most#English "most")*. For example, the superlative of *big* is " *biggest* "; of *confident*, " *most confident* ".

[superseded](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/supersede "supersede")

Especially of a spelling, formerly standard, and still frequently encountered, but now deprecated in favor of another form as the result of a spelling reform. Examples include Portuguese *[idéia](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/id%C3%A9ia#Portuguese "idéia")*, fully replaced by *[ideia](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ideia#Portuguese "ideia")* in [2015](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_Language_Orthographic_Agreement_of_1990 "w:Portuguese Language Orthographic Agreement of 1990").

[superstratum](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/superstratum "superstratum")

A language [stratum](#stratum) which has higher cultural or political prestige than the one which it influences. One historical example is the **superstrate** effect of [Old Norman](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Old_Norman "Old Norman") French on late [Old English](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Old_English "Old English") in the centuries following the 11th-century [Norman conquest of England](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England "w:Norman conquest of England").

[supine](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/supine "supine")

A term for an [infinite](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/infinite "infinite") verb form in some languages. In Latin, a type of [verbal noun](#verbal_noun), used for the [ablative](#ablative) and [accusative](#accusative) case of an [infinitive](#infinitive). In Swedish, a form related to the [past participle](#past_participle), used to form [perfect](#perfect) tenses. In Slovene and Lower Sorbian, a form related to the infinitive, used to indicate purpose after a verb of movement.

[suppletion](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/suppletion "suppletion"), [suppletive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/suppletive "suppletive")

The situation in which the inflected forms of a word come from two or more unrelated roots: for example, *[go](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/go#English "go")* and *[went](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/went#English "went")*; *[be](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/be#English "be")*, *[is](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/is#English "is")*, and *[was](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/was#English "was")*. One or more of these forms, or the entire [paradigm](#paradigm) of the word, may then be called suppletive. Examples from various languages may be found from [Category:Suppletive verbs by language](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Category:Suppletive_verbs_by_language "Category:Suppletive verbs by language").

[surface analysis](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/surface_analysis "surface analysis"), [surface etymology](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/surface_etymology "surface etymology")

The etymology of a term by an [analysis](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/analysis "analysis") based on components occurring in the term's language, regardless of their apparentness or [productivity](#productive), i.e. that term's [synchronic](#synchronic) makeup: for example, *[earth](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/earth#English "earth")* + *[\-en](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-en#English "-en")* for *[earthen](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/earthen#English "earthen")* (actually inherited via Middle English from Old English) and *[bio-](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bio-#English "bio-")* + *[\-logy](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-logy#English "-logy")* for *[biology](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/biology#English "biology")* (actually borrowed from New Latin). Some surface analyses describe actual formations of words actively created by speakers, who might not know their historical origin.  
Sound changes do not invalidate a surface analysis if they are precedented or regular in the term's language and the relevant components are recognizable in the term, such as *[circle](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/circle#English "circle")* + *[\-ar](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-ar#English "-ar")* for *[circular](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/circular#English "circular")* and *[\-able](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-able#English "-able")* + *[\-ity](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-ity#English "-ity")* for *[\-ability](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-ability#English "-ability")* but not *[chuva](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/chuva#Portuguese "chuva")* + *[\-ial](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-ial#Portuguese "-ial")* for *[pluvial](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pluvial#Portuguese "pluvial")*, since the change from /pl/ to /ʃ/ makes *[chuva](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/chuva#Portuguese "chuva")* unrecognizable.

[syllable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/syllable "syllable")

A unit of human speech that is interpreted by the listener as a single sound.

[synchrony](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/synchrony "synchrony")

The viewpoint of analysis of a language which considers only its state at one point (or period) in time, not accounting for historical language change or etymology (as opposed to [diachrony](#diachrony)). A purely **synchronic** analysis of a word or phrase (as e.g. from the intuition of a speaker of the language) which may differ from its true etymological derivation is termed a [surface analysis](#surface_analysis).

[syncope](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/syncope "syncope")

The deletion or elision of sounds inside a word (not at the beginning or end), most often a single [vowel](#vowel), but sometimes a [consonant](#consonant) or a sequence of vowels or consonants. Deletion at the beginning of a word is called [apheresis](#apheresis), at the end [apocope](#apocope).

[syncretism](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/syncretism "syncretism")

The situation in which two or more [inflected](#inflection) forms of a word are identical. For example, English *[walked](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/walked#English "walked")* is both the simple past and the past participle of *[walk](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/walk#English "walk")*, and Ancient Greek *[ἄλλο](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%84%CE%BB%CE%BB%CE%BF#Ancient_Greek "ἄλλο")* (állo) is the neuter nominative, accusative, and vocative singular of *[ἄλλος](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%84%CE%BB%CE%BB%CE%BF%CF%82#Ancient_Greek "ἄλλος")* (állos).

[synecdoche](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/synecdoche "synecdoche")

A [figure of speech](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/figure_of_speech "figure of speech") that uses the name of a part of something to represent the whole, or the whole to represent a part. Compare [metonym](#metonym), which overlaps substantially in [denotation](#denotation).

[syllabic abbreviation](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/syllabic_abbreviation "syllabic abbreviation")

A pronounceable [abbreviation](#abbreviation), similar to an [acronym](#acronym) but formed from syllables (rather than letters) of the underlying phrase. Examples in English are the *[SoHo](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/SoHo#English "SoHo")* neighborhood in New York, formed from ***So** uth of **Ho** uston* (Street), and the country grouping *[Benelux](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Benelux#English "Benelux")*, formed from ***Be** lgium, **Ne** therlands, **Lux** embourg*. Syllabic acronyms are particularly common in certain languages, such as Russian (e.g. *[комсомо́л](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%BA%D0%BE%D0%BC%D1%81%D0%BE%D0%BC%D0%BE%D0%BB#Russian "комсомол")* (komsomól, “ [Komsomol](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Komsomol "Komsomol"), [Young Communist League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Young_Communist_League "w:Young Communist League") ”) from *[**Ком** мунисти́ческий](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%BA%D0%BE%D0%BC%D0%BC%D1%83%D0%BD%D0%B8%D1%81%D1%82%D0%B8%D1%87%D0%B5%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B8%D0%B9#Russian "коммунистический") [**со** ю́з](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D1%81%D0%BE%D1%8E%D0%B7#Russian "союз") [**мол** одёжи](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%BC%D0%BE%D0%BB%D0%BE%D0%B4%D1%91%D0%B6%D1%8C#Russian "молодёжь")* (**Kom** munistíčeskij **so** júz **mol** odjóži)), and Indonesian. Sometimes multiple syllables may be taken from a single word, as in *[универма́г](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D1%83%D0%BD%D0%B8%D0%B2%D0%B5%D1%80%D0%BC%D0%B0%D0%B3#Russian "универмаг")* (univermág, “department store”) from *[**универ** са́льный](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D1%83%D0%BD%D0%B8%D0%B2%D0%B5%D1%80%D1%81%D0%B0%D0%BB%D1%8C%D0%BD%D1%8B%D0%B9#Russian "универсальный") [**маг** ази́н](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%BC%D0%B0%D0%B3%D0%B0%D0%B7%D0%B8%D0%BD#Russian "магазин")* (**univer** sálʹnyj **mag** azín, literally “universal store”). Indonesian syllabic acronyms are often formed from syllables other than the initial one of a word, such as *[balon](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/balon#Indonesian "balon")* (“prospective candidate”) from *[**ba** kal](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bakal "bakal") [ca **lon**](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/calon "calon")*, *[distan](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/distan#Indonesian "distan")* (“department of agriculture”) from *[**di** na **s**](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dinas "dinas") [per **tan** ian](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pertanian "pertanian")* or *[kapolsek](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/kapolsek#Indonesian "kapolsek")* (“chief of police”) from *[**k** epal **a**](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/kepala "kepala") [ke **pol** isian](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/kepolisian "kepolisian") [**sek** tor](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sektor "sektor")*. Abbreviations formed partly from letters and partly from syllables, such as *[гула́г](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%B3%D1%83%D0%BB%D0%B0%D0%B3#Russian "гулаг")* (gulág, “ [gulag](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gulag "gulag") ”) from *[**Г** ла́вное](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%B3%D0%BB%D0%B0%D0%B2%D0%BD%D1%8B%D0%B9#Russian "главный") [**у** правле́ние](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D1%83%D0%BF%D1%80%D0%B0%D0%B2%D0%BB%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B8%D0%B5#Russian "управление") [исправи́тельно](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%B8%D1%81%D0%BF%D1%80%D0%B0%D0%B2%D0%B8%D1%82%D0%B5%D0%BB%D1%8C%D0%BD%D1%8B%D0%B9#Russian "исправительный") - [трудовы́х](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D1%82%D1%80%D1%83%D0%B4%D0%BE%D0%B2%D0%BE%D0%B9#Russian "трудовой") [**лаг** ере́й](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%BB%D0%B0%D0%B3%D0%B5%D1%80%D1%8C#Russian "лагерь")* (**G** lávnoje **u** pravlénije ispravítelʹno-trudovýx **lag** jeréj, literally “Chief Administration of Corrective-Labor Camps”), are generally counted as regular acronyms rather than syllabic abbreviations.

[synonym](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/synonym "synonym")

A word or phrase with a meaning that is the same as, or very similar to, another word or phrase.

Contrast [antonym](#antonym).

## T

*[تَصْحِيف](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D8%B5%D8%AD%D9%81#Arabic "صحف")* (taṣḥīf)

When a copyist has set wrong points ([ʾiʿjām](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/i%27jam "i'jam")) upon the skeleton ([rasm](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rasm "rasm")) of the (Arabic) script.

[tatpurusa](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tatpurusa "tatpurusa")

Another term for a *[determinative](#determinative) compound*.

Contrast [bahuvrihi](#bahuvrihi), [karmadharaya](#karmadharaya) and [dvandva](#dvandva).

[tautology](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tautology "tautology")

Redundant use of words, a [pleonasm](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pleonasm "pleonasm"), an unnecessary and tedious repetition.

[technical](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/technical "technical")

(1) Specifically related to a particular [discipline](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/discipline#English:_specific_branch_of_knowledge_or_practice "discipline"), either (a) exclusively so or (often) (b) with a somewhat [stricter](#strict) sense when used in that way (for example, dependent on which [operational definition](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/operational_definition "operational definition") is being used); (2) Of or related to [technology](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/technology#English "technology").

[telic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/telic "telic")

The [aspect](#aspect) of a verb that denotes an action with a definite endpoint or a goal that is tended towards, or rather an action that is [quantized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantization_\(linguistics\) "w:Quantization (linguistics)") (such that the expression for that action may not describe both the action and a smaller part of that same action); contrast **[atelic](#atelic)**. A kind of telicity distinction can be seen in English when specifying a duration in a (simple past) verb phrase: telic verb phrases take *[in](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/in#English "in")* (*I built a house in an hour.* *Did you just lose two keys in one day?*)

[tense](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tense "tense")

One of the forms of a [verb](#verb), used to distinguish when an action or state of being occurs or exists. The basic tenses in many languages are [present](#present_tense), [past](#past_tense), [future](#future_tense).

[thematicization](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/thematicization "thematicization")

[Insertion](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/insertion "insertion") of a [thematic vowel](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/thematic_vowel "thematic vowel") on the [root](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/root "root") or [stem](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/stem "stem") of the word to make it [undergo](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/undergo "undergo") one of the [productive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/productive "productive") [vocalic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vocalic "vocalic") inflections.

[third person](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/third_person "third person"), [3rd person](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/3rd_person "3rd person")

A [grammatical person](#person) that indicates someone or something that is neither the person or group to which the speaker belongs, nor the person or group that the speaker is speaking to. Examples are the English pronouns *[he](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/he#English "he")*, *[she](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/she#English "she")*, *[it](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/it#English "it")*, *[this](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/this#English "this")*, *[that](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/that#English "that")*, and so on. All [nouns](#noun) are generally considered third person. In some languages (like German, Italian, Spanish, Portuguese, and Hungarian), the third-person conjugation is also used to express the formal *you* (sometimes combined with the plural and/or capitalizing the personal pronoun in writing).

[tmesis](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tmesis "tmesis")

The insertion of one or more words between the components of a [compound](#compound) word. It may produce [prothesis](#prothesis); for example, inserting *[whole](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/whole#English "whole")* into *[another](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/another#English "another")* yields *[a whole nother](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/a_whole_nother#English "a whole nother")* (tmesis), in which *[nother](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nother#Adjective_2 "nother")* is [prothetic](#prothesis).

[tone](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tone "tone")

The pitch of a given syllable in languages where changing the pitch changes the basic meaning of the word. In [Mandarin Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese "w:Mandarin Chinese"), for example, the word pronounced /ma/ (similar to English *[ma](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ma#English "ma")*), when pronounced with a high level tone means “mother”, but when pronounced with a rising tone means “bother”, and when pronounced with a falling tone means “scold”.

topic state

In Luganda, the form of a [noun](#noun) in which there is an [initial vowel](#initial_vowel).

Contrast *[base state](#base_state)*.

[toponym](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/toponym "toponym")

A placename, or a word derived from one.

[topicalized](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/topicalized "topicalized") form

In some languages, such as Okinawan, a topicalized form of a word is a contraction of that word, used as the phrase [topic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/topic "topic"), with the topic marker.

tr., tran., transl.

Translator or translated, often used in quotations.

[transferred sense](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/transferred_sense "transferred sense"), transf.

A (usually [looser](#loosely)) meaning of a word or [phrase](#phrase) developed from a [metaphorical](#metaphorical) application of its original signification (for example, *hunger* has the primary and original sense “want of food”, “craving appetite”, whence developed the transferred sense of “any strong desire or craving”).

[transgressive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/transgressive "transgressive")

A verb form in some Balto-Slavic languages that expresses a coincidentally proceeding or following action.

[transitive verb](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/transitive_verb "transitive verb")

A verb which requires one or more [objects](#object) (e.g. *I **kick** the ball*); contrast **[intransitive verb](#intransitive_verb)**.

translation hub

An English multi-word entry that is unidiomatic ([sum of parts](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Wiktionary:Glossary#S "Wiktionary:Glossary")) and exists purely to host translations and enable navigation from one non-English entry to another non-English entry. An example: [two days after tomorrow](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/two_days_after_tomorrow "two days after tomorrow"). See [Category:English translation hubs](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Category:English_translation_hubs "Category:English translation hubs") for more.

[transliteration](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Wiktionary:Transliteration "Wiktionary:Transliteration")

The conversion of text in one [script](#script) into an equivalent in another [script](#script), or an instance of text so converted (for example, *Ševčenko* and *Shevchenko* are transliterations of Ukrainian *Шевченко* from Cyrillic script to Latin script, according to two different systems).

Transliteration is conducted on a letter-by-letter basis (contrasted with [transcription](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/transcription "transcription") which is converted on the basis of sounds). This may be extended to the conversion of [diacritical](#diacritic) marks into alternate forms without diacritical marks in the same script (e.g. *Mörder* → *Moerder*).

[transnumeral](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/transnumeral "transnumeral")

A [grammatical number](#number) that is ambiguous as to whether it refers to the [singular](#singular) or [plural](#plural). Found in [Sino-Tibetan](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Sino-Tibetan "Sino-Tibetan") languages such as [Chinese](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Chinese "Chinese") and [Japanese](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Japanese "Japanese"), as well as some [Austronesian](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Austronesian "Austronesian") languages such as [Indonesian](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Indonesian "Indonesian") and [Malay](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Malay "Malay"). When a language also has a plural (or some other) number, they are commonly only used for clarity or emphasis.

[troponym](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/troponym "troponym")

A verb that indicates more precisely the manner of doing something by replacing a verb of a more generalized meaning, e.g. “to boil” for “to cook”.

UK

UK English, that is, the English of the [United Kingdom](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom").

[umlaut](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/umlaut "umlaut")

A [sound change](#sound_change) where a [vowel](#vowel) is modified to conform more closely to the vowel in the next [syllable](#syllable) (often specifically [fronted](#front_vowel) or [raised](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/raise "raise") in response to an /i/ or /j/ in the next syllable). Also, an alternative term for *[diaeresis](#diaeresis)*, describing a [diacritic](#diacritic) mark ( ̈) placed over a vowel to indicate that it has undergone the umlaut [sound change](#sound_change).

[unadapted](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/unadapted "unadapted") [borrowing](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/borrowing "borrowing")

A [loanword](#loanword) that has not been conformed to the morpho-syntactic, orthographic, phonological, and/or phonotactical rules of the target language. For example, English *[cubiculum](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cubiculum#English "cubiculum")* is an unadapted borrowing from Latin *[cubiculum](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cubiculum#Latin "cubiculum")*, while English *[cubicle](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cubicle#English "cubicle")* is a standard borrowing from the same Latin word. Unadapted borrowings are often learned ones; see *[learned borrowing](#learned_borrowing)*.

Contrast [adapted borrowings](#adapted_borrowing).

[uncommon](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/uncommon "uncommon")

A term or sense that is attested but not used commonly either in spoken or written language, but more commonly than [rare](#rare) terms. In comparison to a rare term, an uncommon term may be easy to find in a deliberate search, but is very unlikely to be encountered naturally. Uncommon [slang](#slang) or [jargon](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/jargon "jargon") is unlikely to be used even by members of the relevant subcommunity or specialists in the relevant field.

uncomparable

*See **[incomparable](#incomparable)**.*

[uncountable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/uncountable "uncountable"), uncountable noun, [mass noun](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mass_noun "mass noun")

A noun that cannot be used freely with numbers or the [indefinite article](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/indefinite_article "indefinite article"), and which therefore usually takes no [plural](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/plural "plural") form. For example, the English noun *[information](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/information#English "information")* is a mass noun, and at least in its principal senses is uncountable in most varieties of English. For those senses, we cannot say that we have \* *one information*, nor that we have \* *many information* (or \* *many informations*). Many mass noun senses often have corresponding plural [count](#countable) senses that denote *types* of the mass sense, *instances* of the mass sense, or *portions (servings)* of the mass sense. For example, the main sense of *[butter](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/butter#English "butter")* is the uncountable sense, so the plural form *butters* is seldom used, although it occasionally is used to mean "types of butter" (*many [herb butters](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/herb_butter "herb butter") contain garlic*) or "\[packets of\] butter". Compare also other implicit references to a container and the portion/serving that it contains (*get me a water, order two sodas, have a few beers*). Many languages do not distinguish between countable and uncountable nouns. [Antonym](#antonym) of *[countable](#countable)* or *count noun*.

[undeclinable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/undeclinable "undeclinable")

*See **[indeclinable](#indeclinable)**.*

[unidirectional](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/unidirectional "unidirectional") [verb](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verb "verb")

In the [Slavic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavic_languages "w:Slavic languages"), a verb of motion whose motion is unidirectional and expresses a single, completed action. Opposed to [multidirectional verbs](#multidirectional_verb), whose motion is multidirectional or indirect, or whose action is repeated or in a series ([iterative](#iterative)). Also called a [concrete verb](#concrete_verb) or [determinate verb](#determinate_verb). See *[multidirectional verb](#multidirectional_verb)* for more discussion.

[univerbation](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/univerbation "univerbation")

A single word formed from a fixed expression of several words. For example, the single word *albeit* comes from the Middle English expression *al be it*, in which *al* means *although*.

## V

*v*

*See **[verb](#verb)**.*

[varia lectio](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/varia_lectio "varia lectio"), [variant reading](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/variant_reading "variant reading"), [variant](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/variant "variant")

Any one of the readings of a given word or passage in a text which differ from copy to copy, from edition to edition, from manuscript to manuscript, or from translation to translation.

[variety](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/variety#English:_language_variety,_in_linguistics "variety")

A form of a language that may be specific to a region, subpopulation, or other delimitation; in Wiktionary's use of this term, synonymous with [dialect](#dialectal) and [lect](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lect#Noun "lect").

[velar](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/velar "velar")

A [consonant](#consonant) made with the tongue touching the [soft palate](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/soft_palate "soft palate") (also known as the [velum](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/velum "velum")). In English these include /k/, /ɡ/, /ŋ/, as in the final consonants of *sack*, *sag*, and *sang*, respectively.

[verb](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verb "verb")

A word that indicates an action, occurrence or state of being. The [inflection](#inflection) of verbs is commonly called [conjugation](#conjugation).

[verbal noun](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verbal_noun "verbal noun")

A [noun](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/noun "noun") formed from a [verb](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verb "verb").

[verlan](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verlan "verlan")

A type of [backslang](#backslang) used in French, in which the order of the [syllables](#syllable) or sounds of words is changed, usually with the last [syllable](#syllable) coming first. Examples are *[barjot](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/barjot#French "barjot")* from *[jobard](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/jobard#French "jobard")* (“crazy”) and *[meuf](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/meuf#French "meuf")* from *[femme](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/femme#French "femme")* (“woman”). Sometimes this transformation is applied recursively, e.g. *[rebeu](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rebeu#French "rebeu")*, a verlan form of *[beur](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/beur#French "beur")*, which is itself a clipped verlan form of *[arabe](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/arabe#French "arabe")*.

[virile](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/virile "virile")

In Slavic languages, a plural [gender](#gender) used for groups that include men and for [masculine](#masculine) personal nouns.

[vocative case](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vocative_case "vocative case")

A [case](#case) which indicates that someone or something is being directly addressed (spoken to), often by name. For example, in the English phrase *He's dead, Jim* the name *[Jim](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Jim#English "Jim")* would be a vocative.

[voice](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/voice "voice")

A verb characteristic (expressed in some languages by inflection) indicating its relationship with the subject. The usual voices are: [active](#active_voice), [passive](#passive_voice) and [middle](#middle_voice).

[voiced](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/voiced "voiced")

A sound produced with vibration of the [vocal cords](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vocal_cord "vocal cord"); a type of [voicing](#voicing).

[voiceless](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/voiceless "voiceless")

A sound produced without vibration of the [vocal cords](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vocal_cord "vocal cord"); a type of [voicing](#voicing).

[voicing](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/voicing "voicing")

A characteristic of sounds, indicating whether they are produced with vibration of the [vocal cords](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vocal_cord "vocal cord"). In English, all [vowels](#vowel) are voiced, as well as all [approximant](#approximant) consonants, but [plosive](#plosive) and [fricative](#fricative) consonants can be either voiced or voiceless. Examples of voiced sounds in English are /v/, /z/, /b/, /d/, and the corresponding voiceless sounds are /f/, /s/, /p/, /t/.

[vowel](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vowel "vowel")

A sound produced by the vocal cords with relatively little restriction of the oral cavity, forming the prominent sound of a [syllable](#syllable).

[vowel harmony](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vowel_harmony "vowel harmony")

A [phonological](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/phonological "phonological") restriction in some languages which constrains the [vowels](#vowel) found either next to each other or within a word, such that vowels must belong to the same class in a particular classification, like front/back vowels or rounded/unrounded vowels.

[vulgar](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vulgar "vulgar")

Language considered distasteful, obscene, profane, or otherwise unsuitable for use in polite or formal speech. This type of language is avoided in children's media and can be used in colloquial language.

Sometimes, a [dialectal](#dialectal) or [vernacular](#vernacular) language, one that is spoken in a region as a [first language](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/first_language "first language"). But also, instead, a language used commonly or popularly as a [second language](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/second_language "second language") (See *[Vulgate](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Vulgate "Vulgate")*). Specifically in the context of Latin, the European languages that developed in the late middle ages and early modern period, on the expense of Latin.

See also: **[offensive](#offensive)**, **[pejorative](#pejorative)**, **[profanity](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/profanity "profanity")**.

## W

[Wanderwort](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Wanderwort "Wanderwort") or [wanderword](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/wanderword "wanderword")

A wordform which has spread over a substantial area, or to many regions, outside of that of its language of origin, typically due to cultural exchange resulting from travel and trade. Wanderworts are a type of [loanword](#loanword), but a Wanderwort may or may not be an [areal](#areal) word. See also *[Kulturwort](#Kulturwort)*.

Contrast [strata](#stratum).

[weak declension](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/weak_declension#English "weak declension")

A declension of adjectives and nouns in several [Germanic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germanic_languages "w:Germanic languages"), which originally had (and in some languages still have) an *\-n-* in most of their forms. The weak adjective declension is used in conjunction with [definite](#definite) [articles](#article). The weak noun declension is simply one of several possible noun declensions, so named because it uses the same endings as weak adjectives. See the Wikipedia article on the [weak inflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/weak_inflection "w:weak inflection") for more information.

[weak pronoun](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/weak_pronoun#English "weak pronoun")

A pronoun of one syllable which is dependent on another word and cannot be used on its own; sometimes called *[clitic](#clitic)*. Compare with [emphatic](#emphatic) or [strong](#strong_verb).

[weak verb](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/weak_verb "weak verb") and weak conjugation

In [Germanic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Germanic "Germanic") languages, a verb that forms the past tense using a suffix containing a [dental](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dental "dental") consonant (*d*, *t*, *ð* or similar). Verbs of this type are considered "regular" in most Germanic languages, but there are also irregular weak verbs, such as English *[think](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/think#English "think")*, *[thought](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/thought#English "thought")* and *[have](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/have#English "have")*, *[had](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/had#English "had")*.

Contrast [strong verb](#strong_verb), [preterite-present verb](#preterite-present_verb).

[women](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/women "women") 's [speech](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/speech "speech")

In certain languages (for example, [Karajá language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karaj%C3%A1_language "w:Karajá language")), men and women use or historically used distinct words and inflected forms.

Compare [men's speech](#male_speech).
