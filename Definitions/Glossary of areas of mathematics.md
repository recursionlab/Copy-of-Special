[Mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") is a broad subject that is commonly divided in many **areas** or **branches** that may be defined by [their objects of study](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_mathematical_objects "List of mathematical objects"), by the used methods, or by both. For example, [analytic number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_number_theory "Analytic number theory") is a subarea of [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory") devoted to the use of methods of [analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_analysis "Mathematical analysis") for the study of [natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number").

This glossary is alphabetically sorted. This hides a large part of the relationships between areas. For the broadest areas of mathematics, see [Mathematics § Areas of mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics#Areas_of_mathematics "Mathematics"). The [Mathematics Subject Classification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics_Subject_Classification "Mathematics Subject Classification") is a hierarchical list of areas and subjects of study that has been elaborated by the community of mathematicians. It is used by most publishers for classifying mathematical articles and books.

## A

[Absolute differential calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus "Ricci calculus")

An older name of [Ricci calculus](#ricci_calculus)

[Absolute geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_geometry "Absolute geometry")

Also called [neutral geometry](#neutral_geometry),[^1] a [synthetic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthetic_geometry "Synthetic geometry") similar to [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry") but without the [parallel postulate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_postulate "Parallel postulate").[^2]

[Abstract algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_algebra "Abstract algebra")

The part of [algebra](#algebra) devoted to the study of [algebraic structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_structure "Algebraic structure") in themselves.[^3] Occasionally named [modern algebra](#modern_algebra) in course titles.

[Abstract analytic number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_analytic_number_theory "Abstract analytic number theory")

The study of [arithmetic semigroups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_analytic_number_theory "Abstract analytic number theory") as a means to extend notions from [classical analytic number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_number_theory "Analytic number theory").[^4]

Abstract differential geometry

A form of [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry") without the notion of [smoothness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smoothness "Smoothness") from [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus"). Instead it is built using [sheaf theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheaf_theory "Sheaf theory") and [sheaf cohomology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheaf_cohomology "Sheaf cohomology").

[Abstract harmonic analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_harmonic_analysis "Abstract harmonic analysis")

A modern branch of [harmonic analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_analysis "Harmonic analysis") that extends upon the generalized [Fourier transforms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_transform "Fourier transform") that can be defined on [locally compact groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Locally_compact_group "Locally compact group").

[Abstract homotopy theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy_theory "Homotopy theory")

A part of [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") that deals with homotopic functions, i.e. functions from one topological space to another which are homotopic (the functions can be deformed into one another).

[Actuarial science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Actuarial_science "Actuarial science")

The discipline that applies [mathematical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") and [statistical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics "Statistics") methods to [assess risk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Risk_assessment "Risk assessment") in [insurance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insurance "Insurance"), [finance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finance "Finance") and other industries and professions. More generally, actuaries apply rigorous mathematics to model matters of uncertainty.

[Additive combinatorics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_combinatorics "Additive combinatorics")

The part of [arithmetic combinatorics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_combinatorics "Arithmetic combinatorics") devoted to the operations of [addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition "Addition") and [subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction "Subtraction").

[Additive number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_number_theory "Additive number theory")

A part of [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory") that studies subsets of [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer") and their behaviour under addition.

[Affine geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_geometry "Affine geometry")

A branch of [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") that deals with properties that are independent from distances and angles, such as [alignment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collinearity "Collinearity") and [parallelism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_\(geometry\) "Parallel (geometry)").

[Affine geometry of curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_geometry_of_curves "Affine geometry of curves")

The study of [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve") properties that are invariant under [affine transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_transformation "Affine transformation").

[Affine differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_differential_geometry "Affine differential geometry")

A type of [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry") dedicated to differential [invariants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_\(mathematics\) "Invariant (mathematics)") under [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume "Volume") -preserving [affine transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_transformation "Affine transformation").

[Ahlfors theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahlfors_theory "Ahlfors theory")

A part of [complex analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analysis "Complex analysis") being the geometric counterpart of [Nevanlinna theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nevanlinna_theory "Nevanlinna theory"). It was invented by [Lars Ahlfors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lars_Ahlfors "Lars Ahlfors").

[Algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra "Algebra")

One of the major [areas of mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Areas_of_mathematics "Areas of mathematics"). Roughly speaking, it is the art of manipulating and computing with [operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_\(mathematics\) "Operation (mathematics)") acting on symbols called [variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_\(mathematics\) "Variable (mathematics)") that represent indeterminate [numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number "Number") or other [mathematical objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_object "Mathematical object"), such as [vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_\(mathematics_and_physics\) "Vector (mathematics and physics)"), [matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_\(mathematics\) "Matrix (mathematics)"), or elements of [algebraic structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_structure "Algebraic structure").

[Algebraic analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_analysis "Algebraic analysis")

motivated by systems of [linear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_differential_equation "Linear differential equation") [partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equation "Partial differential equation"), it is a branch of [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry") and [algebraic topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_topology "Algebraic topology") that uses methods from [sheaf theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheaf_theory "Sheaf theory") and complex analysis, to study the properties and generalizations of [functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_\(mathematics\) "Function (mathematics)"). It was started by [Mikio Sato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikio_Sato "Mikio Sato").

[Algebraic combinatorics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_combinatorics "Algebraic combinatorics")

an area that employs methods of abstract algebra to problems of [combinatorics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combinatorics "Combinatorics"). It also refers to the application of methods from combinatorics to problems in abstract algebra.

[Algebraic computation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_computation "Algebraic computation")

An older name of [computer algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_algebra "Computer algebra").

[Algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry")

a branch that combines techniques from abstract algebra with the language and problems of geometry. Fundamentally, it studies [algebraic varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_varieties "Algebraic varieties").

[Algebraic graph theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_graph_theory "Algebraic graph theory")

a branch of [graph theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_theory "Graph theory") in which methods are taken from algebra and employed to problems about [graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_\(discrete_mathematics\) "Graph (discrete mathematics)"). The methods are commonly taken from [group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_theory "Group theory") and linear algebra.

[Algebraic K-theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_K-theory "Algebraic K-theory")

an important part of [homological algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homological_algebra "Homological algebra") concerned with defining and applying a certain sequence of [functors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functor "Functor") from [rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_\(mathematics\) "Ring (mathematics)") to [abelian groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group").

[Algebraic number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_number_theory "Algebraic number theory")

The part of [number theory](#number_theory) devoted to the use of algebraic methods, mainly those of [commutative algebra](#commutative_algebra), for the study of [number fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_field "Number field") and their [rings of integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rings_of_integers "Rings of integers").

[Algebraic statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_statistics "Algebraic statistics")

the use of algebra to advance [statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics "Statistics"), although the term is sometimes restricted to label the use of algebraic geometry and [commutative algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_algebra "Commutative algebra") in [statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics "Statistics").

[Algebraic topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_topology "Algebraic topology")

a branch that uses tools from [abstract algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_algebra "Abstract algebra") for [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") to study [topological spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space").

[Algorithmic number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algorithmic_number_theory "Algorithmic number theory")

also known as *computational number theory*, it is the study of [algorithms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algorithm "Algorithm") for performing [number theoretic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory") [computations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computation "Computation").

[Anabelian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anabelian_geometry "Anabelian geometry")

an area of study based on the theory proposed by [Alexander Grothendieck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Grothendieck "Alexander Grothendieck") in the 1980s that describes the way a geometric object of an [algebraic variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety "Algebraic variety") (such as an [algebraic fundamental group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_fundamental_group "Algebraic fundamental group")) can be mapped into another object, without it being an [abelian group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group").

[Analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_analysis "Mathematical analysis")

A wide area of mathematics centered on the study of [continuous functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function") and including such topics as [differentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiation_\(mathematics\) "Differentiation (mathematics)"), [integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integration_\(mathematics\) "Integration (mathematics)"), [limits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_\(mathematics\) "Limit (mathematics)"), and [series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Series_\(mathematics\) "Series (mathematics)").[^5]

[Analytic combinatorics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_combinatorics "Analytic combinatorics")

part of [enumerative combinatorics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enumerative_combinatorics "Enumerative combinatorics") where methods of complex analysis are applied to [generating functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generating_function "Generating function").

[Analytic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_geometry "Analytic geometry")

1\. Also known as [Cartesian geometry](#cartesian_geometry), the study of [Euclidean geometry](#euclidean_geometry) using [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates").

2\. Analogue to [differential geometry](#differential_geometry), where [differentiable functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_function "Differentiable function") are replaced with [analytic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_function "Analytic function"). It is a subarea of both [complex analysis](#complex_analysis) and [algebraic geometry](#algebraic_geometry).

[Analytic number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_number_theory "Analytic number theory")

An area of [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory") that applies methods from [mathematical analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_analysis "Mathematical analysis") to solve problems about [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer").[^6]

[Analytic theory of L-functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L-function "L-function")

[Applied mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Applied_mathematics "Applied mathematics")

a combination of various parts of mathematics that concern a variety of mathematical methods that can be applied to practical and theoretical problems. Typically the methods used are for [science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science "Science"), [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering"), [finance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finance "Finance"), [economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economics "Economics") and [logistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logistics "Logistics").

[Approximation theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Approximation_theory "Approximation theory")

part of [analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analysis "Analysis") that studies how well functions can be approximated by simpler ones (such as [polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial") or [trigonometric polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometric_polynomial "Trigonometric polynomial"))

[Arakelov geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arakelov_geometry "Arakelov geometry")

also known as *Arakelov theory*

[Arakelov theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arakelov_theory "Arakelov theory")

an approach to [Diophantine geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diophantine_geometry "Diophantine geometry") used to study [Diophantine equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diophantine_equation "Diophantine equation") in higher dimensions (using techniques from algebraic geometry). It is named after [Suren Arakelov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suren_Arakelov "Suren Arakelov").

[Arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic "Arithmetic")

1\. Also known as [elementary arithmetic](#elementary_arithmetic), the methods and rules for computing with [addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition "Addition"), [subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction "Subtraction"), [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication "Multiplication") and [division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_\(mathematics\) "Division (mathematics)") of numbers.

2\. Also known as [higher arithmetic](#higher_arithmetic), another name for [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory").

[Arithmetic algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_algebraic_geometry "Arithmetic algebraic geometry")

See [arithmetic geometry](#arithmetic_geometry).

[Arithmetic combinatorics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_combinatorics "Arithmetic combinatorics")

the study of the estimates from [combinatorics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combinatorics "Combinatorics") that are associated with [arithmetic operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_operation "Arithmetic operation") such as addition, [subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction "Subtraction"), [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication "Multiplication") and [division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_\(mathematics\) "Division (mathematics)").

[Arithmetic dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_dynamics "Arithmetic dynamics")

Arithmetic dynamics is the study of the number-theoretic properties of [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer_point "Integer point"), [rational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_point "Rational point"), p-adic, and/or algebraic points under repeated application of a [polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial") or [rational function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_function "Rational function"). A fundamental goal is to describe arithmetic properties in terms of underlying geometric structures.

[Arithmetic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry "Arithmetic geometry")

The use of [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry") and more specially [scheme theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scheme_theory "Scheme theory") for solving problems of number theory.

[Arithmetic topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_topology "Arithmetic topology")

a combination of [algebraic number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_number_theory "Algebraic number theory") and [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") studying analogies between [prime ideals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_ideal "Prime ideal") and [knots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_\(mathematics\) "Knot (mathematics)")

[Arithmetical algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetical_algebraic_geometry "Arithmetical algebraic geometry")

Another name for [arithmetic algebraic geometry](#arithmetic_algebraic_geometry)

[Asymptotic combinatorics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asymptotic_combinatorics "Asymptotic combinatorics")

It uses the internal structure of the objects to derive formulas for their [generating functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generating_function "Generating function") and then complex analysis techniques to get asymptotics.

[Asymptotic theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asymptotic_theory "Asymptotic theory")

the study of [asymptotic expansions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asymptotic_expansion "Asymptotic expansion")

[Auslander–Reiten theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auslander%E2%80%93Reiten_theory "Auslander–Reiten theory")

the study of the [representation theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representation_theory "Representation theory") of [Artinian rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artinian_ring "Artinian ring")

Axiomatic geometry

also known as *[synthetic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthetic_geometry "Synthetic geometry")*: it is a branch of geometry that uses [axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom "Axiom") and [logical arguments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_argument "Logical argument") to draw conclusions as opposed to [analytic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_geometry "Analytic geometry") and algebraic methods.

[Axiomatic set theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiomatic_set_theory "Axiomatic set theory")

the study of systems of [axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom "Axiom") in a context relevant to [set theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_theory "Set theory") and [mathematical logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_logic "Mathematical logic").

## B

[Bifurcation theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bifurcation_theory "Bifurcation theory")

the study of changes in the qualitative or topological structure of a given family. It is a part of [dynamical systems theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamical_systems_theory "Dynamical systems theory")

[Biostatistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biostatistics "Biostatistics")

the development and application of [statistical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical "Statistical") methods to a wide range of topics in [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology "Biology").

[Birational geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birational_geometry "Birational geometry")

a part of [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry") that deals with the geometry (of an algebraic variety) that is dependent only on its [function field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_field_of_an_algebraic_variety "Function field of an algebraic variety").

Bolyai–Lobachevskian geometry

see *[hyperbolic geometry](#hyperbolic_geometry)*

## C

[C\*-algebra theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C*-algebra "C*-algebra")

a [complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra_over_a_field "Algebra over a field") *A* of [continuous linear operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_linear_operator "Continuous linear operator") on a [complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") [Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space") with two additional properties-(i) *A* is a topologically [closed set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_set "Closed set") in the [norm topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_topology "Norm topology") of operators.(ii) *A* is closed under the operation of taking [adjoints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adjoint_of_an_operator "Adjoint of an operator") of operators.

[Cartesian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_geometry "Cartesian geometry")

see *analytic geometry*

[Calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus")

An area of mathematics connected by the [fundamental theorem of calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_calculus "Fundamental theorem of calculus").[^7]

[Calculus of infinitesimals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal_calculus "Infinitesimal calculus")

A foundation of [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus"), first developed in the 17th century,[^8] that makes use of [infinitesimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal "Infinitesimal") numbers.

[Calculus of moving surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus_of_moving_surfaces "Calculus of moving surfaces")

an extension of the theory of [tensor calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") to include deforming [manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold").

[Calculus of variations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus_of_variation "Calculus of variation")

the field dedicated to maximizing or minimizing [functionals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_\(mathematics\) "Functional (mathematics)"). It used to be called *functional calculus*.

[Catastrophe theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catastrophe_theory "Catastrophe theory")

a branch of [bifurcation theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bifurcation_theory "Bifurcation theory") from [dynamical systems theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamical_systems_theory "Dynamical systems theory"), and also a special case of the more general [singularity theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singularity_theory "Singularity theory") from geometry. It analyses the [germs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germ_\(mathematics\) "Germ (mathematics)") of the catastrophe geometries.

[Categorical logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Categorical_logic "Categorical logic")

a branch of [category theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_theory "Category theory") adjacent to the [mathematical logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_logic "Mathematical logic"). It is based on [type theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_theory "Type theory") for [intuitionistic logics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intuitionistic_logic "Intuitionistic logic").

[Category theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_theory "Category theory")

the study of the properties of particular mathematical concepts by formalising them as collections of objects and arrows.

[Chaos theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_theory "Chaos theory")

the study of the behaviour of [dynamical systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamical_system "Dynamical system") that are highly sensitive to their initial conditions.

[Character theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character_theory "Character theory")

a branch of [group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_theory "Group theory") that studies the characters of [group representations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_representation "Group representation") or [modular representations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modular_representation_theory "Modular representation theory").

[Class field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Class_field_theory "Class field theory")

a branch of [algebraic number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_number_theory "Algebraic number theory") that studies [abelian extensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_extension "Abelian extension") of [number fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_field "Number field").

[Classical differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry")

also known as [Euclidean differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"). see *Euclidean differential geometry*.

[Classical algebraic topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_topology "Algebraic topology")

see *algebraic topology*

[Classical analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_analysis "Classical analysis")

usually refers to the more traditional topics of analysis such as [real analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_analysis "Real analysis") and complex analysis. It includes any work that does not use techniques from [functional analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_analysis "Functional analysis") and is sometimes called *hard analysis*. However it may also refer to mathematical analysis done according to the principles of [classical mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Classical_mathematics&action=edit&redlink=1 "Classical mathematics (page does not exist)").

[Classical analytic number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_number_theory "Analytic number theory")

[Classical differential calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_calculus "Differential calculus")

[Classical Diophantine geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diophantine_geometry "Diophantine geometry")

[Classical Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry")

see *Euclidean geometry*

Classical geometry

may refer to [solid geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry "Solid geometry") or classical Euclidean geometry. See *geometry*

[Classical invariant theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_theory "Invariant theory")

## G

[Galois cohomology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galois_cohomology "Galois cohomology")

an application of [homological algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homological_algebra "Homological algebra"), it is the study of [group cohomology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_cohomology "Group cohomology") of [Galois modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galois_module "Galois module").

[Galois theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galois_theory "Galois theory")

named after [Évariste Galois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89variste_Galois "Évariste Galois"), it is a branch of abstract algebra providing a connection between [field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_theory_\(mathematics\) "Field theory (mathematics)") and [group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_theory "Group theory").

[Galois geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galois_geometry "Galois geometry")

a branch of [finite geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_geometry "Finite geometry") concerned with algebraic and [analytic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_geometry "Analytic geometry") geometry over a [Galois field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galois_field "Galois field").

[Game theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Game_theory "Game theory")

the study of [mathematical models](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_model "Mathematical model") of strategic interaction among rational decision-makers.

[Gauge theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauge_theory "Gauge theory")

[General topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_topology "General topology")

also known as *point-set topology*, it is a branch of [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") studying the properties of [topological spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space") and structures defined on them. It differs from other branches of [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") as the [topological spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space") do not have to be similar to manifolds.

[Generalized trigonometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_trigonometry "Generalized trigonometry")

developments of [trigonometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometry "Trigonometry") methods from the application to [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") of Euclidean geometry to any geometry or [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space "Space"). This includes [spherical trigonometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_trigonometry "Spherical trigonometry"), [hyperbolic trigonometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_geometry "Hyperbolic geometry"), [gyrotrigonometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gyrotrigonometry "Gyrotrigonometry"), and [universal hyperbolic trigonometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_trigonometry "Rational trigonometry").

[Geometric algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_algebra "Geometric algebra")

an alternative approach to classical, [computational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_geometry "Computational geometry") and [relativistic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity"). It shows a natural correspondence between geometric entities and elements of algebra.

[Geometric analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_analysis "Geometric analysis")

a discipline that uses methods from [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry") to study [partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equation "Partial differential equation") as well as the applications to geometry.

[Geometric calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_calculus "Geometric calculus")

extends the [geometric algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_algebra "Geometric algebra") to include [differentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") and [integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral").

[Geometric combinatorics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_combinatorics "Geometric combinatorics")

a branch of [combinatorics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combinatorics "Combinatorics"). It includes a number of subareas such as [polyhedral combinatorics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedral_combinatorics "Polyhedral combinatorics") (the study of [faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_\(geometry\) "Face (geometry)") of [convex polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polyhedron "Convex polyhedron")), [convex geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_geometry "Convex geometry") (the study of [convex sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_set "Convex set"), in particular combinatorics of their intersections), and [discrete geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_geometry "Discrete geometry"), which in turn has many applications to [computational geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_geometry "Computational geometry").

[Geometric function theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_function_theory "Geometric function theory")

the study of geometric properties of [analytic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_function "Analytic function").

[Geometric invariant theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_invariant_theory "Geometric invariant theory")

a method for constructing quotients by [group actions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_action_\(mathematics\) "Group action (mathematics)") in [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), used to construct [moduli spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moduli_space "Moduli space").

[Geometric graph theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_graph_theory "Geometric graph theory")

a large and amorphous subfield of [graph theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_theory "Graph theory"), concerned with graphs defined by geometric means.

[Geometric group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_group_theory "Geometric group theory")

the study of [finitely generated groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finitely_generated_group "Finitely generated group") via exploring the connections between algebraic properties of such groups and [topological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") and [geometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") properties of spaces on which these groups [act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_action_\(mathematics\) "Group action (mathematics)") (that is, when the groups in question are realized as geometric symmetries or continuous transformations of some spaces).

[Geometric measure theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_measure_theory "Geometric measure theory")

the study of [geometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") properties of [sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_\(mathematics\) "Set (mathematics)") (typically in [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space")) through [measure theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measure_\(mathematics\) "Measure (mathematics)").

[Geometric number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_number_theory "Geometric number theory")

[Geometric topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_topology "Geometric topology")

a branch of [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") studying manifolds and mappings between them; in particular the [embedding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embedding "Embedding") of one manifold into another.

[Geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry")

a branch of mathematics concerned with [shape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape "Shape") and the properties of [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_\(mathematics\) "Space (mathematics)"). Classically it arose as what is now known as [solid geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry "Solid geometry"); this was concerning practical knowledge of [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length "Length"), [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area") and [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume "Volume"). It was then put into an [axiomatic form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiomatic_system "Axiomatic system") by [Euclid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid "Euclid"), giving rise to what is now known as classical Euclidean geometry. The use of [coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate "Coordinate") by [René Descartes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes "René Descartes") gave rise to [Cartesian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_geometry "Cartesian geometry") enabling a more analytical approach to geometric entities. Since then many other branches have appeared including [projective geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_geometry "Projective geometry"), [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), [non-Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Euclidean_geometry "Non-Euclidean geometry"), [Fractal geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_geometry "Fractal geometry") and algebraic geometry. Geometry also gave rise to the modern discipline of [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology").

[Geometry of numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry_of_number "Geometry of number")

initiated by [Hermann Minkowski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski "Hermann Minkowski"), it is a branch of [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory") studying [convex bodies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_bodies "Convex bodies") and [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer") [vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector").

[Global analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_analysis "Global analysis")

the study of [differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equation "Differential equation") on manifolds and the relationship between [differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equation "Differential equation") and [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology").

[Global arithmetic dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_dynamics "Arithmetic dynamics")

[Graph theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_theory "Graph theory")

a branch of [discrete mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_mathematics "Discrete mathematics") devoted to the study of [graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_\(discrete_mathematics\) "Graph (discrete mathematics)"). It has many applications in [physical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_science "Physical science"), [biological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_science "Biological science") and [social](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_science "Social science") systems.

[Group-character theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character_theory "Character theory")

the part of character theory dedicated to the study of characters of [group representations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_representation "Group representation").

[Group representation theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_representation_theory "Group representation theory")

[Group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_theory "Group theory")

the study of [algebraic structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_structure "Algebraic structure") known as [groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_\(mathematics\) "Group (mathematics)").

[Gyrotrigonometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gyrotrigonometry "Gyrotrigonometry")

a form of [trigonometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometry "Trigonometry") used in [gyrovector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gyrovector_space "Gyrovector space") for [hyperbolic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_geometry "Hyperbolic geometry"). (An analogy of the [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") in Euclidean geometry.)

## K

[K-theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K-theory "K-theory")

originated as the study of a [ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_\(mathematics\) "Ring (mathematics)") generated by [vector bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundle "Vector bundle") over a [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space") or [scheme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scheme_\(mathematics\) "Scheme (mathematics)"). In algebraic topology it is an [extraordinary cohomology theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extraordinary_cohomology_theory "Extraordinary cohomology theory") known as [topological K-theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_K-theory "Topological K-theory"). In algebra and algebraic geometry it is referred to as [algebraic K-theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_K-theory "Algebraic K-theory"). In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_physics "Mathematical physics"), [K-theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K-theory_\(physics\) "K-theory (physics)") has appeared in [type II string theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_II_string_theory "Type II string theory"). (In particular [twisted K-theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twisted_K-theory "Twisted K-theory").)

[K-homology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K-homology "K-homology")

a [homology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homology_\(mathematics\) "Homology (mathematics)") theory on the [category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_\(mathematics\) "Category (mathematics)") of locally [compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_space "Compact space") [Hausdorff spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_space "Hausdorff space").

[Kähler geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%A4hler_manifold "Kähler manifold")

a branch of [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), more specifically a union of [Riemannian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_geometry "Riemannian geometry"), [complex differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry") and [symplectic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symplectic_geometry "Symplectic geometry"). It is the study of [Kähler manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%A4hler_manifold "Kähler manifold"). (named after [Erich Kähler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erich_K%C3%A4hler "Erich Kähler"))

[KK-theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KK-theory "KK-theory")

a common generalization both of [K-homology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K-homology "K-homology") and [K-theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operator_K-theory "Operator K-theory") as an additive [bivariant functor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functor#Bifunctors "Functor") on [separable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separable_space "Separable space") [C\*-algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C*-algebra "C*-algebra").

[Klein geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klein_geometry "Klein geometry")

More specifically, it is a [homogeneous space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_space "Homogeneous space") *X* together with a [transitive action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_action_\(mathematics\) "Group action (mathematics)") on *X* by a [Lie group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group") *G*, which acts as the [symmetry group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_group "Symmetry group") of the geometry.

[Knot theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_theory "Knot theory")

part of [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") dealing with [knots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_\(mathematics\) "Knot (mathematics)")

[Kummer theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kummer_theory "Kummer theory")

provides a description of certain types of [field extensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_extension "Field extension") involving the [adjunction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adjunction_\(field_theory\) "Adjunction (field theory)") of *n* th roots of elements of the base [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_\(mathematics\) "Field (mathematics)")

## R

[Ramsey theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ramsey_theory "Ramsey theory")

[Rational geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_geometry "Rational geometry")

[Real algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_algebraic_geometry "Real algebraic geometry")

[Real algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_algebraic_geometry "Real algebraic geometry")

the part of algebraic geometry that studies [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") points of the algebraic varieties.

[Real analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_analysis "Real analysis")

a branch of mathematical analysis; in particular *hard analysis*, that is the study of [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") and [functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_\(mathematics\) "Function (mathematics)") of [Real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") values. It provides a rigorous formulation of the calculus of [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") in terms of [continuity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function") and [smoothness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_function "Smooth function"), whilst the theory is extended to the [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") in [complex analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analysis "Complex analysis").

see *computability theory*

[Representation theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representation_theory "Representation theory")

a subfield of abstract algebra; it studies [algebraic structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_structure "Algebraic structure") by representing their elements as [linear transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_transformation "Linear transformation") of [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space"). It also studies [modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_\(mathematics\) "Module (mathematics)") over these algebraic structures, providing a way of reducing problems in abstract algebra to problems in linear algebra.

[Representation theory of groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_representation "Group representation")

[Ribbon theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ribbon_theory "Ribbon theory")

a branch of [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") studying [ribbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ribbon_\(mathematics\) "Ribbon (mathematics)").

[Ricci calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus "Ricci calculus")

A foundation of [tensor calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_calculus "Tensor calculus"), developed by [Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorio_Ricci-Curbastro "Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro") in 1887–1896,[^14] and later developed for its applications to [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") and [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry").[^15]

[Ring theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_theory "Ring theory")

[Riemannian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_geometry "Riemannian geometry")

a branch of [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry") that is more specifically, the study of [Riemannian manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold"). It is named after [Bernhard Riemann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernhard_Riemann "Bernhard Riemann") and it features many generalizations of concepts from Euclidean geometry, analysis and calculus.
