## C

[categorical variable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/categorical_variable "categorical variable")

A nominal variable.

[conditional distribution](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=conditional_distribution&action=edit&redlink=1 "conditional distribution (page does not exist)")

Given two jointly distributed random variables *X* and *Y*, the conditional probability distribution of *Y* given *X* (written " *Y* | *X* ") is the probability distribution of *Y* when *X* is known to be a particular value.

[conditional probability](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/conditional_probability "conditional probability")

the probability of some event A, assuming event B. Conditional probability is written P(*A* | *B*), and is read "the probability of *A*, given *B* ".

[completeness](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/completeness "completeness")

?

correlation coefficient

correlation.

[correlation](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/correlation "correlation")

A numeric measure of the strength of linear relationship between two random variables (one can use it to quantify, for example, how shoe size and height are correlated in the population). An example is the [Pearson product-moment correlation coefficient](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=Pearson_product-moment_correlation_coefficient&action=edit&redlink=1 "Pearson product-moment correlation coefficient (page does not exist)"), which is found by dividing the covariance of the two variables by the product of their standard deviations. Independent variables have a correlation of 0. Synonym: correlation coefficient.

[covariance](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/covariance "covariance")

between two random variables *X* and *Y*, with expected values ${\displaystyle E(X)=\mu }$ and ${\displaystyle E(Y)=\nu }$ is defined as the expected value of random variable ${\displaystyle (X-\mu )(Y-\nu )}$, and is written ${\displaystyle \operatorname {cov} (X,Y)}$. It is used for measuring correlation.

[credence](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/credence "credence")

A subjective estimate of probability.

## D

[data set](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/data_set "data set")

A sample and the associated *data points*.

[data point](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/data_point "data point")

A typed measurement - it can be a [Boolean](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Boolean "Boolean") value, a real number, a vector (in which case it's also called a data vector), etc.

[distribution function](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/distribution_function "distribution function")

The function that gives the probability distribution of a random variable. It cannot be negative, and its [integral](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/integral "integral") on the probability space is equal to 1.

[elementary event](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=elementary_event&action=edit&redlink=1 "elementary event (page does not exist)")

(or atomic event) is an event with only one element. For example, when pulling a card out of a deck, "getting the jack of spades" is an elementary event, while "getting a king or an ace" is not.

[estimator](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/estimator "estimator")

A function of the known data that is used to estimate an unknown parameter; an estimate is the result from the actual application of the function to a particular set of data. The mean can be used as an estimator.

[expected value](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/expected_value "expected value")

(or expectation) of a random variable is the sum of the probability of each possible outcome of the experiment multiplied by its payoff ("value"). Thus, it represents the average amount one "expects" to win per bet if bets with identical odds are repeated many times. For example, the expected value of a six-sided die roll is 3.5. The concept is similar to the mean. The expected value of random variable *X* is typically written E(X) or ${\displaystyle \mu }$ ([mu](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=Mu_\(letter\)&action=edit&redlink=1 "Mu (letter) (page does not exist)")).

[experiment](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/experiment "experiment")

[event](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/event "event")

A subset of the sample space, to which a probability can be assigned. For example, on rolling a die, "getting a five or a six" is an event (with a probability of one third if the die is fair).

## I

[independence](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/independence "independence") or [statistical independence](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=statistical_independence&action=edit&redlink=1 "statistical independence (page does not exist)")

Two events are independent if the outcome of one does not affect that of the other (for example, getting a 1 on one die roll does not affect the probability of getting a 1 on a second roll). Similarly, when we assert that two random variables are independent, we intuitively mean that knowing something about the value of one of them does not yield any information about the value of the other.

[interval variable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/interval_variable "interval variable")

A variable with the features of ordinal variable, such that equal differences between its values represent equivalent intervals.

## J

[joint distribution](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=joint_distribution&action=edit&redlink=1 "joint distribution (page does not exist)")

Given two random variables *X* and *Y*, the joint distribution of *X* and *Y* is the probability distribution of X and Y together.

[joint probability](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/joint_probability "joint probability")

the probability of two events occurring together. The joint probability of *A* and *B* is written ${\displaystyle P(A\cap B)}$ or ${\displaystyle P(A,\ B).}$

## K

[kurtosis](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/kurtosis "kurtosis")

A measure of the "peakedness" of the probability distribution of a real-valued random variable. Higher kurtosis means more of the variance is due to infrequent extreme deviations, as opposed to frequent modestly-sized deviations.

## L

[likelihood function](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=likelihood_function&action=edit&redlink=1 "likelihood function (page does not exist)")

(or just likelihood) is a conditional probability function considered a function of its second argument with its first argument held fixed. For example, imagine pulling a numbered ball with the number k from a bag of n balls, numbered 1 to n. Then you could describe a likelihood function for the random variable N as the probability of getting k given that there are n balls: the likelihood will be 1/n for n greater or equal to k, and 0 for n smaller than k. Unlike a probability distribution function, this likelihood function will not sum up to 1 on the sample space.

## M

[marginal distribution](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/marginal_distribution "marginal distribution")

given two jointly distributed random variables *X* and *Y*, the marginal distribution of *X* is simply the probability distribution of *X* ignoring information about *Y*.

[marginal probability](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=marginal_probability&action=edit&redlink=1 "marginal probability (page does not exist)")

The probability of an event, ignoring any information about other events. The marginal probability of *A* is written *P* (*A*). Contrast with conditional probability.

[mean](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mean "mean")

Of a random variable is its expected value. The mean (or sample mean of a data set is just the average value.

[moment about the mean](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=moment_about_the_mean&action=edit&redlink=1 "moment about the mean (page does not exist)")

[mutual independence](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=mutual_independence&action=edit&redlink=1 "mutual independence (page does not exist)")

A collection of events is mutually independent if for any subset of the collection, the joint probability of all events occurring is equal to the product of the joint probabilities of the individual events. Think of the result of a series of coin-flips. This is a stronger condition than pairwise independence.

[median](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/median "median")

placing the numbers you are given in value order and find the middle number.

[probability density](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/probability_density "probability density")

Is used to describe probability in a continuous probability distribution. For example, you can't say that the probability of a man being six feet tall is 20%, but you can say he has 20% of chances of being between five and six feet tall. Probability density is given by a probability density function. Contrast with probability mass.

## R

[random variable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/random_variable "random variable")

A quantity whose values are random and to which a probability distribution is assigned, such as the possible outcomes of a roll of a dice.

[discrete random variable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/discrete_random_variable "discrete random variable")

[ratio variable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ratio_variable "ratio variable")

## S

[sample](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sample "sample") or [statistical sample](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=statistical_sample&action=edit&redlink=1 "statistical sample (page does not exist)")

That part of a population which is actually observed.

[sample space](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sample_space "sample space")

The set of possible outcomes of an experiment. For example, the sample space for rolling a six-sided die will be {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}.

[sampling](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sampling "sampling")

A process of selecting observations to obtain knowledge about a population. There are many methods to choose on which sample to do the observations.

[sampling distribution](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=sampling_distribution&action=edit&redlink=1 "sampling distribution (page does not exist)")

The probability distribution, under repeated sampling of the population, of a given statistic.

[skewness](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/skewness "skewness")

A measure of the asymmetry of the probability distribution of a real-valued random variable. Roughly speaking, a distribution has positive skew (right-skewed) if the higher tail is longer and negative skew (left-skewed) if the lower tail is longer (confusing the two is a common error).

[standard deviation](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/standard_deviation "standard deviation")

The most commonly used measure of statistical dispersion. It is the [square root](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/square_root "square root") of the variance, and is generally written ${\displaystyle \sigma }$ ([sigma](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=Sigma_\(letter\)&action=edit&redlink=1 "Sigma (letter) (page does not exist)")).

[standardized moment](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=standardized_moment&action=edit&redlink=1 "standardized moment (page does not exist)")

[statistic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/statistic "statistic")

The result of applying a statistical algorithm to a data set. It can also be described as an observable random variable.

[statistical inference](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/statistical_inference "statistical inference")

Inference about a population from a random sample drawn from it or, more generally, about a random process from its observed behavior during a finite period of time.

[statistical dispersion](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=statistical_dispersion&action=edit&redlink=1 "statistical dispersion (page does not exist)")

(also called statistical variability) is a measure of how diverse some data is. It can be expressed by the variance or the standard deviation.

[statistical parameter](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=statistical_parameter&action=edit&redlink=1 "statistical parameter (page does not exist)")

is a parameter that indexes a family of probability distributions.

of a random variable is a measure of its statistical dispersion, indicating how far from the expected value its values typically are. The variance of random variable *X* is typically designated as ${\displaystyle \operatorname {var} (X)}$, ${\displaystyle \sigma _{X}^{2}}$, or simply ${\displaystyle \sigma ^{2}}$.
