> In the second part of my report, we deal with the Kähler varieties known as K3, named in honor of [Kummer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernst_Kummer "Ernst Kummer"), [Kähler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erich_K%C3%A4hler "Erich Kähler"), [Kodaira](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kunihiko_Kodaira "Kunihiko Kodaira") and of the beautiful mountain [K2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K2 "K2") in [Kashmir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kashmir "Kashmir").

André [Weil (1958](#CITEREFWeil1958), p. 546), describing the reason for the name "K3 surface"

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a complex analytic **K3 surface** is a compact connected [complex manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_manifold "Complex manifold") of dimension 2 with а trivial [canonical bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_bundle "Canonical bundle") and [irregularity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irregularity_of_a_surface "Irregularity of a surface") zero. An (algebraic) K3 surface over any [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_\(mathematics\) "Field (mathematics)") means a [smooth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_scheme "Smooth scheme"), [proper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proper_morphism "Proper morphism"), [geometrically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrically_\(algebraic_geometry\) "Geometrically (algebraic geometry)") connected [algebraic surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_surface "Algebraic surface") that satisfies the same conditions. In the [Enriques–Kodaira classification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enriques%E2%80%93Kodaira_classification "Enriques–Kodaira classification") of surfaces, K3 surfaces form one of the four classes of minimal surfaces of [Kodaira dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kodaira_dimension "Kodaira dimension") zero. A simple example is the Fermat [quartic surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quartic_surface "Quartic surface") ${\textstyle x^{4}+y^{4}+z^{4}+w^{4}=0}$ in [complex projective 3-space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_projective_space "Complex projective space").

Together with two-dimensional compact [complex tori](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_tori "Complex tori"), K3 surfaces are the [Calabi–Yau manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabi%E2%80%93Yau_manifold "Calabi–Yau manifold") (and also the [hyperkähler manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperk%C3%A4hler_manifold "Hyperkähler manifold")) of dimension two. As such, they are at the center of the classification of algebraic surfaces, between the positively curved [del Pezzo surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Del_Pezzo_surface "Del Pezzo surface") (which are easy to classify) and the negatively curved surfaces of [general type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_type "General type") (which are essentially unclassifiable). K3 surfaces can be considered the simplest algebraic varieties whose structure does not reduce to [curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_curve "Algebraic curve") or [abelian varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_varieties "Abelian varieties"), and yet where a substantial understanding is possible. A complex K3 surface has real dimension 4, and it plays an important role in the study of smooth [4-manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-manifold "4-manifold"). K3 surfaces have been applied to [Kac–Moody algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kac%E2%80%93Moody_algebra "Kac–Moody algebra"), [mirror symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirror_symmetry_\(string_theory\) "Mirror symmetry (string theory)") and [string theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_theory "String theory").

It can be useful to think of complex algebraic K3 surfaces as part of the broader family of complex analytic K3 surfaces. Many other types of algebraic varieties do not have such non-algebraic deformations.

## Definition

There are several equivalent ways to define K3 surfaces. The only compact complex surfaces with trivial canonical bundle are K3 surfaces and compact complex tori, and so one can add any condition excluding the latter to define K3 surfaces. For example, it is equivalent to define a complex analytic K3 surface as a [simply connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simply_connected "Simply connected") compact complex manifold of dimension 2 with a nowhere-vanishing holomorphic [2-form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form"). (The latter condition says exactly that the canonical bundle is trivial.)

There are also some variants of the definition. Over the complex numbers, some authors consider only the algebraic K3 surfaces. (An algebraic K3 surface is automatically [projective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_variety "Projective variety").[^1]) Or one may allow K3 surfaces to have [du Val singularities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Du_Val_singularities "Du Val singularities") (the [canonical singularities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_singularities "Canonical singularities") of dimension 2), rather than being smooth.

## Calculation of the Betti numbers

The [Betti numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Betti_number "Betti number") of a complex analytic K3 surface are computed as follows.[^2] (A similar argument gives the same answer for the Betti numbers of an algebraic K3 surface over any field, defined using [l-adic cohomology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L-adic_cohomology "L-adic cohomology").) By definition, the canonical bundle ${\displaystyle K_{X}=\Omega _{X}^{2}}$ is trivial, and the irregularity *q* (*X*) (the dimension ${\displaystyle h^{1}(X,O_{X})}$ of the [coherent sheaf cohomology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coherent_sheaf_cohomology "Coherent sheaf cohomology") group ${\displaystyle H^{1}(X,O_{X})}$) is zero. By [Serre duality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serre_duality "Serre duality"),

$$
{\displaystyle h^{2}(X,{\mathcal {O}}_{X})=h^{0}(X,K_{X})=1.}
$$

As a result, the arithmetic genus (or [holomorphic Euler characteristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holomorphic_Euler_characteristic "Holomorphic Euler characteristic")) of *X* is:

$$
{\displaystyle \chi (X,{\mathcal {O}}_{X}):=\sum _{i}(-1)^{i}h^{i}(X,{\mathcal {O}}_{X})=1-0+1=2.}
$$

On the other hand, the [Riemann–Roch theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann%E2%80%93Roch_theorem_for_surfaces "Riemann–Roch theorem for surfaces") (Noether's formula) says:

$$
{\displaystyle \chi (X,{\mathcal {O}}_{X})={\frac {1}{12}}\left(c_{1}(X)^{2}+c_{2}(X)\right),}
$$

where ${\displaystyle c_{i}(X)}$ is the *i* -th [Chern class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chern_class "Chern class") of the [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle"). Since ${\displaystyle K_{X}}$ is trivial, its first Chern class ${\displaystyle c_{1}(K_{X})=-c_{1}(X)}$ is zero, and so ${\displaystyle c_{2}(X)=24}$.

Next, the [exponential sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_sequence "Exponential sequence") ${\displaystyle 0\to \mathbb {Z} _{X}\to O_{X}\to O_{X}^{*}\to 0}$ gives an [exact sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exact_sequence "Exact sequence") of cohomology groups ${\displaystyle 0\to H^{1}(X,\mathbb {Z} )\to H^{1}(X,O_{X})}$, and so ${\displaystyle H^{1}(X,\mathbb {Z} )=0}$. Thus the Betti number ${\displaystyle b_{1}(X)}$ is zero, and by [Poincaré duality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_duality "Poincaré duality"), ${\displaystyle b_{3}(X)}$ is also zero. Finally, ${\displaystyle c_{2}(X)=24}$ is equal to the topological [Euler characteristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_characteristic "Euler characteristic")

$$
{\displaystyle \chi (X)=\sum _{i}(-1)^{i}b_{i}(X).}
$$

Since ${\displaystyle b_{0}(X)=b_{4}(X)=1}$ and ${\displaystyle b_{1}(X)=b_{3}(X)=0}$, it follows that ${\displaystyle b_{2}(X)=22}$.[^3]

## Properties

- Any two complex analytic K3 surfaces are [diffeomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffeomorphic "Diffeomorphic") as smooth 4-manifolds, by [Kunihiko Kodaira](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kunihiko_Kodaira "Kunihiko Kodaira").[^4]
- Every complex analytic K3 surface has a [Kähler metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%A4hler_metric "Kähler metric"), by [Yum-Tong Siu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yum-Tong_Siu "Yum-Tong Siu").[^5] (Analogously, but much easier: every algebraic K3 surface over a field is projective.) By [Shing-Tung Yau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shing-Tung_Yau "Shing-Tung Yau") 's solution to the [Calabi conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabi_conjecture "Calabi conjecture"), it follows that every complex analytic K3 surface has a [Ricci-flat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci-flat "Ricci-flat") Kähler metric.
- The [Hodge numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_theory#Hodge_theory_for_complex_projective_varieties "Hodge theory") of any K3 surface are listed in the Hodge diamond:
	|  |  | 1 |  |  |
	| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
	|  | 0 |  | 0 |  |
	| 1 |  | 20 |  | 1 |
	|  | 0 |  | 0 |  |
	|  |  | 1 |  |  |
	One way to show this is to calculate the [Jacobian ideal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_ideal "Jacobian ideal") of a specific K3 surface, and then using a variation of [Hodge structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_structure "Hodge structure") on the [moduli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moduli_space "Moduli space") of algebraic K3 surfaces to show that all such K3 surfaces have the same Hodge numbers. A more low-brow calculation can be done using the calculation of the Betti numbers along with the parts of the [Hodge structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_structure "Hodge structure") computed on ${\displaystyle H^{2}(X;\mathbb {Z} )}$ for an arbitrary K3 surface. In this case, Hodge symmetry forces ${\displaystyle H^{0}(X;\Omega _{X}^{2})\cong \mathbb {C} }$, hence ${\displaystyle H^{1}(X,\Omega _{X})\cong \mathbb {C} ^{20}}$. For K3 surfaces in [characteristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic_\(algebra\) "Characteristic (algebra)") *p* > 0, this was first shown by Alexey Rudakov and [Igor Shafarevich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Igor_Shafarevich "Igor Shafarevich").[^6]
- For a complex analytic K3 surface *X*, the intersection form (or [cup product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cup_product "Cup product")) on ${\displaystyle H^{2}(X,\mathbb {Z} )\cong \mathbb {Z} ^{22}}$ is a [symmetric bilinear form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_bilinear_form "Symmetric bilinear form") with values in the integers, known as the **K3 lattice**. This is isomorphic to the even [unimodular lattice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unimodular_lattice "Unimodular lattice") ${\displaystyle \operatorname {II} _{3,19}}$, or equivalently ${\displaystyle E_{8}(-1)^{\oplus 2}\oplus U^{\oplus 3}}$, where *U* is the hyperbolic lattice of rank 2 and ${\displaystyle E_{8}}$ is the [E8 lattice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E8_lattice "E8 lattice").[^7]
- [Yukio Matsumoto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yukio_Matsumoto "Yukio Matsumoto") 's [11/8 conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-manifold#Smooth_4-manifolds "4-manifold") predicts that every smooth [oriented](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oriented "Oriented") 4-manifold *X* with even intersection form has second Betti number at least 11/8 times the absolute value of the [signature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signature_\(topology\) "Signature (topology)"). This would be optimal if true, since equality holds for a complex K3 surface, which has signature 3−19 = −16. The conjecture would imply that every simply connected smooth 4-manifold with even intersection form is [homeomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphic "Homeomorphic") to a [connected sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connected_sum "Connected sum") of copies of the K3 surface and of ${\displaystyle S^{2}\times S^{2}}$.[^8]
- Every complex surface that is diffeomorphic to a K3 surface is a K3 surface, by Robert Friedman and [John Morgan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Morgan_\(mathematician\) "John Morgan (mathematician)"). On the other hand, there are smooth complex surfaces (some of them projective) that are homeomorphic but not diffeomorphic to a K3 surface, by Kodaira and [Michael Freedman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Freedman "Michael Freedman").[^9] These "homotopy K3 surfaces" all have Kodaira dimension 1.

## Examples

- The [double cover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Branched_covering "Branched covering") *X* of the [projective plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_plane "Projective plane") branched along a smooth sextic (degree 6) curve is a K3 surface of genus 2 (that is, degree 2 *g* −2 = 2). (This terminology means that the inverse image in *X* of a general [hyperplane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperplane "Hyperplane") in ${\displaystyle \mathbf {P} ^{2}}$ is a smooth curve of [genus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genus_\(mathematics\) "Genus (mathematics)") 2.)
- A smooth quartic (degree 4) surface in ${\displaystyle \mathbf {P} ^{3}}$ is a K3 surface of genus 3 (that is, degree 4).
- A [Kummer surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kummer_surface "Kummer surface") is the quotient of a two-dimensional [abelian variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_variety "Abelian variety") *A* by the action ${\displaystyle a\mapsto -a}$. This results in 16 singularities, at the 2-torsion points of *A*. The [minimal resolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resolution_of_singularities "Resolution of singularities") of this singular surface may also be called a Kummer surface; that resolution is a K3 surface. When *A* is the [Jacobian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_variety "Jacobian variety") of a curve of genus 2, Kummer showed that the quotient ${\displaystyle A/(\pm 1)}$ can be embedded into ${\displaystyle \mathbf {P} ^{3}}$ as a quartic surface with 16 [nodes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singular_point_of_an_algebraic_variety#Nodes "Singular point of an algebraic variety").
- More generally: for any quartic surface *Y* with du Val singularities, the minimal resolution of *Y* is an algebraic K3 surface.
- The intersection of a [quadric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadric_\(algebraic_geometry\) "Quadric (algebraic geometry)") and a cubic in ${\displaystyle \mathbf {P} ^{4}}$ is a K3 surface of genus 4 (that is, degree 6).
- The intersection of three quadrics in ${\displaystyle \mathbf {P} ^{5}}$ is a K3 surface of genus 5 (that is, degree 8).
- The complete intersection of two bihomogeneous forms of bidegrees ${\displaystyle (1,2)}$ and ${\displaystyle (2,1)}$ in ${\displaystyle \mathbf {P} ^{2}\times \mathbf {P} ^{2}}$ is a K3 surface, typically of Picard rank 2
- There are several databases of K3 surfaces with du Val singularities in [weighted projective spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weighted_projective_space "Weighted projective space").[^10]

## The Picard lattice

The [Picard group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Picard_group "Picard group") Pic(*X*) of a complex analytic K3 surface *X* is the abelian group of complex analytic line bundles on *X*. For an algebraic K3 surface, Pic(*X*) is the group of algebraic line bundles on *X*. The two definitions agree for a complex algebraic K3 surface, by [Jean-Pierre Serre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Serre "Jean-Pierre Serre") 's [GAGA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GAGA "GAGA") theorem.

The Picard group of a K3 surface *X* is always a [finitely generated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finitely_generated_abelian_group "Finitely generated abelian group") free abelian group; its rank is called the **Picard number** ${\displaystyle \rho }$. In the complex case, Pic(*X*) is a subgroup of ${\displaystyle H^{2}(X,\mathbb {Z} )\cong \mathbb {Z} ^{22}}$. It is an important feature of K3 surfaces that many different Picard numbers can occur. For *X* a complex algebraic K3 surface, ${\displaystyle \rho }$ can be any integer between 1 and 20. In the complex analytic case, ${\displaystyle \rho }$ may also be zero. (In that case, *X* contains no closed complex curves at all. By contrast, an algebraic surface always contains many continuous families of curves.) Over an [algebraically closed field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraically_closed_field "Algebraically closed field") of characteristic *p* > 0, there is a special class of K3 surfaces, [supersingular K3 surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supersingular_K3_surface "Supersingular K3 surface"), with Picard number 22.

The **Picard lattice** of a K3 surface is the abelian group Pic(*X*) together with its intersection form, a symmetric bilinear form with values in the integers. (Over ${\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }$, the intersection form is the restriction of the intersection form on ${\displaystyle H^{2}(X,\mathbb {Z} )}$. Over a general field, the intersection form can be defined using the [intersection theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intersection_theory "Intersection theory") of curves on a surface, by identifying the Picard group with the [divisor class group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divisor_class_group "Divisor class group").) The Picard lattice of a K3 surface is always *even*, meaning that the integer ${\displaystyle u^{2}}$ is even for each ${\displaystyle u\in \operatorname {Pic} (X)}$.

The [Hodge index theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_index_theorem "Hodge index theorem") implies that the Picard lattice of an algebraic K3 surface has signature ${\displaystyle (1,\rho -1)}$. Many properties of a K3 surface are determined by its Picard lattice, as a symmetric bilinear form over the integers. This leads to a strong connection between the theory of K3 surfaces and the arithmetic of symmetric bilinear forms. As a first example of this connection: a complex analytic K3 surface is algebraic if and only if there is an element ${\displaystyle u\in \operatorname {Pic} (X)}$ with ${\displaystyle u^{2}>0}$.[^11]

Roughly speaking, the space of all complex analytic K3 surfaces has complex dimension 20, while the space of K3 surfaces with Picard number ${\displaystyle \rho }$ has dimension ${\displaystyle 20-\rho }$ (excluding the supersingular case). In particular, algebraic K3 surfaces occur in 19-dimensional families. More details about [moduli spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moduli_space "Moduli space") of K3 surfaces are given below.

The precise description of which lattices can occur as Picard lattices of K3 surfaces is complicated. One clear statement, due to [Viacheslav Nikulin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viacheslav_Nikulin "Viacheslav Nikulin") and [David Morrison](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_R._Morrison_\(mathematician\) "David R. Morrison (mathematician)"), is that every even lattice of signature ${\displaystyle (1,\rho -1)}$ with ${\displaystyle \rho \leq 11}$ is the Picard lattice of some complex projective K3 surface.[^12] The space of such surfaces has dimension ${\displaystyle 20-\rho }$.

## Elliptic K3 surfaces

An important subclass of K3 surfaces, easier to analyze than the general case, consists of the K3 surfaces with an [elliptic fibration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_fibration "Elliptic fibration") ${\displaystyle X\to \mathbf {P} ^{1}}$. "Elliptic" means that all but finitely many fibers of this morphism are smooth curves of genus 1. The singular fibers are unions of [rational curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_curve "Rational curve"), with the possible types of singular fibers classified by Kodaira. There are always some singular fibers, since the sum of the topological Euler characteristics of the singular fibers is ${\displaystyle \chi (X)=24}$. A general elliptic K3 surface has exactly 24 singular fibers, each of type ${\displaystyle I_{1}}$ (a nodal cubic curve).[^13]

Whether a K3 surface is elliptic can be read from its Picard lattice. Namely, in characteristic not 2 or 3, a K3 surface *X* has an elliptic fibration if and only if there is a nonzero element ${\displaystyle u\in \operatorname {Pic} (X)}$ with ${\displaystyle u^{2}=0}$.[^14] (In characteristic 2 or 3, the latter condition may also correspond to a [quasi-elliptic fibration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enriques%E2%80%93Kodaira_classification#Surfaces_of_Kodaira_dimension_1 "Enriques–Kodaira classification").) It follows that having an elliptic fibration is a codimension-1 condition on a K3 surface. So there are 19-dimensional families of complex analytic K3 surfaces with an elliptic fibration, and 18-dimensional moduli spaces of projective K3 surfaces with an elliptic fibration.

Example: Every smooth quartic surface *X* in ${\displaystyle \mathbf {P} ^{3}}$ that contains a line *L* has an elliptic fibration ${\displaystyle X\to \mathbf {P} ^{1}}$, given by projecting away from *L*. The moduli space of all smooth quartic surfaces (up to isomorphism) has dimension 19, while the subspace of quartic surfaces containing a line has dimension 18.

## Rational curves on K3 surfaces

In contrast to positively curved varieties such as del Pezzo surfaces, a complex algebraic K3 surface *X* is not [uniruled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniruled_variety "Uniruled variety"); that is, it is not covered by a continuous family of rational curves. On the other hand, in contrast to negatively curved varieties such as surfaces of general type, *X* contains a large discrete set of rational curves (possibly singular). In particular, [Fedor Bogomolov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fedor_Bogomolov "Fedor Bogomolov") and [David Mumford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Mumford "David Mumford") showed that every curve on *X* is [linearly equivalent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linearly_equivalent "Linearly equivalent") to a positive linear combination of rational curves.[^15]

Another contrast to negatively curved varieties is that the [Kobayashi metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kobayashi_metric "Kobayashi metric") on a complex analytic K3 surface *X* is identically zero. The proof uses that an algebraic K3 surface *X* is always covered by a continuous family of images of elliptic curves.[^16] (These curves are singular in *X*, unless *X* happens to be an elliptic K3 surface.) A stronger question that remains open is whether every complex K3 surface admits a nondegenerate holomorphic map from ${\displaystyle \mathbb {C} ^{2}}$ (where "nondegenerate" means that the derivative of the map is an isomorphism at some point).[^17]

## The period map

Define a **marking** of a complex analytic K3 surface *X* to be an isomorphism of lattices from ${\displaystyle H^{2}(X,\mathbb {Z} )}$ to the K3 lattice ${\displaystyle \Lambda =E_{8}(-1)^{\oplus 2}\oplus U^{\oplus 3}}$. The space *N* of marked complex K3 surfaces is a non- [Hausdorff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_space "Hausdorff space") complex manifold of dimension 20.[^18] The set of isomorphism classes of complex analytic K3 surfaces is the quotient of *N* by the [orthogonal group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_group "Orthogonal group") ${\displaystyle O(\Lambda )}$, but this quotient is not a geometrically meaningful moduli space, because the action of ${\displaystyle O(\Lambda )}$ is far from being [properly discontinuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Properly_discontinuous "Properly discontinuous").[^19] (For example, the space of smooth quartic surfaces is irreducible of dimension 19, and yet every complex analytic K3 surface in the 20-dimensional family *N* has arbitrarily small deformations which are isomorphic to smooth quartics.[^20]) For the same reason, there is not a meaningful moduli space of compact complex tori of dimension at least 2.

The [period mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Period_mapping "Period mapping") sends a K3 surface to its [Hodge structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_structure "Hodge structure"). When stated carefully, the [Torelli theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torelli_theorem "Torelli theorem") holds: a K3 surface is determined by its Hodge structure. The period domain is defined as the 20-dimensional complex manifold

$$
{\displaystyle D=\{u\in P(\Lambda \otimes \mathbb {C} ):u^{2}=0,\,u\cdot {\overline {u}}>0\}.}
$$

The period mapping ${\displaystyle N\to D}$ sends a marked K3 surface *X* to the complex line ${\displaystyle H^{0}(X,\Omega ^{2})\subset H^{2}(X,\mathbb {C} )\cong \Lambda \otimes \mathbb {C} }$. This is surjective, and a local isomorphism, but not an isomorphism (in particular because *D* is Hausdorff and *N* is not). However, the **global Torelli theorem** for K3 surfaces says that the quotient map of sets

$$
{\displaystyle N/O(\Lambda )\to D/O(\Lambda )}
$$

is bijective. It follows that two complex analytic K3 surfaces *X* and *Y* are isomorphic if and only if there is a **Hodge isometry** from ${\displaystyle H^{2}(X,\mathbb {Z} )}$ to ${\displaystyle H^{2}(Y,\mathbb {Z} )}$, that is, an isomorphism of abelian groups that preserves the intersection form and sends ${\displaystyle H^{0}(X,\Omega ^{2})\subset H^{2}(X,\mathbb {C} )}$ to ${\displaystyle H^{0}(Y,\Omega ^{2})}$.[^21]

## Moduli spaces of projective K3 surfaces

A **polarized** K3 surface *X* of **genus** *g* is defined to be a projective K3 surface together with an [ample line bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ample_line_bundle "Ample line bundle") *L* such that *L* is primitive (that is, not 2 or more times another line bundle) and ${\displaystyle c_{1}(L)^{2}=2g-2}$. This is also called a polarized K3 surface of **degree** 2 *g* −2.[^22]

Under these assumptions, *L* is [basepoint-free](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basepoint-free "Basepoint-free"). In characteristic zero, [Bertini's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bertini%27s_theorem "Bertini's theorem") implies that there is a smooth curve *C* in the [linear system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_system_of_divisors "Linear system of divisors") | *L* |. All such curves have genus *g*, which explains why (*X*,*L*) is said to have genus *g*.

The vector space of sections of *L* has dimension *g* + 1, and so *L* gives a morphism from *X* to projective space ${\displaystyle \mathbf {P} ^{g}}$. In most cases, this morphism is an embedding, so that *X* is isomorphic to a surface of degree 2 *g* −2 in ${\displaystyle \mathbf {P} ^{g}}$.

There is an irreducible [coarse moduli space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coarse_moduli_space "Coarse moduli space") ${\displaystyle {\mathcal {F}}_{g}}$ of polarized complex K3 surfaces of genus *g* for each ${\displaystyle g\geq 2}$; it can be viewed as a [Zariski open](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zariski_open "Zariski open") subset of a [Shimura variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shimura_variety "Shimura variety") for the group [*SO* (2,19)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indefinite_orthogonal_group "Indefinite orthogonal group"). For each *g*, ${\displaystyle {\mathcal {F}}_{g}}$ is a [quasi-projective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-projective "Quasi-projective") complex variety of dimension 19.[^23] [Shigeru Mukai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shigeru_Mukai "Shigeru Mukai") showed that this moduli space is [unirational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unirational "Unirational") if ${\displaystyle g\leq 13}$ or ${\displaystyle g=18,20}$. In contrast, Valery Gritsenko, [Klaus Hulek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klaus_Hulek "Klaus Hulek") and Gregory Sankaran showed that ${\displaystyle {\mathcal {F}}_{g}}$ is of [general type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_type "General type") if ${\displaystyle g\geq 63}$ or ${\displaystyle g=47,51,55,58,59,61}$. A survey of this area was given by [Voisin (2008)](#CITEREFVoisin2008).

The different 19-dimensional moduli spaces ${\displaystyle {\mathcal {F}}_{g}}$ overlap in an intricate way. Indeed, there is a countably infinite set of codimension-1 subvarieties of each ${\displaystyle {\mathcal {F}}_{g}}$ corresponding to K3 surfaces of Picard number at least 2. Those K3 surfaces have polarizations of infinitely many different degrees, not just 2 *g* –2. So one can say that infinitely many of the other moduli spaces ${\displaystyle {\mathcal {F}}_{h}}$ meet ${\displaystyle {\mathcal {F}}_{g}}$. This is imprecise, since there is not a well-behaved space containing all the moduli spaces ${\displaystyle {\mathcal {F}}_{g}}$. However, a concrete version of this idea is the fact that any two complex algebraic K3 surfaces are deformation-equivalent through algebraic K3 surfaces.[^24]

More generally, a **quasi-polarized** K3 surface of genus *g* means a projective K3 surface with a primitive [nef](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nef_line_bundle "Nef line bundle") and [big](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_line_bundle "Big line bundle") line bundle *L* such that ${\displaystyle c_{1}(L)^{2}=2g-2}$. Such a line bundle still gives a morphism to ${\displaystyle \mathbf {P} ^{g}}$, but now it may contract finitely many (−2)-curves, so that the image *Y* of *X* is singular. (A **(−2)-curve** on a surface means a curve isomorphic to ${\displaystyle \mathbf {P} ^{1}}$ with self-intersection −2.) The moduli space of quasi-polarized K3 surfaces of genus *g* is still irreducible of dimension 19 (containing the previous moduli space as an open subset). Formally, it works better to view this as a moduli space of K3 surfaces *Y* with du Val singularities.[^25]

## The ample cone and the cone of curves

A remarkable feature of algebraic K3 surfaces is that the Picard lattice determines many geometric properties of the surface, including the [convex cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_cone "Convex cone") of ample divisors (up to automorphisms of the Picard lattice). The ample cone is determined by the Picard lattice as follows. By the Hodge index theorem, the intersection form on the real vector space ${\displaystyle N^{1}(X):=\operatorname {Pic} (X)\otimes \mathbb {R} }$ has signature ${\displaystyle (1,\rho -1)}$. It follows that the set of elements of ${\displaystyle N^{1}(X)}$ with positive self-intersection has two [connected components](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connected_component_\(topology\) "Connected component (topology)"). Call the **positive cone** the component that contains any ample divisor on *X*.

Case 1: There is no element *u* of Pic(*X*) with ${\displaystyle u^{2}=-2}$. Then the ample cone is equal to the positive cone. Thus it is the standard round cone.

Case 2: Otherwise, let ${\displaystyle \Delta =\{u\in \operatorname {Pic} (X):u^{2}=-2\}}$, the set of **roots** of the Picard lattice. The [orthogonal complements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_complement "Orthogonal complement") of the roots form a set of hyperplanes which all go through the positive cone. Then the ample cone is a connected component of the complement of these hyperplanes in the positive cone. Any two such components are isomorphic via the orthogonal group of the lattice Pic(*X*), since that contains the [reflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_\(mathematics\) "Reflection (mathematics)") across each root hyperplane. In this sense, the Picard lattice determines the ample cone up to isomorphism.[^26]

A related statement, due to Sándor Kovács, is that knowing one ample divisor *A* in Pic(*X*) determines the whole [cone of curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone_of_curves "Cone of curves") of *X*. Namely, suppose that *X* has Picard number ${\displaystyle \rho \geq 3}$. If the set of roots ${\displaystyle \Delta }$ is empty, then the closed cone of curves is the closure of the positive cone. Otherwise, the closed cone of curves is the closed convex cone spanned by all elements ${\displaystyle u\in \Delta }$ with ${\displaystyle A\cdot u>0}$. In the first case, *X* contains no (−2)-curves; in the second case, the closed cone of curves is the closed convex cone spanned by all (−2)-curves.[^27] (If ${\displaystyle \rho =2}$, there is one other possibility: the cone of curves may be spanned by one (−2)-curve and one curve with self-intersection 0.) So the cone of curves is either the standard round cone, or else it has "sharp corners" (because every (−2)-curve spans an *isolated* extremal ray of the cone of curves).

## Automorphism group

K3 surfaces are somewhat unusual among algebraic varieties in that their automorphism groups may be infinite, discrete, and highly nonabelian. By a version of the Torelli theorem, the Picard lattice of a complex algebraic K3 surface *X* determines the automorphism group of *X* up to [commensurability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commensurability_\(group_theory\) "Commensurability (group theory)"). Namely, let the **Weyl group** *W* be the subgroup of the orthogonal group *O* (Pic(*X*)) generated by reflections in the set of roots ${\displaystyle \Delta }$. Then *W* is a [normal subgroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_subgroup "Normal subgroup") of *O* (Pic(*X*)), and the automorphism group of *X* is commensurable with the quotient group *O* (Pic(*X*))/ *W*. A related statement, due to Hans Sterk, is that Aut(*X*) acts on the nef cone of *X* with a rational polyhedral [fundamental domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_domain "Fundamental domain").[^28]

## Relation to string duality

K3 surfaces appear almost ubiquitously in [string duality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_duality "String duality") and provide an important tool for the understanding of it. [String compactifications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compactification_\(physics\)#Compactification_in_string_theory "Compactification (physics)") on these surfaces are not trivial, yet they are simple enough to analyze most of their properties in detail. The type IIA string, the type IIB string, the E <sub>8</sub> ×E <sub>8</sub> heterotic string, the Spin(32)/Z2 heterotic string, and M-theory are related by compactification on a K3 surface. For example, the Type IIA string compactified on a K3 surface is equivalent to the heterotic string compactified on a 4-torus ([Aspinwall (1996)](#CITEREFAspinwall1996)).

## History

Quartic surfaces in ${\displaystyle \mathbf {P} ^{3}}$ were studied by [Ernst Kummer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernst_Kummer "Ernst Kummer"), [Arthur Cayley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Cayley "Arthur Cayley"), [Friedrich Schur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Schur "Friedrich Schur") and other 19th-century geometers. More generally, [Federigo Enriques](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federigo_Enriques "Federigo Enriques") observed in 1893 that for various numbers *g*, there are surfaces of degree 2 *g* −2 in ${\displaystyle \mathbf {P} ^{g}}$ with trivial canonical bundle and irregularity zero.[^29] In 1909, Enriques showed that such surfaces exist for all ${\displaystyle g\geq 3}$, and [Francesco Severi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francesco_Severi "Francesco Severi") showed that the moduli space of such surfaces has dimension 19 for each *g*.[^30]

André [Weil (1958)](#CITEREFWeil1958) gave K3 surfaces their name (see the quotation above) and made several influential conjectures about their classification. Kunihiko Kodaira completed the basic theory around 1960, in particular making the first systematic study of complex analytic K3 surfaces which are not algebraic. He showed that any two complex analytic K3 surfaces are deformation-equivalent and hence diffeomorphic, which was new even for algebraic K3 surfaces. An important later advance was the proof of the Torelli theorem for complex algebraic K3 surfaces by [Ilya Piatetski-Shapiro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ilya_Piatetski-Shapiro "Ilya Piatetski-Shapiro") and Igor Shafarevich (1971), extended to complex analytic K3 surfaces by Daniel Burns and [Michael Rapoport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Rapoport "Michael Rapoport") (1975).
