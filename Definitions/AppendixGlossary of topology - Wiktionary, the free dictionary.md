This is a glossary of some terms used in the branch of [mathematics](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mathematics "mathematics") known as [topology](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/topology "topology"). Although there is no absolute distinction between different areas of topology, the focus here is on [general topology](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=general_topology&action=edit&redlink=1 "general topology (page does not exist)"). The following definitions are also fundamental to [algebraic topology](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/algebraic_topology "algebraic topology"), [differential topology](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/differential_topology "differential topology") and [geometric topology](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=geometric_topology&action=edit&redlink=1 "geometric topology (page does not exist)").

All spaces in this glossary are assumed to be [topological spaces](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/topological_space "topological space") unless stated otherwise.

## A

accessible

See [**${\displaystyle T_{1}}$**](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=T1_space&action=edit&redlink=1 "T1 space (page does not exist)").

[accumulation point](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/accumulation_point "accumulation point")

See [limit point](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/limit_point "limit point").

[Alexandrov topology](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Alexandrov_topology "Alexandrov topology")

A space ${\displaystyle X}$ has the [Alexandrov topology](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Alexandrov_topology "Alexandrov topology") (or is **finitely generated**) if arbitrary intersections of open sets in ${\displaystyle X}$ are open, or equivalently, if arbitrary unions of closed sets are closed.

almost discrete

A space is almost discrete if every open set is closed (hence clopen). The almost discrete spaces are precisely the finitely generated zero-dimensional spaces.

[approach space](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=approach_space&action=edit&redlink=1 "approach space (page does not exist)")

An [approach space](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=approach_space&action=edit&redlink=1 "approach space (page does not exist)") is a generalization of metric space based on point-to-set distances, instead of point-to-point.

## B

Baire space

This has two distinct common meanings:
1. A space is a **Baire space** if the intersection of any [countable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/countable "countable") collection of dense open sets is dense; see [Baire space](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Baire_space "Baire space").
2. **Baire space** is the set of all functions from the natural numbers to the natural numbers, with the topology of pointwise convergence; see [Baire space (set theory)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baire_space_\(set_theory\) "w:Baire space (set theory)").

[base](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/base "base")

A collection ${\displaystyle B}$ of open sets is a [base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/base_\(topology\) "w:base (topology)") (or **basis**) for a topology ${\displaystyle \tau }$ if every open set in ${\displaystyle \tau }$ is a union of sets in ${\displaystyle B}$. The topology ${\displaystyle \tau }$ is the smallest topology on ${\displaystyle X}$ containing ${\displaystyle B}$ and is said to be generated by ${\displaystyle B}$.

[basis](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/basis "basis")

See **base**.

[Borel algebra](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=Borel_algebra&action=edit&redlink=1 "Borel algebra (page does not exist)")

The [Borel algebra](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=Borel_algebra&action=edit&redlink=1 "Borel algebra (page does not exist)") on a topological space ${\displaystyle (X,\tau )}$ is the smallest [${\displaystyle \sigma }$ -algebra](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sigma-algebra "sigma-algebra") containing all the open sets. It is obtained by taking intersection of all ${\displaystyle \sigma }$ -algebras on ${\displaystyle X}$ containing ${\displaystyle \tau }$.

Borel set

A Borel set is an element of a Borel algebra.

[boundary](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/boundary "boundary")

The [boundary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/boundary_\(topology\) "w:boundary (topology)") (or **frontier**) of a set is the set's closure minus its interior. Equivalently, the boundary of a set is the intersection of its closure with the closure of its complement. Boundary of a set ${\displaystyle A}$ is denoted by ${\displaystyle \partial A}$

[bounded](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bounded "bounded")

A set in a metric space is bounded if it has [finite](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/finite_set "finite set") diameter. Equivalently, a set is bounded if it is contained in some open ball of finite radius. A [function](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/function "function") taking values in a metric space is bounded if its [image](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/image "image") is a bounded set.

## C

[category of topological spaces](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=category_of_topological_spaces&action=edit&redlink=1 "category of topological spaces (page does not exist)")

The [category](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/category_theory "category theory") **[Top](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=Category_of_topological_spaces&action=edit&redlink=1 "Category of topological spaces (page does not exist)")** has [topological spaces](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/topological_space "topological space") as [objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/object_\(category_theory\) "w:object (category theory)") and [continuous maps](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=continuous_map&action=edit&redlink=1 "continuous map (page does not exist)") as [morphisms](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/morphism "morphism").

[Cauchy sequence](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Cauchy_sequence "Cauchy sequence")

A [sequence](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sequence "sequence") ${\displaystyle x_{n}}$ in a metric space ${\displaystyle (M,d)}$ is a [Cauchy sequence](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Cauchy_sequence "Cauchy sequence") if, for every [positive](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/positive_number "positive number") [real number](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/real_number "real number") ${\displaystyle r}$, there is an [integer](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/integer "integer") ${\displaystyle N}$ such that for all integers ${\displaystyle m,n>N}$, we have ${\displaystyle d(x_{m},x_{n})<r}$.

[clopen set](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=clopen_set&action=edit&redlink=1 "clopen set (page does not exist)")

A set is [clopen](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/clopen "clopen") if it is both open and closed.

closed ball

If ${\displaystyle (M,d)}$ is a [metric space](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/metric_space "metric space"), a closed ball is a set of the form ${\displaystyle D(x;r):=\{y{\text{ in }}M:d(x,y)\leq r\}}$, where ${\displaystyle x}$ is in ${\displaystyle M}$ and ${\displaystyle r}$ is a positive real number, the **radius** of the ball. A closed ball of radius ${\displaystyle r}$ is a **closed ${\displaystyle r}$ -ball**. Every closed ball is a closed set in the topology induced on ${\displaystyle M}$ by ${\displaystyle d}$. Note that the closed ball ${\displaystyle D(x;r)}$ might not be equal to the [closure](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/closure "closure") of the open ball ${\displaystyle B(x;r)}$.

[closed set](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/closed_set "closed set")

A set is [closed](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/closed "closed") if its complement is a member of the topology.

[closed function](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=closed_function&action=edit&redlink=1 "closed function (page does not exist)")

A function from one space to another is closed if the [image](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/image "image") of every closed set is closed.

[closure](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/closure "closure")

The [closure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/closure_\(topology\) "w:closure (topology)") of a set is the smallest closed set containing the original set. It is equal to the intersection of all closed sets which contain it. An element of the closure of a set ${\displaystyle S}$ is a **point of closure** of ${\displaystyle S}$.

closure operator

See **[Kuratowski closure axioms](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=Kuratowski_closure_axioms&action=edit&redlink=1 "Kuratowski closure axioms (page does not exist)")**.

[coarser topology](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=coarser_topology&action=edit&redlink=1 "coarser topology (page does not exist)")

If ${\displaystyle X}$ is a set, and if ${\displaystyle T_{1}}$ and ${\displaystyle T_{2}}$ are topologies on ${\displaystyle X}$, then ${\displaystyle T_{1}}$ is [coarser](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/coarser "coarser") (or **smaller**, **weaker**) than ${\displaystyle T_{2}}$ if ${\displaystyle T_{1}}$ is contained in ${\displaystyle T_{2}}$. Beware, some authors, especially [analysts](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mathematical_analysis "mathematical analysis"), use the term **stronger**.

comeagre

A subset ${\displaystyle A}$ of a space ${\displaystyle X}$ is **comeagre** (**comeager**) if its [complement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/complement_\(set_theory\) "w:complement (set theory)") ${\displaystyle X\backslash A}$ is [meagre](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=meagre_set&action=edit&redlink=1 "meagre set (page does not exist)"). Also called **residual**.

[compact](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/compact "compact"), [compact space](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/compact_space "compact space")

A space is [compact](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/compact "compact") if every open cover has a [finite](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/finite "finite") subcover. Every compact space is Lindelöf and paracompact. Therefore, every compact [Hausdorff space](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Hausdorff_space "Hausdorff space") is normal. See also **quasicompact**.

[compact-open topology](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=compact-open_topology&action=edit&redlink=1 "compact-open topology (page does not exist)")

The [compact-open topology](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=compact-open_topology&action=edit&redlink=1 "compact-open topology (page does not exist)") on the set ${\displaystyle C(X,Y)}$ of all continuous maps between two spaces ${\displaystyle X}$ and ${\displaystyle Y}$ is defined as follows: given a compact subset ${\displaystyle K}$ of ${\displaystyle X}$ and an open subset ${\displaystyle U}$ of ${\displaystyle Y}$, let ${\displaystyle V(K,U)}$ denote the set of all maps ${\displaystyle f}$ in ${\displaystyle C(X,Y)}$ such that ${\displaystyle f(K)}$ is contained in ${\displaystyle (U)}$. Then the collection of all such ${\displaystyle V(K,U)}$ is a subbase for the compact-open topology.

[complete](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/complete "complete"), [complete space](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=complete_space&action=edit&redlink=1 "complete space (page does not exist)")

A metric space is [complete](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/complete "complete") if every Cauchy sequence converges.

completely metrizable/-isable

See **complete space**.

completely normal

A space is completely normal if any two separated sets have [disjoint](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/disjoint "disjoint") neighborhoods.

completely normal Hausdorff

A completely normal Hausdorff space (or [${\displaystyle T_{5}}$ space](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=T5_space&action=edit&redlink=1 "T5 space (page does not exist)")) is a completely normal ${\displaystyle T_{1}}$ space. (A completely normal space is Hausdorff [if and only if](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/if_and_only_if "if and only if") it is ${\displaystyle T_{1}}$, so the terminology is [consistent](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/consistent "consistent").) Every completely normal Hausdorff space is normal Hausdorff.

[completely regular](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=completely_regular&action=edit&redlink=1 "completely regular (page does not exist)")

A space is [completely regular](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=completely_regular_space&action=edit&redlink=1 "completely regular space (page does not exist)") if, whenever ${\displaystyle C}$ is a closed set and ${\displaystyle x}$ is a point not in ${\displaystyle C}$, then ${\displaystyle C}$ and ${\displaystyle \{x\}}$ are functionally separated.

[completely ${\displaystyle T_{3}}$](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=completely_T3&action=edit&redlink=1 "completely T3 (page does not exist)")

See **[Tychonoff](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Tychonoff_space "Tychonoff space")**.

component

See **[connected component](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/connected_space "connected space")** / **Path-connected component**.

[connected](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/connected "connected")

A space is [connected](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=connected_\(topology\)&action=edit&redlink=1 "connected (topology) (page does not exist)") if it is not the union of a pair of [disjoint](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/disjoint "disjoint") nonempty open sets. Equivalently, a space is connected if the only clopen sets are the whole space and the empty set.

[connected component](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/connected_component "connected component")

A [connected component](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/connected_space "connected space") of a space is a [maximal](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/maximal "maximal") nonempty connected subspace. Each connected component is closed, and the set of connected components of a space is a [partition](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=partition_of_a_set&action=edit&redlink=1 "partition of a set (page does not exist)") of that space.

[continuous](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/continuous "continuous")

A function from one space to another is [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/continuity_\(topology\) "w:continuity (topology)") if the [preimage](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/preimage "preimage") of every open set is open.

[contractible](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/contractible "contractible")

A space ${\displaystyle X}$ is contractible if the [identity map](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=identity_map&action=edit&redlink=1 "identity map (page does not exist)") on ${\displaystyle X}$ is homotopic to a [constant](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/constant "constant") map. Every contractible space is simply connected.

coproduct topology

If ${\displaystyle \{X_{i}\}}$ is a collection of spaces and ${\displaystyle X}$ is the (set-theoretic) [disjoint union](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/disjoint_union "disjoint union") of ${\displaystyle \{X_{i}\}}$, then the coproduct topology (or **disjoint union topology**, **topological sum** of the ${\displaystyle \{X_{i}\}}$ on ${\displaystyle X}$ is the finest topology for which all the injection maps are continuous.

countably compact

A space is countably compact if every [countable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/countable "countable") open cover has a [finite](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/finite "finite") subcover. Every countably compact space is pseudocompact and weakly countably compact.

countably locally finite

A collection of subsets of a space ${\displaystyle X}$ is **countably locally finite** (or **${\displaystyle \sigma }$ -locally finite**) if it is the union of a [countable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/countable "countable") collection of locally finite collections of subsets of ${\displaystyle X}$.

[cover](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cover "cover")

A collection of subsets of a space is a cover (or **covering**) of that space if the union of the collection is the whole space.

covering

See **cover**.

cut point

If ${\displaystyle X}$ is a connected space with more than one point, then a point ${\displaystyle x}$ of ${\displaystyle X}$ is a cut point if the subspace ${\displaystyle X-\{x\}}$ is disconnected.

## D

[dense set](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=dense_set&action=edit&redlink=1 "dense set (page does not exist)")

A set is dense if it has nonempty intersection with every nonempty open set. Equivalently, a set is dense if its closure is the whole space.

derived set

If ${\displaystyle X}$ is a space and ${\displaystyle S}$ is a subset of ${\displaystyle X}$, the derived set of ${\displaystyle S}$ in ${\displaystyle X}$ is the set of limit points of ${\displaystyle S}$ in ${\displaystyle X}$.

diameter

If ${\displaystyle (M,d)}$ is a metric space and ${\displaystyle S}$ is a subset of ${\displaystyle M}$, the diameter of ${\displaystyle S}$ is the [supremum](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/supremum "supremum") of the distances ${\displaystyle d(x,y)}$, where ${\displaystyle x}$ and ${\displaystyle y}$ range over ${\displaystyle S}$.

discrete metric

The discrete metric on a set ${\displaystyle X}$ is the function ${\displaystyle d:X\times X\rightarrow }$; [**R**](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/real_number "real number") such that for all ${\displaystyle x}$, ${\displaystyle y}$ in ${\displaystyle X}$, ${\displaystyle d(x,x)=0}$ and ${\displaystyle d(x,y)=1}$ if ${\displaystyle x\neq y}$. The discrete metric induces the discrete topology on *X*.

[discrete space](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=discrete_space&action=edit&redlink=1 "discrete space (page does not exist)")

A space ${\displaystyle X}$ ' is discrete if every subset of ${\displaystyle X}$ is open. We say that ${\displaystyle X}$ carries the **discrete topology**.

[discrete topology](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/discrete_topology "discrete topology")

See **discrete space**.

disjoint union topology

See **coproduct topology**.

[dispersion point](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=dispersion_point&action=edit&redlink=1 "dispersion point (page does not exist)")

If ${\displaystyle X}$ is a connected space with more than one point, then a point ${\displaystyle x}$ of ${\displaystyle X}$ is a dispersion point if the subspace ${\displaystyle X-\{x\}}$ is hereditarily disconnected (its only connected components are the one-point sets).

[distance](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/distance "distance")

See **[metric space](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/metric_space "metric space")**.

[dunce hat](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dunce_hat "dunce hat")

## E

[entourage](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/entourage "entourage")

See **[uniform space](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/uniform_space "uniform space")**.

exterior

The exterior of a set is the interior of its complement.

## F

[${\displaystyle F_{\sigma }}$ set](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=F-sigma_set&action=edit&redlink=1 "F-sigma set (page does not exist)")

An [${\displaystyle F_{\sigma }}$ set](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=F-sigma_set&action=edit&redlink=1 "F-sigma set (page does not exist)") is a [countable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/countable "countable") union of closed sets.

[filter](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/filter "filter")

A filter on a space ${\displaystyle X}$ is a nonempty family ${\displaystyle F}$ of subsets of ${\displaystyle X}$ such that the following conditions hold:
1. The [empty set](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/empty_set "empty set") is not in ${\displaystyle F}$.
2. The intersection of any [finite](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/finite_set "finite set") number of elements of ${\displaystyle F}$ is again in ${\displaystyle F}$.
3. If ${\displaystyle A}$ is in ${\displaystyle F}$ and if ${\displaystyle B}$ contains ${\displaystyle A}$, then ${\displaystyle B}$ is in ${\displaystyle F}$.

[finer topology](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=finer_topology&action=edit&redlink=1 "finer topology (page does not exist)")

If ${\displaystyle X}$ is a set, and if ${\displaystyle T_{1}}$ and ${\displaystyle T_{2}}$ are topologies on ${\displaystyle X}$, then ${\displaystyle T_{2}}$ is [finer](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=finer_topology&action=edit&redlink=1 "finer topology (page does not exist)") (or **larger**, **stronger**) than ${\displaystyle T_{1}}$ if ${\displaystyle T_{2}}$ contains ${\displaystyle T_{1}}$. Beware, some authors, especially [analysts](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mathematical_analysis "mathematical analysis"), use the term **weaker**.

finitely generated

See **[Alexandrov topology](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Alexandrov_topology "Alexandrov topology")**.

[first category](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=first_category&action=edit&redlink=1 "first category (page does not exist)")

See **[meagre](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=meagre_set&action=edit&redlink=1 "meagre set (page does not exist)")**.

[first-countable](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=first-countable&action=edit&redlink=1 "first-countable (page does not exist)")

A space is [first-countable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-countable_space "w:First-countable space") if every point has a [countable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/countable "countable") local base.

Fréchet

See ${\displaystyle T_{1}}$.

frontier

See **[boundary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boundary_\(topology\) "w:Boundary (topology)")**.

full set

A [compact](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/compact_space "compact space") subset ${\displaystyle K}$ of the [complex plane](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/complex_plane "complex plane") is called **full** if its [complement](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/complement "complement") is connected. For example, the [closed unit disk](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=closed_unit_disk&action=edit&redlink=1 "closed unit disk (page does not exist)") is full, while the [unit circle](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/unit_circle "unit circle") is not.

functionally separated

Two sets ${\displaystyle A}$ and ${\displaystyle B}$ in a space ${\displaystyle X}$ are functionally separated if there is a continuous map ${\displaystyle f:X\rightarrow [0,1]}$ such that ${\displaystyle f(A)=0}$ and ${\displaystyle f(B)=1}$.

## G

[${\displaystyle G_{\delta }}$ set](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=G-delta_set&action=edit&redlink=1 "G-delta set (page does not exist)")

A [${\displaystyle G_{\delta }}$ set](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=G-delta_set&action=edit&redlink=1 "G-delta set (page does not exist)") is a [countable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/countable "countable") intersection of open sets.

## H

[Hausdorff](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Hausdorff "Hausdorff")

A [Hausdorff space](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Hausdorff_space "Hausdorff space") (or **[${\displaystyle T_{2}}$](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=T2_space&action=edit&redlink=1 "T2 space (page does not exist)") space**) is one in which every two distinct points have [disjoint](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/disjoint "disjoint") neighbourhoods. Every Hausdorff space is ${\displaystyle T_{1}}$.

[hereditary](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hereditary "hereditary")

A property of spaces is said to be hereditary if whenever a space has that property, then so does every subspace of it. For example, second-countability is a hereditary property.

[homeomorphism](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/homeomorphism "homeomorphism")

If ${\displaystyle X}$ and ${\displaystyle Y}$ are spaces, a [homeomorphism](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/homeomorphism "homeomorphism") from ${\displaystyle X}$ to ${\displaystyle Y}$ is a [bijective](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bijective "bijective") function ${\displaystyle f:X\rightarrow Y}$ such that ${\displaystyle f}$ and ${\displaystyle f^{-1}}$ are continuous. The spaces ${\displaystyle X}$ and ${\displaystyle Y}$ are then said to be **homeomorphic**. From the standpoint of topology, homeomorphic spaces are identical.

[homogeneous](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/homogeneous "homogeneous")

A space ${\displaystyle X}$ is [homogeneous](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/homogeneous "homogeneous") if, for every ${\displaystyle x}$ and ${\displaystyle y}$ in ${\displaystyle X}$, there is a homeomorphism ${\displaystyle f:X\rightarrow }$; ${\displaystyle X}$ such that ${\displaystyle f(x)=y}$. Intuitively, the space looks the same at every point. Every [topological group](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/topological_group "topological group") is homogeneous.

[homotopic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/homotopic "homotopic"), [homotopic maps](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=homotopic_maps&action=edit&redlink=1 "homotopic maps (page does not exist)")

Two continuous maps ${\displaystyle f,g:X\rightarrow Y}$ are [homotopic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/homotopic "homotopic") (in ${\displaystyle Y}$) if there is a continuous map ${\displaystyle H:X\times [0,1]\rightarrow Y\rightarrow Y}$ such that ${\displaystyle H(x,0)=f(x)}$ and ${\displaystyle H(x,1)=g(x)}$ for all ${\displaystyle x}$ in ${\displaystyle X}$. Here, ${\displaystyle X\times [0,1]}$ is given the product topology. The function ${\displaystyle H}$ is called a **homotopy** (in ${\displaystyle Y}$) between ${\displaystyle f}$ and ${\displaystyle g}$.

homotopy

See **[homotopic maps](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=homotopic_maps&action=edit&redlink=1 "homotopic maps (page does not exist)")**.

[hyper-connected](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=hyper-connected&action=edit&redlink=1 "hyper-connected (page does not exist)")

A space is hyper-connected if no two non-empty open sets are disjoint. Every hyper-connected space is connected.

identification map

See **[quotient map](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/quotient_map "quotient map")**.

[identification space](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/identification_space "identification space")

See **[quotient space](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/quotient_space "quotient space")**.

[indiscrete space](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=indiscrete_space&action=edit&redlink=1 "indiscrete space (page does not exist)")

See **[trivial topology](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/trivial_topology "trivial topology")**.

[Infinite-dimensional topology](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=Infinite-dimensional_topology&action=edit&redlink=1 "Infinite-dimensional topology (page does not exist)")

[interior](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/interior "interior")

The [interior](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/interior "interior") of a set is the largest open set contained in the original set. It is equal to the union of all open sets contained in it. An element of the interior of a set ${\displaystyle S}$ is an **interior point** of ${\displaystyle S}$.

[isolated point](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=isolated_point&action=edit&redlink=1 "isolated point (page does not exist)")

More generally, if ${\displaystyle S}$ is a subset of a space ${\displaystyle X}$, and if ${\displaystyle x}$ is a point of ${\displaystyle S}$, then ${\displaystyle x}$ is an isolated point of ${\displaystyle S}$ if ${\displaystyle \{x\}}$ is open in the subspace topology on ${\displaystyle S}$.

[isometric](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/isometric "isometric") [isomorphism](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/isomorphism "isomorphism")

If *M* <sub>1</sub> and *M* <sub>2</sub> are metric spaces, an isometric isomorphism from *M* <sub>1</sub> to *M* <sub>2</sub> is a [bijective](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bijection "bijection") isometry *f*: *M* <sub>1</sub> → *M* <sub>2</sub>. [isometry](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/isometry "isometry")

If (*M* <sub>1</sub>, *d* <sub>1</sub>) and (*M* <sub>2</sub>, *d* <sub>2</sub>) are metric spaces, an isometry from *M* <sub>1</sub> to *M* <sub>2</sub> is a function *f*: *M* <sub>1</sub> → *M* <sub>2</sub> such that *d* <sub>2</sub> (*f* (*x*), *f* (*y*)) = *d* <sub>1</sub> (*x*, *y*) for all *x*, *y* in *M* <sub>1</sub>. Every isometry is [injective](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=Injective_function&action=edit&redlink=1 "Injective function (page does not exist)"), although not every isometry is [surjective](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/surjection "surjection").

## K

[Kolmogorov axiom](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=Kolmogorov_axiom&action=edit&redlink=1 "Kolmogorov axiom (page does not exist)")

See [${\displaystyle T_{0}}$](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=T0_space&action=edit&redlink=1 "T0 space (page does not exist)").

[Kuratowski closure axioms](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=Kuratowski_closure_axioms&action=edit&redlink=1 "Kuratowski closure axioms (page does not exist)")

The [Kuratowski closure axioms](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=Kuratowski_closure_axioms&action=edit&redlink=1 "Kuratowski closure axioms (page does not exist)") is a set of [axioms](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/axiom "axiom") satisfied by the function which takes each subset of ${\displaystyle X}$ to its closure:
1. *[isotonicity](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/isotonicity "isotonicity")*: Every set is contained in its closure.
2. *[idempotence](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/idempotence "idempotence")*: The closure of the closure of a set is equal to the closure of that set.
3. *Preservation of binary unions*: The closure of the union of two sets is the union of their closures.
4. *Preservation of nullary unions*: The closure of the empty set is empty.

If ${\displaystyle c}$ is a function from the [power set](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/power_set "power set") of ${\displaystyle X}$ to itself, then ${\displaystyle c}$ is a **closure operator** if it satisfies the Kuratowski closure axioms. The Kuratowski closure axioms can then be used to define a topology on ${\displaystyle X}$ by declaring the closed sets to be the [fixed points](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=fixed_point_\(mathematics\)&action=edit&redlink=1 "fixed point (mathematics) (page does not exist)") of this operator, i.e. a set ${\displaystyle A}$ is closed [if and only if](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/if_and_only_if "if and only if") ${\displaystyle c(A)=A}$.

[meagre](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/meagre "meagre")

If ${\displaystyle X}$ is a space and ${\displaystyle A}$ is a subset of ${\displaystyle X}$, then ${\displaystyle A}$ is meagre in ${\displaystyle X}$ (or of **first category** in ${\displaystyle X}$) if it is the [countable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/countable "countable") union of nowhere dense sets. A metric invariant is a property which is preserved under isometric isomorphism.

[metric map](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=metric_map&action=edit&redlink=1 "metric map (page does not exist)")

If ${\displaystyle X}$ and ${\displaystyle Y}$ are metric spaces with metrics ${\displaystyle d_{X}}$ and ${\displaystyle d_{Y}}$ respectively, then a metric map is a function ${\displaystyle f}$ from ${\displaystyle X}$ to ${\displaystyle Y}$, such that for any points ${\displaystyle x}$ and ${\displaystyle y}$ in ${\displaystyle X}$, ${\displaystyle d_{Y}(f(x),f(y))\leq d_{X}(x,y)}$. A metric map is strictly metric if the above inequality is strict for all ${\displaystyle x}$ and ${\displaystyle y}$ in ${\displaystyle X}$.

[metric space](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/metric_space "metric space")

A [metric space](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/metric_space "metric space") ${\displaystyle (M,d)}$ is a set ${\displaystyle M}$ equipped with a function ${\displaystyle d:M\times M\rightarrow }$ [${\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }$](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/real_number "real number") satisfying the following axioms for all ${\displaystyle x}$, ${\displaystyle y}$ and ${\displaystyle z}$ in ${\displaystyle M}$:
1. ${\displaystyle d(x,y)\geq 0}$
2. ${\displaystyle d(x,x)=0}$
3. ${\displaystyle {\text{if }}d(x,y)=0{\text{ then }}x=y}$ — identity of indiscernibles
4. ${\displaystyle d(x,y)=d(y,x)}$ — symmetry
5. ${\displaystyle d(x,z)\leq d(x,y)+d(y,z)}$ — [triangle inequality](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/triangle_inequality "triangle inequality")

The function ${\displaystyle d}$ is a **metric** on ${\displaystyle M}$, and ${\displaystyle d(x,y)}$ is the **distance** between ${\displaystyle x}$ and ${\displaystyle y}$. The collection of all open balls of *M* is a base for a topology on ${\displaystyle M}$; this is the topology on ${\displaystyle M}$ induced by ${\displaystyle d}$. Every metric space is Hausdorff and paracompact (and hence normal and Tychonoff). Every metric space is first-countable.

[metrizable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/metrizable "metrizable") **/** Metrisable

A space is [metrizable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/metrizable "metrizable") if it is homeomorphic to a metric space. Every metrizable space is Hausdorff and paracompact (and hence normal and Tychonoff). Every metrizable space is first-countable.

[monolith](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/monolith "monolith")

Every non-empty ultra-connected compact space ${\displaystyle X}$ has a largest proper open subset; this subset is called a **monolith**.

## P

[paracompact](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/paracompact "paracompact")

A space is [paracompact](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/paracompact "paracompact") if every open cover has a locally finite open refinement. Paracompact Hausdorff spaces are normal.

[partition of unity](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/partition_of_unity "partition of unity")

A partition of unity of a space ${\displaystyle X}$ is a set of continuous functions from ${\displaystyle X}$ to ${\displaystyle [0,1]}$ such that any point has a neighbourhood where all but a [finite](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/finite_set "finite set") number of the functions are identically zero, and the sum of all the functions on the entire space is identically ${\displaystyle 1}$.

## Q

quasicompact

See **[compact](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/compact_space "compact space")**. Some authors define "compact" to include the [Hausdorff](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Hausdorff "Hausdorff") separation axiom, and they use the term **quasicompact** to mean what we call in this glossary simply "compact" (without the Hausdorff axiom). This convention is most commonly found in French, and branches of mathematics heavily influenced by the French.

quotient map

If ${\displaystyle X}$ and ${\displaystyle Y}$ are spaces, and if ${\displaystyle f}$ is a [surjection](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/surjection "surjection") from ${\displaystyle X}$ to ${\displaystyle Y}$, then ${\displaystyle f}$ is a quotient map (or **identification map**) if, for every subset ${\displaystyle U}$ of ${\displaystyle Y}$, ${\displaystyle U}$ is open in ${\displaystyle Y}$ [if and only if](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/if_and_only_if "if and only if") ${\displaystyle f^{-1}(U)}$ is open in ${\displaystyle X}$. In other words, ${\displaystyle Y}$ has the ${\displaystyle f}$ -strong topology. Equivalently, ${\displaystyle f}$ is a quotient map if and only if it is the transfinite composition of maps ${\displaystyle X\rightarrow X/Z}$, where ${\displaystyle Z\subset X}$ is a subset. Note that this doesn't imply that *f* is an open function.

[quotient space](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/quotient_space "quotient space")

If ${\displaystyle X}$ is a space, ${\displaystyle Y}$ is a set, and ${\displaystyle f:X\rightarrow Y}$ is any [surjective](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/surjective "surjective") function, then the [quotient topology](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/quotient_space "quotient space") on ${\displaystyle Y}$ induced by ${\displaystyle f}$ is the finest topology for which ${\displaystyle f}$ is continuous. The space ${\displaystyle X}$ is a quotient space or **identification space**. By definition, ${\displaystyle f}$ is a quotient map. The most common example of this is to consider an [equivalence relation](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/equivalence_relation "equivalence relation") on ${\displaystyle X}$, with ${\displaystyle Y}$ the set of [equivalence classes](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/equivalence_class "equivalence class") and ${\displaystyle f}$ the natural projection map. This construction is dual to the construction of the subspace topology.

A cover ${\displaystyle K}$ is a refinement of a cover ${\displaystyle L}$ if every member of ${\displaystyle K}$ is a subset of some member of ${\displaystyle L}$.

[regular](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/regular "regular")

A space is [regular](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/regular "regular") if, whenever ${\displaystyle C}$ is a closed set and ${\displaystyle x}$ is a point not in ${\displaystyle C}$, then ${\displaystyle C}$ and ${\displaystyle x}$ have [disjoint](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/disjoint "disjoint") neighbourhoods.

A space is [regular Hausdorff](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=T3_space&action=edit&redlink=1 "T3 space (page does not exist)") (or ${\displaystyle T_{3}}$) if it is a regular ${\displaystyle T_{0}}$ space. (A regular space is Hausdorff [if and only if](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/if_and_only_if "if and only if") it is ${\displaystyle T_{0}}$, so the terminology is consistent.)

An open subset ${\displaystyle U}$ of a space ${\displaystyle X}$ is regular open if it equals the interior of its closure. The regular open subsets of a space form a [complete Boolean algebra](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=complete_Boolean_algebra&action=edit&redlink=1 "complete Boolean algebra (page does not exist)").

[relatively compact](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=relatively_compact&action=edit&redlink=1 "relatively compact (page does not exist)")

A subset ${\displaystyle Y}$ of a space ${\displaystyle X}$ is [relatively compact](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=relatively_compact&action=edit&redlink=1 "relatively compact (page does not exist)") in ${\displaystyle X}$ if the closure of ${\displaystyle Y}$ in ${\displaystyle X}$ is compact.

residual

If ${\displaystyle X}$ is a space and ${\displaystyle A}$ is a subset of ${\displaystyle X}$, then ${\displaystyle A}$ is residual in ${\displaystyle X}$ if the complement of ${\displaystyle A}$ is meagre in ${\displaystyle X}$. Also called **comeagre** or **comeager**.

## S

Second category

See **meagre**.

[second-countable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/second-countable "second-countable")

A space is [second-countable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/second-countable "second-countable") if it has a [countable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/countable "countable") base for its topology. Every second-countable space is first-countable, separable, and Lindelöf.

[semilocally simply connected](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=semilocally_simply_connected&action=edit&redlink=1 "semilocally simply connected (page does not exist)")

A space ${\displaystyle X}$ is [semilocally simply connected](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=semilocally_simply_connected&action=edit&redlink=1 "semilocally simply connected (page does not exist)") if, for every point ${\displaystyle x}$ in ${\displaystyle X}$, there is a neighbourhood ${\displaystyle U}$ of ${\displaystyle x}$ such that every loop at ${\displaystyle x}$ in ${\displaystyle U}$ is homotopic in ${\displaystyle X}$ to the constant loop ${\displaystyle X}$. Every simply connected space and every locally simply connected space is semilocally simply connected. (Compare with locally simply connected; here, the homotopy is allowed to live in ${\displaystyle X}$, whereas in the definition of locally simply connected, the homotopy must live in ${\displaystyle U}$.)

[separable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/separable "separable")

A space is [separable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/separable "separable") if it has a [countable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/countable "countable") dense subset.

[separated](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/separated "separated")

Two sets ${\displaystyle A}$ and ${\displaystyle B}$ are [separated](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/separated "separated") if each is [disjoint](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/disjoint "disjoint") from the other's closure.

sequentially compact

A space is sequentially compact if every [sequence](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sequence "sequence") has a convergent subsequence. Every sequentially compact space is countably compact, and every first-countable, countably compact space is sequentially compact.

[short map](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=short_map&action=edit&redlink=1 "short map (page does not exist)")

See **[metric map](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=metric_map&action=edit&redlink=1 "metric map (page does not exist)")**

[simply connected](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/simply_connected "simply connected")

A space is [simply connected](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/simply_connected "simply connected") if it is path-connected and every loop is homotopic to a [constant](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/constant "constant") map.

smaller topology

See **[coarser topology](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=coarser_topology&action=edit&redlink=1 "coarser topology (page does not exist)")**.

${\displaystyle f}$ -Strong topology

Let ${\displaystyle f\colon X\rightarrow Y}$ be a map of topological spaces. We say that ${\displaystyle Y}$ has the ${\displaystyle f}$ -strong topology if, for every subset ${\displaystyle U\subset Y}$, one has that ${\displaystyle U}$ is open in ${\displaystyle Y}$ if and only if ${\displaystyle f^{-1}(U)}$ is open in ${\displaystyle X}$

stronger topology

See **[finer topology](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=finer_topology&action=edit&redlink=1 "finer topology (page does not exist)")**. Beware, some authors, especially [analysts](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mathematical_analysis "mathematical analysis"), use the term **weaker topology**.

[subbase](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/subbase "subbase")

A collection of open sets is a [subbase](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/subbase "subbase") (or **subbasis**) for a topology if every non-empty proper open set in the topology is a union of [finite](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/finite_set "finite set") intersections of sets in the subbase. If ${\displaystyle B}$ is *any* collection of subsets of a set ${\displaystyle X}$, the topology on ${\displaystyle X}$ generated by ${\displaystyle B}$ is the smallest topology containing ${\displaystyle B}$; this topology consists of the empty set, ${\displaystyle X}$ and all unions of finite intersections of elements of ${\displaystyle B}$.

[subbasis](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/subbasis "subbasis")

See **[subbase](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/subbase "subbase")**.

subcover

A cover ${\displaystyle K}$ is a subcover (or **subcovering**) of a cover ${\displaystyle L}$ if every member of ${\displaystyle K}$ is a member of ${\displaystyle L}$.

subcovering

See **subcover**.

subspace

If *T* is a topology on a space ${\displaystyle X}$, and if ${\displaystyle A}$ is a subset of ${\displaystyle X}$, then the [subspace topology](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/subspace_topology "subspace topology") on ${\displaystyle A}$ induced by ${\displaystyle T}$ consists of all intersections of open sets in ${\displaystyle T}$ with ${\displaystyle A}$. This construction is dual to the construction of the quotient topology.

## T

[${\displaystyle T_{0}}$](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=T0_space&action=edit&redlink=1 "T0 space (page does not exist)")

A space is [${\displaystyle T_{0}}$](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=T0_space&action=edit&redlink=1 "T0 space (page does not exist)") (or **Kolmogorov**) if for every pair of distinct points *x* and *y* in the space, either there is an open set containing *x* but not *y*, or there is an open set containing *y* but not *x*.

[${\displaystyle T_{1}}$](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=T1_space&action=edit&redlink=1 "T1 space (page does not exist)")

A space is [${\displaystyle T_{1}}$](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=T1_space&action=edit&redlink=1 "T1 space (page does not exist)") (or **Fréchet** or **accessible**) if for every pair of distinct points *x* and *y* in the space, there is an open set containing *x* but not *y*. (Compare with ${\displaystyle T_{0}}$; here, we are allowed to specify which point will be contained in the open set.) Equivalently, a space is ${\displaystyle T_{1}}$ if all its [singletons](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=singleton_\(mathematics\)&action=edit&redlink=1 "singleton (mathematics) (page does not exist)") are closed. Every ${\displaystyle T_{1}}$ space is ${\displaystyle T_{0}}$.

[top](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/top "top")

[topological invariant](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=topological_invariant&action=edit&redlink=1 "topological invariant (page does not exist)")

A topological invariant is a property which is preserved under homeomorphism. For example, compactness and connectedness are topological properties, whereas boundedness and completeness are not. [Algebraic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/algebraic "algebraic") topology is the study of topologically invariant [abstract algebra](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/abstract_algebra "abstract algebra") constructions on topological spaces.

[topological space](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/topological_space "topological space")

A [topological space](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/topological_space "topological space") ${\displaystyle (X,T)}$ is a set ${\displaystyle X}$ equipped with a collection ${\displaystyle T}$ of subsets of ${\displaystyle X}$ satisfying the following [axioms](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/axiom "axiom"):

1. The empty set and ${\displaystyle X}$ are in ${\displaystyle T}$.
2. The union of any collection of sets in ${\displaystyle T}$ is also in ${\displaystyle T}$.
3. The intersection of any pair of sets in ${\displaystyle T}$ is also in ${\displaystyle T}$.

The collection ${\displaystyle T}$ is a **topology** on ${\displaystyle X}$.

topological sum

See **coproduct topology**.

[topologically complete](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=topologically_complete&action=edit&redlink=1 "topologically complete (page does not exist)")

A space is [topologically complete](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=topologically_complete&action=edit&redlink=1 "topologically complete (page does not exist)") if it is homeomorphic to a complete metric space.

topology

See **[topological space](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/topological_space "topological space")**.

totally bounded

A metric space ${\displaystyle M}$ is totally bounded if, for every ${\displaystyle r>0}$, there exist a [finite](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/finite_set "finite set") cover of ${\displaystyle M}$ by open balls of radius ${\displaystyle r}$. A metric space is compact if and only if it is complete and totally bounded.

totally disconnected

A space is totally disconnected if it has no connected subset with more than one point.

[trivial topology](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/trivial_topology "trivial topology")

The [trivial topology](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/trivial_topology "trivial topology") (or **indiscrete topology**) on a set ${\displaystyle X}$ consists of precisely the empty set and the entire space ${\displaystyle X}$.

[Tychonoff](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=Tychonoff&action=edit&redlink=1 "Tychonoff (page does not exist)")

A [Tychonoff space](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Tychonoff_space "Tychonoff space") (or **completely regular Hausdorff** space, **completely ${\displaystyle T_{3}}$** space, ${\displaystyle T_{3.5}}$ space) is a completely regular ${\displaystyle T_{0}}$ space. (A completely regular space is Hausdorff [if and only if](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/if_and_only_if "if and only if") it is ${\displaystyle T_{0}}$, so the terminology is consistent.) Every Tychonoff space is regular Hausdorff.

## U

ultra-connected

A space is ultra-connected if no two non-empty closed sets are disjoint. Every ultra-connected space is path-connected.

ultrametric

A metric is an ultrametric if it satisfies the following stronger version of the triangle inequality: for all ${\displaystyle x,y,z}$ in ${\displaystyle M,d(x,z)\leq max(d(x,y),d(y,z))}$.

[uniform isomorphism](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=uniform_isomorphism&action=edit&redlink=1 "uniform isomorphism (page does not exist)")

If ${\displaystyle X}$ and ${\displaystyle Y}$ are [uniform spaces](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/uniform_space "uniform space"), a uniform isomorphism from ${\displaystyle X}$ to ${\displaystyle Y}$ is a bijective function ${\displaystyle f:X\rightarrow Y}$ such that ${\displaystyle f}$ and ${\displaystyle f^{-1}}$ are [uniformly continuous](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/uniformly_continuous "uniformly continuous"). The spaces are then said to be uniformly isomorphic and share the same [uniform properties](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=uniform_properties&action=edit&redlink=1 "uniform properties (page does not exist)").

[uniformisable](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/uniformisable "uniformisable")

A space is uniformizable if it is homeomorphic to a uniform space.

[uniform space](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/uniform_space "uniform space")

A [uniform space](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/uniform_space "uniform space") is a set ${\displaystyle U}$ equipped with a nonempty collection ${\displaystyle \Phi }$ of subsets of the [Cartesian product](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product") ${\displaystyle X\times X}$ satisfying the following [axioms](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/axiom "axiom"):

1. if ${\displaystyle U}$ is in ${\displaystyle \Phi }$, then ${\displaystyle U}$ contains ${\displaystyle \{(x,x)|x{\text{ in}}X\}}$.
2. if ${\displaystyle U}$ is in ${\displaystyle \Phi }$, then ${\displaystyle \{(y,x)|(x,y){\text{ in}}U\}}$ is also in ${\displaystyle \Phi }$
3. if ${\displaystyle U}$ is in ${\displaystyle \Phi }$ and ${\displaystyle V}$ is a subset of ${\displaystyle X\times X}$ which contains ${\displaystyle U}$, then ${\displaystyle V}$ is in ${\displaystyle \Phi }$
4. if ${\displaystyle U}$ and ${\displaystyle V}$ are in ${\displaystyle \Phi }$, then ${\displaystyle U\cap V}$ is in ${\displaystyle \Phi }$
5. if ${\displaystyle U}$ is in ${\displaystyle \Phi }$, then there exists ${\displaystyle V}$ in ${\displaystyle \Phi }$ such that, whenever ${\displaystyle (x,y)}$ and ${\displaystyle (y,z)}$ are in ${\displaystyle V}$, then ${\displaystyle (x,z)}$ is in ${\displaystyle (U)}$.

The elements of ${\displaystyle \Phi }$ are called **entourages**, and ${\displaystyle \Phi }$ itself is called a **uniform structure** on ${\displaystyle U}$.

## W

[weak topology](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=weak_topology&action=edit&redlink=1 "weak topology (page does not exist)")

The [weak topology](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=weak_topology&action=edit&redlink=1 "weak topology (page does not exist)") on a set, with respect to a collection of functions from that set into topological spaces, is the coarsest topology on the set which makes all the functions continuous.

weaker topology

See **[coarser topology](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=coarser_topology&action=edit&redlink=1 "coarser topology (page does not exist)")**. Beware, some authors, especially [analysts](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/analyst "analyst"), use the term **stronger topology**.

weakly countably compact

A space is weakly countably compact (or **limit point compact**) if every [infinite](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/infinite "infinite") subset has a limit point.

weakly hereditary

A property of spaces is said to be weakly hereditary if whenever a space has that property, then so does every closed subspace of it. For example, compactness and the Lindelöf property are both weakly hereditary properties, although neither is hereditary.

weight

The [weight of a space](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=weight_of_a_space&action=edit&redlink=1 "weight of a space (page does not exist)") ${\displaystyle X}$ is the smallest [cardinal number](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cardinal_number "cardinal number") ${\displaystyle \kappa }$ such that ${\displaystyle X}$ has a base of cardinal ${\displaystyle \kappa }$. (Note that such a cardinal number exists, because the entire topology forms a base, and because the class of cardinal numbers is [well-ordered](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=well_order&action=edit&redlink=1 "well order (page does not exist)").)

well-connected

See **ultra-connected**. (Some authors use this term strictly for ultra-connected compact spaces.)

## Z

zero-dimensional

A space is [zero-dimensional](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/zero-dimensional "zero-dimensional") if it has a base of clopen sets.
