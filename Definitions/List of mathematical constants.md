A [mathematical constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_constant "Mathematical constant") is a key [number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number "Number") whose value is fixed by an unambiguous definition, often referred to by a symbol (e.g., an [alphabet letter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Letter_\(alphabet\) "Letter (alphabet)")), or by mathematicians' names to facilitate using it across multiple [mathematical problems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_problem "Mathematical problem").[^2] For example, the constant [π](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi "Pi") may be defined as the ratio of the length of a circle's [circumference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumference "Circumference") to its [diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diameter "Diameter"). The following list includes a [decimal expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_expansion "Decimal expansion") and set containing each number, ordered by year of discovery.

The column headings may be clicked to sort the table alphabetically, by decimal value, or by set. Explanations of the symbols in the right hand column can be found by clicking on them.

Continued fractions with more than 20 known terms have been truncated, with an [ellipsis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsis "Ellipsis") to show that they continue. Rational numbers have two continued fractions; the version in this list is the shorter one. Decimal representations are [rounded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rounding "Rounding") or padded to 10 places if the values are known.
