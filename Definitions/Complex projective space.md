The Riemann sphere, the one-dimensional complex projective space, i.e. the complex projective line.

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), **complex projective space** is the [projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_space "Projective space") with respect to the field of [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number"). By analogy, whereas the points of a [real projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_projective_space "Real projective space") label the lines through the origin of a real [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), the points of a complex projective space label the *[complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane")* lines through the origin of a complex Euclidean space (see [below](#Introduction) for an intuitive account). Formally, a complex projective space is the space of complex lines through the origin of an (*n* +1)-dimensional complex [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space"). The space is denoted variously as **P** (**C** <sup><i>n</i> +1</sup>), **P** <sub><i>n</i></sub> (**C**) or **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup>. When *n* = 1, the complex projective space **CP** <sup>1</sup> is the [Riemann sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_sphere "Riemann sphere"), and when *n* = 2, **CP** <sup>2</sup> is the [complex projective plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_projective_plane "Complex projective plane") (see there for a more elementary discussion).

Complex projective space was first introduced by [von Staudt (1860)](#CITEREFvon_Staudt1860) as an instance of what was then known as the "geometry of position", a notion originally due to [Lazare Carnot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lazare_Carnot "Lazare Carnot"), a kind of [synthetic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthetic_geometry "Synthetic geometry") that included other projective geometries as well. Subsequently, near the turn of the 20th century it became clear to the [Italian school of algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_school_of_algebraic_geometry "Italian school of algebraic geometry") that the complex projective spaces were the most natural domains in which to consider the solutions of [polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial") equations – [algebraic varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety "Algebraic variety") ([Grattan-Guinness 2005](#CITEREFGrattan-Guinness2005), pp. 445–446). In modern times, both the [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") and geometry of complex projective space are well understood and closely related to that of the [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-sphere "N-sphere"). Indeed, in a certain sense the (2 *n* +1)-sphere can be regarded as a family of circles parametrized by **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup>: this is the [Hopf fibration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hopf_fibration "Hopf fibration"). Complex projective space carries a ([Kähler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%A4hler_metric "Kähler metric")) [metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor"), called the [Fubini–Study metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fubini%E2%80%93Study_metric "Fubini–Study metric"), in terms of which it is a [Hermitian symmetric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermitian_symmetric_space "Hermitian symmetric space") of rank 1.

Complex projective space has many applications in both mathematics and [quantum physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_physics "Quantum physics"). In [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), complex projective space is the home of [projective varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_variety "Projective variety"), a well-behaved class of [algebraic varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety "Algebraic variety"). In topology, the complex projective space plays an important role as a [classifying space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classifying_space "Classifying space") for complex [line bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_bundle "Line bundle"): families of complex lines parametrized by another space. In this context, the infinite union of projective spaces ([direct limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_limit "Direct limit")), denoted **CP** <sup>∞</sup>, is the classifying space [K(Z,2)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K\(Z,2\) "K(Z,2)"). In quantum physics, the [wave function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_function "Wave function") associated to a [pure state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pure_state "Pure state") of a quantum mechanical system is a [probability amplitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_amplitude "Probability amplitude"), meaning that it has unit norm, and has an inessential overall phase: that is, the wave function of a pure state is naturally a point in the [projective Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_Hilbert_space "Projective Hilbert space") of the state space. Complex projective manifold is 2n dimensional space or it is n dimensional complex space.

## Introduction

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Railroad-Tracks-Perspective.jpg/250px-Railroad-Tracks-Perspective.jpg)

Parallel lines in the plane intersect at the vanishing point in the line at infinity.

The notion of a projective plane arises out of the idea of perspection in geometry and art: that it is sometimes useful to include in the Euclidean plane an additional "imaginary" line that represents the horizon that an artist, painting the plane, might see. Following each direction from the origin, there is a different point on the horizon, so the horizon can be thought of as the set of all directions from the origin. The Euclidean plane, together with its horizon, is called the [real projective plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_projective_plane "Real projective plane"), and the horizon is sometimes called a [line at infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_at_infinity "Line at infinity"). By the same construction, projective spaces can be considered in higher dimensions. For instance, the real projective 3-space is a Euclidean space together with a [plane at infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_at_infinity "Plane at infinity") that represents the horizon that an artist (who must, necessarily, live in four dimensions) would see.

These [real projective spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_projective_space "Real projective space") can be constructed in a slightly more rigorous way as follows. Here, let **R** <sup><i>n</i> +1</sup> denote the [real coordinate space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_coordinate_space "Real coordinate space") of *n* +1 dimensions, and regard the landscape to be painted as a [hyperplane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperplane "Hyperplane") in this space. Suppose that the eye of the artist is the origin in **R** <sup><i>n</i> +1</sup>. Then along each line through his eye, there is a point of the landscape or a point on its horizon. Thus the real projective space is the space of lines through the origin in **R** <sup><i>n</i> +1</sup>. Without reference to coordinates, this is the space of lines through the origin in an (*n* +1)-dimensional real [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space").

To describe the complex projective space in an analogous manner requires a generalization of the idea of vector, line, and direction. Imagine that instead of standing in a real Euclidean space, the artist is standing in a complex Euclidean space **C** <sup><i>n</i> +1</sup> (which has real dimension 2 *n* +2) and the landscape is a *complex* hyperplane (of real dimension 2 *n*). Unlike the case of real Euclidean space, in the complex case there are directions in which the artist can look which do not see the landscape (because it does not have high enough dimension). However, in a complex space, there is an additional "phase" associated with the directions through a point, and by adjusting this phase the artist can guarantee that they typically see the landscape. The "horizon" is then the space of directions, but such that two directions are regarded as "the same" if they differ only by a phase. The complex projective space is then the landscape (**C** <sup><i>n</i></sup>) with the horizon attached "at infinity". Just like the real case, the complex projective space is the space of directions through the origin of **C** <sup><i>n</i> +1</sup>, where two directions are regarded as the same if they differ by a phase.

## Construction

Complex projective space is a [complex manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_manifold "Complex manifold") that may be described by *n* + 1 complex coordinates as

${\displaystyle Z=(Z_{1},Z_{2},\ldots ,Z_{n+1})\in \mathbb {C} ^{n+1},\qquad (Z_{1},Z_{2},\ldots ,Z_{n+1})\neq (0,0,\ldots ,0)}$

where the tuples differing by an overall rescaling are identified:

${\displaystyle (Z_{1},Z_{2},\ldots ,Z_{n+1})\equiv (\lambda Z_{1},\lambda Z_{2},\ldots ,\lambda Z_{n+1});\quad \lambda \in \mathbb {C} ,\qquad \lambda \neq 0.}$

That is, these are [homogeneous coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_coordinates "Homogeneous coordinates") in the traditional sense of [projective geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_geometry "Projective geometry"). The point set **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup> is covered by the patches ${\displaystyle U_{i}=\{Z\mid Z_{i}\neq 0\}}$. In *U* <sub><i>i</i></sub>, one can define a coordinate system by

${\displaystyle z_{1}=Z_{1}/Z_{i},\quad z_{2}=Z_{2}/Z_{i},\quad \dots ,\quad z_{i-1}=Z_{i-1}/Z_{i},\quad z_{i}=Z_{i+1}/Z_{i},\quad \dots ,\quad z_{n}=Z_{n+1}/Z_{i}.}$

The coordinate transitions between two different such charts *U* <sub><i>i</i></sub> and *U* <sub><i>j</i></sub> are [holomorphic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holomorphic_function "Holomorphic function") (in fact they are [fractional linear transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractional_linear_transformation "Fractional linear transformation")). Thus **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup> carries the structure of a [complex manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_manifold "Complex manifold") of complex dimension *n*, and *[a fortiori](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_fortiori "A fortiori")* the structure of a real [differentiable manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold") of real dimension 2 *n*.

One may also regard **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup> as a [quotient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_space_\(topology\) "Quotient space (topology)") of the unit 2 *n* + 1 [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere") in **C** <sup><i>n</i> +1</sup> under the action of [U(1)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_group "Unitary group"):

**CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup> = *S* <sup>2 <i>n</i> +1</sup> /U(1).

This is because every line in **C** <sup><i>n</i> +1</sup> intersects the unit sphere in a [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle"). By first projecting to the unit sphere and then identifying under the natural action of U(1) one obtains **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup>. For *n* = 1 this construction yields the classical [Hopf bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hopf_bundle "Hopf bundle") ${\displaystyle S^{3}\to S^{2}}$. From this perspective, the differentiable structure on **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup> is induced from that of *S* <sup>2 <i>n</i> +1</sup>, being the quotient of the latter by a [compact group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_group "Compact group") that acts properly.

## Topology

The topology of **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup> is determined inductively by the following [cell decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CW_complex "CW complex"). Let *H* be a fixed hyperplane through the origin in **C** <sup><i>n</i> +1</sup>. Under the projection map **C** <sup><i>n</i> +1</sup> \\{0} → **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup>, *H* goes into a subspace that is homeomorphic to **CP** <sup><i>n</i> −1</sup>. The complement of the image of *H* in **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup> is homeomorphic to **C** <sup><i>n</i></sup>. Thus **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup> arises by attaching a 2 *n* -cell to **CP** <sup><i>n</i> −1</sup>:

${\displaystyle \mathbf {CP} ^{n}=\mathbf {CP} ^{n-1}\cup \mathbf {C} ^{n}.}$

Alternatively, if the 2 *n* -cell is regarded instead as the open unit ball in **C** <sup><i>n</i></sup>, then the attaching map is the Hopf fibration of the boundary. An analogous inductive cell decomposition is true for all of the projective spaces; see ([Besse 1978](#CITEREFBesse1978)).

### CW-decomposition

One useful way to construct the complex projective spaces ${\displaystyle \mathbf {CP} ^{n}}$ is through a recursive construction using [CW-complexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CW_complex "CW complex"). Recall that there is a homeomorphism ${\displaystyle \mathbf {CP} ^{1}\cong S^{2}}$ to the 2-sphere, giving the first space. We can then induct on the cells to get a [pushout map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pushout_\(category_theory\) "Pushout (category theory)") 
$$
{\displaystyle {\begin{matrix}S^{3}&\hookrightarrow &D^{4}\\\downarrow &&\downarrow \\\mathbf {CP} ^{1}&\to &\mathbf {CP} ^{2}\end{matrix}}}
$$
 where ${\displaystyle D^{4}}$ is the four ball, and ${\displaystyle S^{3}\to \mathbf {CP} ^{1}}$ represents the generator in ${\displaystyle \pi _{3}(S^{2})}$ (hence it is homotopy equivalent to the [Hopf map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hopf_fibration "Hopf fibration")). We can then inductively construct the spaces as pushout diagrams 
$$
{\displaystyle {\begin{matrix}S^{2n-1}&\hookrightarrow &D^{2n}\\\downarrow &&\downarrow \\\mathbf {CP} ^{n-1}&\to &\mathbf {CP} ^{n}\end{matrix}}}
$$
 where ${\displaystyle S^{2n-1}\to \mathbf {CP} ^{n-1}}$ represents an element in 
$$
{\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\pi _{2n-1}(\mathbf {CP} ^{n-1})&\cong \pi _{2n-1}(S^{2n-2})\\&\cong \mathbb {Z} /2\end{aligned}}}
$$
 The isomorphism of homotopy groups is described below, and the isomorphism of homotopy groups is a standard calculation in [stable homotopy theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_homotopy_theory "Stable homotopy theory") (which can be done with the [Serre spectral sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serre_spectral_sequence "Serre spectral sequence"), [Freudenthal suspension theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freudenthal_suspension_theorem "Freudenthal suspension theorem"), and the [Postnikov tower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postnikov_tower "Postnikov tower")). The map comes from the [fiber bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle "Fiber bundle") 
$$
{\displaystyle S^{1}\hookrightarrow S^{2n-1}\twoheadrightarrow \mathbf {CP} ^{n-1}}
$$
 giving a non-contractible map, hence it represents the generator in ${\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} /2}$. Otherwise, there would be a homotopy equivalence ${\displaystyle \mathbf {CP} ^{n}\simeq \mathbf {CP} ^{n-1}\times D^{n}}$, but then it would be homotopy equivalent to ${\displaystyle S^{2}}$, a contradiction which can be seen by looking at the homotopy groups of the space.

### Point-set topology

Complex projective space is [compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_space "Compact space") and [connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connected_space "Connected space"), being a quotient of a compact, connected space.

### Homotopy groups

From the fiber bundle

${\displaystyle S^{1}\hookrightarrow S^{2n+1}\twoheadrightarrow \mathbf {CP} ^{n}}$

or more suggestively

${\displaystyle U(1)\hookrightarrow S^{2n+1}\twoheadrightarrow \mathbf {CP} ^{n}}$

**CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup> is [simply connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simply_connected "Simply connected"). Moreover, by the [long exact homotopy sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long_exact_homotopy_sequence "Long exact homotopy sequence"), the second homotopy group is π <sub>2</sub> (**CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup>) ≅ **Z**, and all the higher homotopy groups agree with those of *S* <sup>2 <i>n</i> +1</sup>: π <sub><i>k</i></sub> (**CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup>) ≅ π <sub><i>k</i></sub> (*S* <sup>2 <i>n</i> +1</sup>) for all *k* > 2.

### Homology

In general, the [algebraic topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_topology "Algebraic topology") of **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup> is based on the rank of the [homology groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homology_group "Homology group") being zero in odd dimensions; also *H* <sub>2 <i>i</i></sub> (**CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup>, **Z**) is [infinite cyclic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_cyclic "Infinite cyclic") for *i* = 0 to *n*. Therefore, the [Betti numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Betti_number "Betti number") run

1, 0, 1, 0,..., 0, 1, 0, 0, 0,...

That is, 0 in odd dimensions, 1 in even dimensions 0 through 2n. The [Euler characteristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_characteristic "Euler characteristic") of **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup> is therefore *n* + 1. By [Poincaré duality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_duality "Poincaré duality") the same is true for the ranks of the [cohomology groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cohomology_group "Cohomology group"). In the case of cohomology, one can go further, and identify the [graded ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graded_ring "Graded ring") structure, for [cup product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cup_product "Cup product"); the generator of *H* <sup>2</sup> (**CP** <sup>n</sup>, **Z**) is the class associated to a [hyperplane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperplane "Hyperplane"), and this is a ring generator, so that the ring is isomorphic with

**Z** \[*T*\]/(*T* <sup><i>n</i> +1</sup>),

with *T* a degree two generator. This implies also that the [Hodge number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_number "Hodge number") *h* <sup><i>i</i>,<i>i</i></sup> = 1, and all the others are zero. See ([Besse 1978](#CITEREFBesse1978)).

### K-theory

It follows from induction and [Bott periodicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bott_periodicity "Bott periodicity") that

${\displaystyle K_{\mathbf {C} }^{*}(\mathbf {CP} ^{n})=K_{\mathbf {C} }^{0}(\mathbf {CP} ^{n})=\mathbf {Z} [H]/(H-1)^{n+1}.}$

The [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle") satisfies

${\displaystyle T\mathbf {CP} ^{n}\oplus \vartheta ^{1}=H^{\oplus n+1},}$

where ${\displaystyle \vartheta ^{1}}$ denotes the trivial line bundle, from the [Euler sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_sequence "Euler sequence"). From this, the [Chern classes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chern_class "Chern class") and [characteristic numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic_number "Characteristic number") can be calculated explicitly.

### Classifying space

There is a space ${\displaystyle \mathbf {CP} ^{\infty }}$ which, in a sense, is the [inductive limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inductive_limit "Inductive limit") of ${\displaystyle \mathbf {CP} ^{n}}$ as ${\displaystyle n\to \infty }$. It is [BU(1)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BU\(1\) "BU(1)"), the [classifying space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classifying_space "Classifying space") of [U(1)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U\(1\) "U(1)"), the circle group, in the sense of [homotopy theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy_theory "Homotopy theory"), and so classifies complex [line bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_bundle "Line bundle"). Equivalently it accounts for the first [Chern class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chern_class "Chern class"). This can be seen heuristically by looking at the fiber bundle maps 
$$
{\displaystyle S^{1}\hookrightarrow S^{2n+1}\twoheadrightarrow \mathbf {CP} ^{n}}
$$
 and ${\displaystyle n\to \infty }$. This gives a fiber bundle (called the **universal circle bundle**) 
$$
{\displaystyle S^{1}\hookrightarrow S^{\infty }\twoheadrightarrow \mathbf {CP} ^{\infty }}
$$
 constructing this space. Note using the long [exact sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exact_sequence "Exact sequence") of homotopy groups, we have ${\displaystyle \pi _{2}(\mathbf {CP} ^{\infty })=\pi _{1}(S^{1})}$ hence ${\displaystyle \mathbf {CP} ^{\infty }}$ is an [Eilenberg–MacLane space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eilenberg%E2%80%93MacLane_space "Eilenberg–MacLane space"), a ${\displaystyle K(\mathbb {Z} ,2)}$. Because of this fact, and [Brown's representability theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brown%27s_representability_theorem "Brown's representability theorem"), we have the following isomorphism 
$$
{\displaystyle H^{2}(X;\mathbb {Z} )\cong [X,\mathbf {CP} ^{\infty }]}
$$
 for any nice CW-complex ${\displaystyle X}$. Moreover, from the theory of [Chern classes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chern_class "Chern class"), every complex line bundle ${\displaystyle L\to X}$ can be represented as a pullback of the universal line bundle on ${\displaystyle \mathbf {CP} ^{\infty }}$, meaning there is a pullback square 
$$
{\displaystyle {\begin{matrix}L&\to &{\mathcal {L}}\\\downarrow &&\downarrow \\X&\to &\mathbf {CP} ^{\infty }\end{matrix}}}
$$
 where ${\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}\to \mathbf {CP} ^{\infty }}$ is the associated vector bundle of the principal ${\displaystyle U(1)}$ -bundle ${\displaystyle S^{\infty }\to \mathbf {CP} ^{\infty }}$. See, for instance, ([Bott & Tu 1982](#CITEREFBottTu1982)) and ([Milnor & Stasheff 1974](#CITEREFMilnorStasheff1974)).

## Differential geometry

The natural metric on **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup> is the [Fubini–Study metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fubini%E2%80%93Study_metric "Fubini–Study metric"), and its holomorphic isometry group is the [projective unitary group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_unitary_group "Projective unitary group") PU(*n* +1), where the stabilizer of a point is

${\displaystyle \mathrm {P} (1\times \mathrm {U} (n))\cong \mathrm {PU} (n).}$

It is a [Hermitian symmetric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermitian_symmetric_space "Hermitian symmetric space") ([Kobayashi & Nomizu 1996](#CITEREFKobayashiNomizu1996)), represented as a coset space

${\displaystyle U(n+1)/(U(1)\times U(n))\cong SU(n+1)/S(U(1)\times U(n)).}$

The geodesic symmetry at a point *p* is the unitary transformation that fixes *p* and is the negative identity on the orthogonal complement of the line represented by *p*.

### Geodesics

Through any two points *p*, *q* in complex projective space, there passes a unique *complex* line (a **CP** <sup>1</sup>). A [great circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_circle "Great circle") of this complex line that contains *p* and *q* is a [geodesic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic "Geodesic") for the Fubini–Study metric. In particular, all of the geodesics are closed (they are circles), and all have equal length. (This is always true of Riemannian globally symmetric spaces of rank 1.)

The [cut locus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cut_locus "Cut locus") of any point *p* is equal to a hyperplane **CP** <sup><i>n</i> −1</sup>. This is also the set of fixed points of the geodesic symmetry at *p* (less *p* itself). See ([Besse 1978](#CITEREFBesse1978)).

### Sectional curvature pinching

It has [sectional curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sectional_curvature "Sectional curvature") ranging from 1/4 to 1, and is the roundest manifold that is not a sphere (or covered by a sphere): by the [1/4-pinched sphere theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_geometry#Pinched_sectional_curvature "Riemannian geometry"), any complete, simply connected [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold") with curvature strictly between 1/4 and 1 is diffeomorphic to the sphere. Complex projective space shows that 1/4 is sharp. Conversely, if a complete simply connected Riemannian manifold has sectional curvatures in the closed interval \[1/4,1\], then it is either diffeomorphic to the sphere, or isometric to the complex projective space, the [quaternionic projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternionic_projective_space "Quaternionic projective space"), or else the [Cayley plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cayley_plane "Cayley plane") F <sub>4</sub> /Spin(9); see ([Brendle & Schoen 2008](#CITEREFBrendleSchoen2008)).

### Spin structure

The odd-dimensional projective spaces can be given a [spin structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_structure "Spin structure"), the even-dimensional ones cannot.

## Algebraic geometry

Complex projective space is a special case of a [Grassmannian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grassmannian "Grassmannian"), and is a [homogeneous space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_space "Homogeneous space") for various [Lie groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group"). It is a [Kähler manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%A4hler_manifold "Kähler manifold") carrying the [Fubini–Study metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fubini%E2%80%93Study_metric "Fubini–Study metric"), which is essentially determined by symmetry properties. It also plays a central role in [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"); by [Chow's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry_and_analytic_geometry#Chow.27s_theorem "Algebraic geometry and analytic geometry"), any compact complex [submanifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Submanifold "Submanifold") of **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup> is the zero locus of a finite number of polynomials, and is thus a projective [algebraic variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety "Algebraic variety"). See ([Griffiths & Harris 1994](#CITEREFGriffithsHarris1994))

### Zariski topology

In [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), complex projective space can be equipped with another topology known as the [Zariski topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zariski_topology "Zariski topology") ([Hartshorne 1977](#CITEREFHartshorne1977), §II.2). Let *S* = **C** \[*Z* <sub>0</sub>,...,*Z* <sub><i>n</i></sub>\] denote the [commutative ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring "Commutative ring") of polynomials in the (*n* +1) variables *Z* <sub>0</sub>,...,*Z* <sub><i>n</i></sub>. This ring is [graded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graded_ring "Graded ring") by the total degree of each polynomial:

${\displaystyle S=\bigoplus _{n=0}^{\infty }S_{n}.}$

Define a subset of **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup> to be *closed* if it is the simultaneous solution set of a collection of homogeneous polynomials. Declaring the complements of the closed sets to be open, this defines a topology (the Zariski topology) on **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup>.

### Structure as a scheme

Another construction of **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup> (and its Zariski topology) is possible. Let *S* <sub>+</sub> ⊂ *S* be the [ideal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_\(ring_theory\) "Ideal (ring theory)") spanned by the homogeneous polynomials of positive degree:

${\displaystyle \bigoplus _{n>0}S_{n}.}$

Define [Proj *S*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proj "Proj") to be the set of all [homogeneous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_ideal "Homogeneous ideal") [prime ideals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_ideal "Prime ideal") in *S* that do not contain *S* <sub>+</sub>. Call a subset of Proj *S* closed if it has the form

${\displaystyle V(I)=\{p\in \operatorname {Proj} S\mid p\supseteq I\}}$

for some ideal *I* in *S*. The complements of these closed sets define a topology on Proj *S*. The ring *S*, by [localization at a prime ideal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Localization_of_a_ring "Localization of a ring"), determines a [sheaf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheaf_\(mathematics\) "Sheaf (mathematics)") of [local rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_ring "Local ring") on Proj *S*. The space Proj *S*, together with its topology and sheaf of local rings, is a [scheme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scheme_\(mathematics\) "Scheme (mathematics)"). The subset of closed points of Proj *S* is homeomorphic to **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup> with its Zariski topology. Local sections of the sheaf are identified with the [rational functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_function "Rational function") of total degree zero on **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup>.

### Line bundles

All line bundles on complex projective space can be obtained by the following construction. A function *f*: **C** <sup><i>n</i> +1</sup> \\{0} → **C** is called [homogeneous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_function "Homogeneous function") of degree *k* if

${\displaystyle f(\lambda z)=\lambda ^{k}f(z)}$

for all λ ∈ **C** \\{0} and *z* ∈ **C** <sup><i>n</i> +1</sup> \\{0}. More generally, this definition makes sense in [cones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone_\(linear_algebra\) "Cone (linear algebra)") in **C** <sup><i>n</i> +1</sup> \\{0}. A set *V* ⊂ **C** <sup><i>n</i> +1</sup> \\{0} is called a cone if, whenever *v* ∈ *V*, then *λv* ∈ *V* for all λ ∈ **C** \\{0}; that is, a subset is a cone if it contains the complex line through each of its points. If *U* ⊂ **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup> is an open set (in either the analytic topology or the [Zariski topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zariski_topology "Zariski topology")), let *V* ⊂ **C** <sup><i>n</i> +1</sup> \\{0} be the cone over *U*: the preimage of *U* under the projection **C** <sup><i>n</i> +1</sup> \\{0} → **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup>. Finally, for each integer *k*, let *O* (*k*)(*U*) be the set of functions that are homogeneous of degree *k* in *V*. This defines a [sheaf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheaf_\(mathematics\) "Sheaf (mathematics)") of sections of a certain line bundle, denoted by *O* (*k*).

In the special case *k* = −1, the bundle *O* (−1) is called the [tautological line bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tautological_line_bundle "Tautological line bundle"). It is equivalently defined as the subbundle of the product

${\displaystyle \mathbf {C} ^{n+1}\times \mathbf {CP} ^{n}\to \mathbf {CP} ^{n}}$

whose fiber over *L* ∈ **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup> is the set

${\displaystyle \{(x,L)\mid x\in L\}.}$

These line bundles can also be described in the language of [divisors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divisor_\(algebraic_geometry\) "Divisor (algebraic geometry)"). Let *H* = **CP** <sup><i>n</i> −1</sup> be a given complex hyperplane in **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup>. The space of [meromorphic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meromorphic_function "Meromorphic function") on **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup> with at most a simple pole along *H* (and nowhere else) is a one-dimensional space, denoted by *O* (*H*), and called the [hyperplane bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperplane_bundle "Hyperplane bundle"). The dual bundle is denoted *O* (− *H*), and the *k* <sup>th</sup> tensor power of *O* (*H*) is denoted by *O* (*kH*). This is the sheaf generated by holomorphic multiples of a meromorphic function with a pole of order *k* along *H*. It turns out that

${\displaystyle O(kH)\cong O(k).}$

Indeed, if *L* (*z*) = 0 is a linear defining function for *H*, then *L* <sup>− <i>k</i></sup> is a meromorphic section of *O* (*k*), and locally the other sections of *O* (*k*) are multiples of this section.

Since *H* <sup>1</sup> (**CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup>,**Z**) = 0, the line bundles on **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup> are classified up to isomorphism by their [Chern classes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chern_class "Chern class"), which are integers: they lie in *H* <sup>2</sup> (**CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup>,**Z**) = **Z**. In fact, the first Chern classes of complex projective space are generated under [Poincaré duality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_duality "Poincaré duality") by the homology class associated to a hyperplane *H*. The line bundle *O* (*kH*) has Chern class *k*. Hence every holomorphic line bundle on **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup> is a tensor power of *O* (*H*) or *O* (− *H*). In other words, the [Picard group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Picard_group "Picard group") of **CP** <sup><i>n</i></sup> is generated as an abelian group by the hyperplane class \[*H*\] ([Hartshorne 1977](#CITEREFHartshorne1977)).
