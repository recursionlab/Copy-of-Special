In [set theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_theory "Set theory"), **Ω-logic** is an [infinitary logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitary_logic "Infinitary logic") and [deductive system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deductive_system "Deductive system") proposed by [W. Hugh Woodin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W._Hugh_Woodin "W. Hugh Woodin") ([1999](#CITEREFWoodin1999)) as part of an attempt to generalize the theory of [determinacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinacy "Determinacy") of [pointclasses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pointclass "Pointclass") to cover [the structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hereditarily_countable_set "Hereditarily countable set") ${\displaystyle H_{\aleph _{2}}}$. Just as the [axiom of projective determinacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_of_projective_determinacy "Axiom of projective determinacy") yields a canonical theory of ${\displaystyle H_{\aleph _{1}}}$, he sought to find axioms that would give a canonical theory for the larger structure. The theory he developed involves a controversial argument that the [continuum hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_hypothesis "Continuum hypothesis") is false.

## Analysis

Woodin's **Ω-conjecture** asserts that if there is a proper class of [Woodin cardinals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Woodin_cardinal "Woodin cardinal") (for technical reasons, most results in the theory are most easily stated under this assumption), then Ω-logic satisfies an analogue of the [completeness theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%B6del%27s_completeness_theorem "Gödel's completeness theorem"). From this conjecture, it can be shown that, if there is any single axiom which is comprehensive over ${\displaystyle H_{\aleph _{2}}}$ (in Ω-logic), it must imply that the continuum is not ${\displaystyle \aleph _{1}}$. Woodin also isolated a specific axiom, a variation of [Martin's maximum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin%27s_maximum "Martin's maximum"), which states that any Ω-consistent ${\displaystyle \Pi _{2}}$ (over ${\displaystyle H_{\aleph _{2}}}$) sentence is true; this axiom implies that the continuum is ${\displaystyle \aleph _{2}}$.

Woodin also related his Ω-conjecture to a proposed abstract definition of large cardinals: he took a "large cardinal property" to be a ${\displaystyle \Sigma _{2}}$ property ${\displaystyle P(\alpha )}$ of ordinals which implies that α is a [strong inaccessible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inaccessible_cardinal "Inaccessible cardinal"), and which is invariant under forcing by sets of cardinal less than α. Then the Ω-conjecture implies that if there are arbitrarily large models containing a large cardinal, this fact will be provable in Ω-logic.

The theory involves a definition of **Ω-validity**: a statement is an Ω-valid consequence of a set theory *T* if it holds in every model of *T* having the form ${\displaystyle V_{\alpha }^{\mathbb {B} }}$ for some ordinal ${\displaystyle \alpha }$ and some forcing notion ${\displaystyle \mathbb {B} }$. This notion is clearly preserved under forcing, and in the presence of a proper class of Woodin cardinals it will also be invariant under forcing (in other words, Ω-satisfiability is preserved under forcing as well). There is also a notion of **Ω-provability**;[^1] here the "proofs" consist of [universally Baire sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universally_Baire_set "Universally Baire set") and are checked by verifying that for every countable transitive model of the theory, and every forcing notion in the model, the generic extension of the model (as calculated in *V*) contains the "proof", restricted its own reals. For a proof-set *A* the condition to be checked here is called " *A* -closed". A complexity measure can be given on the proofs by their ranks in the [Wadge hierarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wadge_hierarchy "Wadge hierarchy"). Woodin showed that this notion of "provability" implies Ω-validity for sentences which are ${\displaystyle \Pi _{2}}$ over *V*. The Ω-conjecture states that the converse of this result also holds. In all currently known [core models](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Core_model "Core model"), it is known to be true; moreover the consistency strength of the large cardinals corresponds to the least proof-rank required to "prove" the existence of the cardinals.
